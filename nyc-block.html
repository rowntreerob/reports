Assessing Kober’s NYCHA Argument

Kober is a serious, Abundance-adjacent critic (Manhattan Institute, ex–City Planning), and on NYCHA he is making a money argument, not a process argument. That distinction is the whole game, and it’s why the critique is, as you put it, hard to ignore. Let me separate where he is simply right, where he is incomplete, and where the deeper paradigmatic problem lives.

Where Kober is correct — and the math backs him

His core claim is that PACT and the Trust “can, at best, treat only a fraction of the NYCHA stock.” The numbers in the plan itself confirm this:

  • The need: $78.3B over 20 years ≈ $3.9B/year of accruing capital need, and that’s before inflation and continued aging.
  • The City’s contribution: $5.6B in the entire 5-year capital plan ≈ ~$1.1B/year — the “most City capital in recent history,” and still roughly a quarter of annual accrual, with nothing left over for the backlog.
  • The conversion pipeline: 62,000 PACT + 25,000 Trust = 87,000 units, ~49% of the 177,000 stock at full build-out. To date only ~44,600 (25%) have an identified path.

So the structural gap Kober is pointing at is real and specific: roughly half the portfolio — ~90,000 units — has no comprehensive recapitalization pathway at all. Those units get the incremental $6B/500-project work (boilers, roofs, elevators) and the CRI/MLRR patch-and-repair model, but not gut rehab. For that segment, “managed decline” is an accurate description, and the plan’s own celebrated data point — a “slight” PNA decrease in 2026 against a $78B backlog — is the tell. That is bleeding slowed, not stopped.

He’s also right on a subtle budgetary point worth surfacing: Mamdani is leaning on the expense budget ($118M for vacant-unit turnover, recurring repair dollars) for much of the visible improvement. Expense-funded repairs are a treadmill — they keep units habitable but do not recapitalize building systems. That reinforces his “fraction” thesis.

And his sharpest point is the federal-dependence trap: PACT and the Trust both run on RAD/Section 8 conversion and HUD appropriations for the project-based vouchers. The plan’s own Chapter 4 concedes HUD is “increasingly vulnerable to funding cuts.” So the entire recapitalization engine is staked on a federal partner the document admits is hostile — and the plan offers no City-funded fallback at scale. That is an unhedged risk, and Kober is right to name it.

Where Kober is incomplete — and oddly so for an Abundance critic

Two omissions weaken his read:

1. He ignores the plan’s new value-capture leg. Section 3.3 — “Reboot NYCHA’s role as a public developer” — is precisely the Abundance move: monetize the 2,400-acre land bank and air rights to generate non-federal, non-City-capital dollars for repairs. The tools are real: - SMRRT Loan — a City-controlled revolving subordinate loan explicitly designed to avoid private equity, debuting at Fulton/Elliott-Chelsea. - Transfer of Assistance — moving Section 8 to new construction so the vacated unit can be re-funded. - Development-rights sales — Campos Plaza II raised $19.5M (≈20% of that development’s $92M need) purely from selling air rights to a neighboring builder, who in turn produced 196 units (60 affordable). - Mixed-income infill on NYCHA land to cross-subsidize.

For a critic working in the Abundance tradition — which prizes exactly this kind of creative use of the public balance sheet — to wave past this entirely is a real gap. This is the plan’s most intellectually interesting attempt to build a third funding leg so it isn’t hostage to Washington.

2. His argument is internally tensioned. He faults Mamdani for not solving NYCHA with City capital — then concedes in the same breath that “no mayor has been willing to do that,” and that doing so would cannibalize the new-construction pipeline. You can’t simultaneously hold that City capital is the missing answer and that deploying it would be irresponsible. The honest conclusion is that there is no City-only answer, which actually vindicates the plan’s reliance on conversions and value capture as the least-bad available tools.

But the value-capture leg doesn’t rescue the plan — and here’s the deeper point

Even granting Kober’s omissions, his “fraction” critique survives, because the new tools are pilots, not a scaled solution: SMRRT debuts at one site; air-rights sales raised $19.5M against a $78B need; mixed-income infill is slow and politically radioactive (Fulton/Elliott-Chelsea has real tenant opposition). The arithmetic distance between these instruments and the backlog is several orders of magnitude.

This exposes the limit Kober gestures at but doesn’t fully articulate, and it’s the most important thing for your Abundance framing:

NYCHA’s crisis is a capital/subsidy problem, and Abundance is a supply/process theory. You cannot permit, streamline, or deregulate your way out of a $78B funding hole.

The Abundance toolkit works beautifully on the process pieces of Chapter 3 — vacant-unit turnaround (the 73-day cut, the $59K/unit pipeline), CRI/Ombudsman models, faster lease-up. Those are genuine “make government deliver” wins. But the actual disease — decades of withdrawn federal operating and capital subsidy — is not a barrier you clear; it’s money you either have or don’t. The campaign borrowed Abundance’s build-more, make-government-work energy, and that energy translates cleanly to new construction (Chapter 4) and even to NYCHA’s operational reforms. It does not translate to recapitalizing public housing, because there’s no proceduralist sleight-of-hand that conjures $78B.

The political irony Kober correctly flags

His closing observation — tenant activists criticized the plan but “lack the political clout” to make NYCHA the overriding priority — is the real punchline. A DSA-aligned mayor is continuing and expanding PACT, the very RAD-conversion mechanism his base calls privatization, because the financial logic is inescapable. The Trust (public management, public workforce) is the ideologically preferred vehicle, yet it gets the smaller target (25,000 vs. 62,000) and only two named first sites. That ordering tells you which engine the administration actually trusts to move volume. The plan’s ideology shows up in the framing and the accountability overlays (Post-Conversion meetings, Technical Roundtables, “no private equity” SMRRT), not in the underlying financial architecture, which is fundamentally the Adams/de Blasio playbook.

Bottom line

Kober’s NYCHA critique is the strongest section of his piece because it targets the one problem the Abundance frame cannot reach. He is right that the plan stabilizes rather than resolves, that ~half the stock lacks a recap path, and that the strategy is dangerously federal-dependent. He is unfair in ignoring the value-capture innovations — but those innovations, honestly scaled, don’t refute him; they confirm that the administration knows it needs a non-federal leg and also knows it can’t build one large enough.

The defensible reading of Chapter 3 is therefore not “Mamdani solved NYCHA” but “Mamdani is running the most aggressive available holding action — maximizing conversions, squeezing land value, and accelerating operations — while waiting for a federal partner that the plan itself doubts will arrive.” That’s a coherent position. It is just not the transformational one the rhetoric implies, and it marks the precise boundary where the Abundance agenda stops being able to help.
