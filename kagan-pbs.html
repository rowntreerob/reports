Robert Kagan: U.S. Faces Likely Defeat as Iran Controls the Strait of Hormuz

Executive Summary

Robert Kagan, senior fellow at the Brookings Institution and contributing writer for The Atlantic, argues that the United States is heading toward a strategic defeat in its conflict with Iran. His central claim is that Iran’s control of the Strait of Hormuz — which he considers more consequential than Iran obtaining a nuclear weapon — gives Tehran enormous global leverage that the U.S. has no realistic military option to reverse. Kagan contends that without a full-scale land invasion to remove the Iranian regime (which he neither advocates nor expects), the damage to American power and credibility cannot be undone. He situates this failure within a broader collapse of U.S. global alliances under the Trump administration.

TL;DR

Kagan argues U.S. defeat in the Iran conflict is likely because Iran controls the Strait of Hormuz and America lacks the will or means to reverse it.

Key Points

  • Iran’s control of the Strait of Hormuz gives it greater strategic leverage than a nuclear weapon would, according to Kagan — including the ability to charge tolls and control who transits the strait.
  • Despite 37 days of effective U.S. bombing and the elimination of Iran’s entire leadership, Iran has made zero concessions and has not opened the strait.
  • Iran’s counter-proposal in negotiations amounted to demanding war reparations, sanctions relief, and formal recognition of its control over the strait — which Kagan calls “a slap in Trump’s face.”
  • Kagan assesses that it is the United States, not Iran, that is more afraid of restarting the war, pointing to Trump’s limited response even after Iran violated the ceasefire by attacking American ships.
  • The only military solution Kagan sees is a full-scale land invasion to remove the Iranian regime — something he says neither Trump nor the American public will support.
  • Unlike U.S. defeats in Vietnam or Afghanistan, Kagan argues this one cannot easily be undone because it structurally shifts regional and global power.
  • The Trump administration has simultaneously undermined NATO and U.S. alliances in East Asia, compounding the strategic damage.
  • Japan and South Korea, almost entirely dependent on Gulf energy, will be forced to seek alternative security arrangements.
  • China and Russia are the biggest strategic beneficiaries of the conflict so far, with U.S. global influence measurably diminishing.
  • Iran, despite suffering severe losses, is likely to emerge from this conflict stronger than when it began.

Takeaways & Implications

Kagan’s analysis suggests the U.S. may be experiencing a historic strategic inflection point — one where military superiority fails to translate into political outcomes, and where the costs of inaction and incomplete action are comparably severe. The combination of Hormuz leverage shifting to Iran and the simultaneous erosion of Western alliances points to a potential restructuring of the global order favoring China and Russia. For policymakers and observers, the interview is a stark warning that the framing of “winning” or “not losing” has become increasingly detached from the actual geopolitical consequences on the ground.
