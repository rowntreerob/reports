The California Governor’s Housing Forum: A Debate Within the Democratic Family

On Friday, Ezra Klein—co-author of Abundance, the book that has done more than any other text to reframe Democratic thinking on supply-side liberalism—moderated a 90-minute housing-only forum at Oakland’s Calvin Simmons Theater featuring the five leading Democratic candidates for California Governor: former Treasury official and investor Tom Steyer, former HHS Secretary and California AG Xavier Becerra, former Congresswoman Katie Porter, San Jose Mayor Matt Mahan, and former Los Angeles Mayor Antonio Villaraigosa. Assemblymember Buffy Wicks—architect of much of California’s recent YIMBY legislative breakthrough—opened the evening with a frank account of how “California’s road to housing hell was paved with very good intentions” and entrenched interests “perfectly content with the status quo.”

Klein structured the debate around three pressure points where the post-2019 Newsom housing reforms have stalled: construction cost, the state-local enforcement gap, and homelessness. What emerged was less a fight over goals—all five candidates profess YIMBY commitments—than a revealing set of disagreements over which Democratic priors to abandon to actually deliver units.

1. The Cost Problem: Labor, Speed, and Fees

Klein opened with the RAND finding that California construction costs roughly twice Texas’s per square foot, and that affordable housing in California costs four times a Texas market-rate unit.

  • Steyer leaned hardest on modular/prefab construction, claiming a 20%+ cost reduction is available if the state guarantees orders and rewrites building codes. Klein pressed him on the graveyard of failed modular firms (Katerra, Veev, Factory_OS); Steyer’s answer was essentially that public procurement can give these firms the scale private capital couldn’t. He paired this with a pledge to call a Day-One special election to close the commercial Prop 13 “split roll” loophole, generating ~$20B he would route to cities to convert housing from an “unfunded mandate” into a “funded” one.

  • Becerra was the most labor-deferential, defending Wicks’s tiered framework (below ~8 stories, developers negotiate; taller projects pay prevailing wage). Klein hit him directly with the Terner Center finding that prevailing wage adds roughly $94,000 per unit and asked what cost driver Becerra would cut to offset higher wages. Becerra pivoted to Trump tariffs, streamlining red tape, and a stable financing pipeline—an answer the audience could judge as either holistic or evasive.

  • Porter seized on the RAND study’s actual headline finding—that time is the dominant cost driver (49 months in California vs. 27 in Texas)—and was the only candidate to say plainly that she had broken with labor at the Labor Fed by opposing skilled-and-trained and prevailing wage on residential. She backed a single statewide permit, hard 30-day caps on city fee assessments, and consolidated affordable-housing financing to replace the seven-source capital stack. This was the clearest YIMBY-orthodox answer of the night.

  • Mahan offered the most operationally specific account, having actually cut San Jose’s impact fees by two-thirds, moved multifamily approvals downtown and along transit to by-right ministerial review with CEQA exemption, and watched 2,000 units break ground last year. He wants Sacramento to cap local fees statewide and require feasibility studies before any city imposes higher ones—reframing fees as the policy lever cities actually control, unlike interest rates or lumber.

  • Villaraigosa took the politically riskiest position on the tax question. Klein noted his site defends Prop 13, then walked him into a longer concession: that the commercial/residential property tax burden has flipped from 60/40 to 40/60 since 1978, that pre-1978 owners are unfairly advantaged, and that California needs a comprehensive Think Long-style tax overhaul including a services tax. He still wouldn’t endorse raising residential property taxes outright—a revealing limit on how far even reform-minded Democrats will go.

2. State vs. Local: Carrots, Sticks, and the Huntington Beach Problem

Klein pressed on the central enforcement question: state housing laws have multiplied, yet cities like Huntington Beach simply absorb litigation as a cost of doing business.

  • Becerra, who sued Huntington Beach as AG, defended litigation as a necessary last resort but conceded fines are often “a cost of doing business” and that enforcement is too slow. His proposed framework—escalating incentives that flip into penalties—is essentially the regulatory model he used at HHS for electronic medical records.

  • Mahan made the sharpest critique: the next governor “cannot bring a lawyer’s mindset” to what is fundamentally a market and process failure. He prefers expanding builder’s remedy as automatic state override when cities miss permitting deadlines or stack fees, citing the San Diego 2,000-unit project that still hasn’t been built years after Becerra sued. He also revived tax increment financing (with guardrails) as the missing tool since redevelopment was killed in 2011.

  • Steyer reframed the entire local-control debate by insisting cities aren’t primarily NIMBY—they’re broke. Without new revenue, every housing approval is an unfunded liability for schools and services. Hence his Prop 13 split-roll initiative as the carrot before any stick.

  • Villaraigosa invoked his LA record (more housing built in 8 recession years than the prior 12; downtown population tripled; the subway-to-the-sea now opening) and argued for an “accountability board” inside HCD to verify cities aren’t just adopting housing elements but actually building them. He singled out LA’s Measure ULA transfer tax for an 84% drop in multifamily construction and called for putting NEPA and CEQA on the same clock.

  • Porter used the master-planning question (California Forever, the Tejon Ranch saga, Irvine itself) to argue for housing-type imagination—long-term European-style leases, dorms, boarding-house revivals—and acknowledged the limits of large private master plans while defending innovation in form.

3. Homelessness: The $24 Billion Question

This was where the debate got most uncomfortable for orthodox progressive priors.

  • Becerra said outright that California spent $24B from 2018-2023 without outcomes accountability, framed homelessness as inseparable from mental health, and called for a stabilization fund for pre-eviction cash assistance—the cheapest intervention by orders of magnitude.

  • Porter went further, citing her own academic work on bankruptcy: the median cost of eviction prevention is ~$6,000 per family vs. $800K-$1M per permanent supportive housing unit. She attacked the fragmented architecture of California’s 97 overlapping safety-net programs and proposed consolidating them into a single “CalNec” cash benefit, arguing the paternalism of program-based aid “is racist, it’s sexist, and it’s wrong.”

  • Steyer broke with Housing First orthodoxy by emphasizing emergency interim housing (private rooms, pets allowed, wraparound services) over the shelter-or-PSH binary, noting that only 1-in-7 unhoused people enter homelessness with mental illness, but nearly all develop it on the street.

  • Mahan delivered the night’s most operationally credible homelessness answer, describing San Jose’s 23 interim sites, the no-encampment-zone bargain with neighbors, and—critically—an honest admission that publicly building new affordable housing at $1M/unit when older stock can be bought at $300K/unit has failed the public-trust test. He explicitly repudiated the political bargain Democrats made: “We made a mistake telling voters that if they invested in affordable housing, the tents would disappear.”

  • Villaraigosa was bluntest of all, citing the LAO finding that of all the homelessness spending, only rental assistance and Homekey worked, and challenging Democrats directly: “We’ve been in office for 28 years. Why don’t we build mental facilities?” He called for reviving locked mental health treatment via CARE Court enforcement and asked, in the line that probably travels furthest from the night: “What is compassionate, humane, or progressive about people living in their poop?”

What the Debate Revealed

The substantive cleavages mapped less onto left-right than onto how willing each candidate is to break with a specific Democratic constituency:

Candidate Constituency Most Willing to Break With
Porter Building trades (prevailing wage on residential)
Mahan Affordable housing developers (subsidize purchase, not new build)
Villaraigosa Disability/civil-liberties left (involuntary mental health commitment)
Steyer Commercial property owners (split-roll Prop 13)
Becerra Almost none explicitly—the most coalition-preserving answers

The forum confirmed how thoroughly the Abundance framework has restructured the intra-Democratic debate: no candidate defended CEQA as written, none defended the status quo cost stack, all conceded the $24B homelessness spend underperformed, and all accepted that local control has limits. The remaining disagreements—on labor standards, on coercive mental health treatment, on whether the binding constraint is money (Steyer), time (Porter), fees (Mahan), enforcement (Becerra), or political will (Villaraigosa)—are now the actual terrain of the California gubernatorial race, and a reasonable preview of where the national Democratic housing debate is heading.

The closing book recommendations were telling: Villaraigosa picked Leonhardt’s Ours Was the Shining Future (Democrats forgot working people); Mahan picked Dunkelman’s Why Nothing Works (the Abundance prequel). Both texts are, in different registers, indictments of the party these candidates want to lead.
