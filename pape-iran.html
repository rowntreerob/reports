Summary: Robert Pape on Iran, the Death of NATO, and the Coming Multipolar Order

In this sixth installment of The Escalation Trap, University of Chicago political scientist Robert Pape offers a sobering geopolitical assessment of the unfolding Iran crisis—and what it portends for the broader U.S.-led order. For an audience attuned to the long arc of Middle Eastern conflict (from Mossadegh’s overthrow forward), Pape’s argument lands as a familiar but newly urgent warning: American hegemony is unraveling in real time, and the Persian Gulf is the proximate accelerant.

The “Third Way” That Isn’t

Pape argues Trump is desperately seeking a face-saving exit from the confrontation he helped engineer—a negotiated off-ramp that would let him claim victory while avoiding either (a) the emergence of Iran as a fourth pole of world power, or (b) full kinetic escalation. Tehran, however, is deliberately denying him that fig leaf. The Iranian negotiating team’s recent itinerary—Pakistan, then Russia, then Oman, conspicuously not meeting U.S. envoys—was, in Pape’s framing, a calculated humiliation: “even Putin is more polite when he wants to flip us the bird.” Meanwhile, Trump has not paused military buildup; a third carrier is moving into theater.

Iran’s Counter-Strategy: Building the Fourth Pole

Rather than concede, Iran is methodically constructing the architecture of an alternative power center:

  • Pakistan — geographically adjacent, nuclear-armed, with the port of Karachi and Chinese-developed infrastructure in Balochistan offering a potential overland and maritime route around the U.S. blockade. Pakistan’s existing security understanding with Saudi Arabia adds further leverage.
  • Russia — already Iran’s principal military-intelligence backer in this conflict; a candidate for nuclear-umbrella-adjacent backstopping.
  • Oman — the venue for any eventual deal on Strait of Hormuz transit tolls, which Pape sees as both symbolic (prestige) and material (revenue) prizes.

The critical point: these talks are happening without Americans in the room. That alone, Pape insists, is a structural defeat regardless of outcomes.

“NATO Is Dead”

Pape’s bluntest claim—and one that resonates with anyone who has tracked the institutional erosion accelerated under Trump’s second term—is that NATO has been functionally killed. Not the political shell, but the operational core: the Article 5 reality in which an American supreme commander would actually direct allied militaries (the Eisenhower model). No serious European general staff now believes the U.S. will fight alongside them; Washington has, in Pape’s words, “explained to the world that we’ll wreck you.”

He invokes Mearsheimer’s 1990 “Back to the Future” essay (Pape was Mearsheimer’s first graduate student) as the baseline forecast: absent NATO’s institutional lid, expect German nuclear ambitions, Franco-British-German coordination failures echoing the 1930s, and a strategic windfall for Russia. The 35-year delay of that prediction does not falsify it—it simply means we’re now arriving at the destination.

He also punctures a core MAGA myth: from 1990 to today, U.S. share of world GDP held steady at ~26% while Europe’s fell by a third. America was not the sucker; it was the beneficiary of the arrangement it is now dismantling.

The Escalation Ladder

Pape lays out a tactical framework:

  1. Stage One — Demonstration attacks (current phase): mutual gunboat diplomacy, ship seizures, effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz. Credible signaling without strategic kinetics.
  2. Stage Two — Threshold breaking: U.S. strikes on Iranian coastal fast-boat facilities would constitute “salami-slicing” a major threshold, inviting reciprocal escalation.
  3. Iranian response logic has shifted. The 2020 Soleimani killing produced a “half-tit-for-tat” — restrained, deconflicted missile strikes on empty buildings, reflecting the late Supreme Leader’s balancing role. With that figure now dead, Pape sees “two tits for a tat” — disproportionate escalation.

The Real Target: The Gulf Monarchies

Pape’s most provocative forecast: Iran’s strategic objective over the next 6–8 months is to topple the regimes in Riyadh and Abu Dhabi—not in a day, but through a combination of kinetic pressure and covert political subversion. The UAE and Saudi leadership were the loudest external advocates for regime change in Tehran; Iran will return the favor. This, Pape suggests, explains the otherwise startling deployment of Israeli Iron Dome components to the UAE—the first time the system has left Israeli territory. It is evidence, absent classified sourcing, that his analysis is correct: the Gulf monarchies now sit “between Iraq and a hard place.”

The China Card

If Trump pivots to Beijing to pressure Xi over Iranian financial flows, Xi’s price will not be symbolic. It will be Taiwan. Pape is unequivocal: every door Trump opens in this crisis is a door through which a revisionist power walks.

Coda: Statesmanship and Its Absence

Pape closes with an unusual note for a critic—genuine respect for U.S. military professionalism, the post-Vietnam reconstruction of the force, and the deeply embedded American norm of civilian control (which he correctly identifies as far more culturally entrenched in the U.S. military than in most allied forces). The implicit contrast is devastating: an institutionally serious military is being directed by a White House operating on grievance and improvisation, with no discernible strategy—only reaction. “Donald Trump kept telling us he could handle it better than anybody else. Well, start handling it, sir.”

Bottom line for the informed reader: Pape is describing not a discrete crisis but a phase transition in the international system—the Persian Gulf as the hinge on which post-1945 American primacy finally breaks. The historical rhyme is uncomfortable: just as the 1953 coup in Iran inaugurated a 70-year cycle of Western interventionism that produced 1979, 2003, and the present moment, this confrontation may inaugurate the post-American Middle East—with nuclear Pakistan, resurgent Russia, and an ascendant China filling the vacuum that hollowed-out American statecraft can no longer occupy.
