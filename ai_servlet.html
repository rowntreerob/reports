The Ezra Klein Show: “Our Tax System Should Make You Furious”

A Summary of Ezra Klein’s Conversation with Tax Law Professor Ray Madoff

The Core Problem: A Two-Tiered Tax System

Ray Madoff, a Boston College tax law professor and author of The Second Estate: How the Tax Code Made an American Aristocracy, argues that America operates two fundamentally different tax systems: one for wage earners and one for the ultra-wealthy.

Working Americans pay federal income taxes up to 37% plus payroll taxes up to 15.3%. In fact, 80% of Americans pay more in payroll taxes than income taxes. The oft-cited statistic that “the top 1% pays 40% of income taxes” is misleading—it refers to the top 1% of income earners (high-paid doctors, lawyers, finance workers), not the wealthiest Americans. Billionaires like Bezos, Musk, and Zuckerberg are often in the 40% of non-payers because they avoid taxable income entirely.

“Salaries Are for Suckers”

The 2021 ProPublica leak (via IRS contractor Charles Littlejohn) revealed shocking true tax rates: Warren Buffett: 0.1%, Jeff Bezos: 0.98%, Michael Bloomberg: 1.3%.

The mechanism works like this: - Step 1: Take a minimal salary (Bezos capped his at $82,000 for 20+ years) to avoid income and payroll taxes. - Step 2: Accumulate wealth through unrealized stock gains—never taxed unless sold. - Step 3: Instead of selling stock, borrow against it. Loans aren’t taxable events, interest rates are favorable (collateral is risk-free), and you simply roll loans over indefinitely.

Selling stock triggers ~23% capital gains tax—still better than salary rates, but borrowing is better still. With $100+ billion in wealth, funding even a $25-100 million annual lifestyle is “penny change.”

The Estate Tax: A Tax in Name Only

The estate tax was designed as a backstop to prevent dynastic wealth. Today, despite the 40% headline rate on estates over $15 million, it’s riddled with loopholes:

  • Minority discounts: Split a $100M business into pieces, each discounted 30-40%, shrinking taxable value by half.
  • Dynasty trusts: Perpetual vehicles funneling wealth to descendants forever—openly named despite the estate tax’s anti-dynasty purpose.
  • GRATs (Grantor-Retained Annuity Trusts): Roll profits out tax-free.
  • Charitable vehicles and 501(c)(4)s: Preserve control while avoiding gift and capital gains taxes.

Madoff’s telling evidence: In 2025, Trump and a fully Republican Congress had the chance to “kill the death tax”—a 30-year GOP priority—and didn’t. Why? The loopholes serve the wealthy better than outright repeal, which would expose the income tax system’s generosity to inherited wealth.

The numbers tell the story: Estate tax returns filed dropped from 122,000 in 2000 to just 2,584 taxable returns in 2021. In 2024, the estate tax raised only $30 billion against the $50 trillion held by America’s richest 1%.

The Angel of Death Loophole

When appreciated assets pass at death, “step-up in basis” wipes away all unrealized gains. Heirs are treated as having purchased the stock at current market value—they can sell immediately with zero capital gains tax. Lifetime gains escape taxation entirely.

The Buybacks Shift

Before 1982, companies distributed 70%+ of profits as dividends, taxed as ordinary income. After rule changes enabling stock buybacks, dividends fell below 20%. This inflated share prices (the Dow sat near 3,000 from the 1920s-1982; it’s ~45,000 today) while converting taxable dividend income into untaxed capital appreciation.

The Fairness Question

Quoting Andrew Mellon’s 1924 book, Madoff notes that even that famed anti-tax Treasury Secretary believed wage income should be taxed more lightly than investment income, since wages are precarious while investments are secure. Today’s system is the reverse—what Klein calls “cruel.”

What Won’t Work: Wealth Taxes

Madoff is skeptical of wealth taxes: - States: People flee (she met a rich person who uses an app to count Florida days). California’s proposed retroactive one-time tax sets a dangerous precedent. - Federal: Constitutional issues with this Supreme Court likely doom it. - Practical: Valuing art, crypto, partnership interests, private businesses annually is enormously complex and feels invasive to the public.

What Would Work

Madoff proposes a more achievable path:

  1. Tax gains at transfer (Canada’s model): Tax unrealized gains when property is sold, gifted, OR passed at death—eliminating the step-up loophole.
  2. Equalize capital gains and ordinary income rates (with inflation adjustment), as Reagan’s 1986 Tax Reform Act briefly did.
  3. Tax inheritances as income to recipients. Currently, you could receive $100 billion with no reporting requirement—find $100 on the street and you must report it.
  4. Close the life insurance loophole, a favorite vehicle of the superwealthy.

The Precedent for Reform

The 1986 Tax Reform Act proves major reform is possible. It eliminated tax shelters that had allowed high-earning professionals to offset income with paper losses, broadening the base so effectively that salaried high-earners genuinely pay significant taxes today.

Why It Matters Beyond Revenue

Klein and Madoff agree the moral stakes extend beyond fairness: - Social solidarity: When people see those above them escaping taxes, they feel like suckers. - Political power: Concentrated wealth (the top 1% holds 32% of US wealth, highest since the 1920s) produces concentrated political power—see Elon Musk. - Fiscal necessity: Interest on national debt now exceeds military spending at $1 trillion annually—the third-largest federal expense.

Book Recommendations

  • The Age of Extraction – Tim Wu
  • The Rise and Fall of the Neoliberal Order – Gary Gerstle
  • Crossroads – Jonathan Franzen

Bottom line: The wealthiest Americans have effectively been written out of the tax system—not because they’re villains, but because decades of loopholes have created parallel rules. Fixing it requires reforming how we tax investment gains and inheritances, not punishing the rich.
