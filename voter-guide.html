San Francisco Housing 2026: A Voter’s Page on the CA-11 Race

A four-minute read. Linked appendices for the voter who wants the evidence.

What’s actually changed

For most of the last fifteen years, San Francisco produced about 2,500 housing units a year against a need that ran four to five times higher. That story is the one most voters carry into the 2026 election. It is now partly out of date.

In the last twenty-four months, the policy stack has shifted. The federal Low Income Housing Tax Credit was expanded in a bipartisan deal that drove California’s 2025 affordable approvals to 25,781 units, a 63 percent jump. A $10 billion state housing bond is moving through the California legislature and is likely to appear on the November 2026 ballot for voter approval — its final form and procedural path are still under determination, but it is the largest pending fiscal commitment in the housing pipeline. SB 79, signed in 2024, requires every city to allow mid-rise housing within a half-mile of high-quality transit. AB 2011 upzoned commercial corridors. The Family Zoning Plan passed the Board of Supervisors in late 2024. Factory-built housing rules are being rewritten. San Francisco’s own affordable deliveries have run roughly 1,200 units per year in 2024 and 2025, well above the 650-unit average from 2018-2023.

The binding question for 2026-2031 is no longer whether more intervention is needed. It is whether the intervention now in motion — and the intervention voters themselves will weigh in on this November — will be allowed to work, or whether new layers of friction and centralized control will be added on top of it.

What it actually costs

San Francisco’s publicly financed affordable housing currently costs about $1.04 million per unit. That figure understates the true cost. At 1979 Mission Street, the public pro forma lists land acquisition at zero — but the parcel cost $40 million, paid through earlier inclusionary negotiations and absorbed into the public capital stack. MOHCD’s own published per-unit land cost is roughly $120,000. The honest fully-loaded number at 1979 Mission is closer to $1.16 million per unit.

By contrast, private mixed-income projects in San Francisco are delivering at $543,000 per unit. Oakland’s oWOW is delivering factory-built affordable units at $350,000 with no city gap financing. Mercy Housing’s design-build model is delivering at $530,000.

The arithmetic implication is direct. Letting the private market scale at the upper income tiers preserves public subsidy dollars for the 0-30% AMI tier, where no private mechanism can close the gap and where deep public investment is irreducible. Applying the most expensive model uniformly across the income ladder is a choice with a measurable cost: roughly two units forgone for every one unit built.

Bottom line

Scott Wiener arrives as the leading legislative practitioner of the policy stack now beginning to deliver. His record — SB 35, SB 423, SB 9, SB 79 — is the legal architecture beneath the Family Zoning Plan and most of what is currently moving in California housing. His $10K-per-unit municipal payment is independently aligned with what the political-science research identifies as the binding constraint: positive financial incentives at the jurisdictional level. His weaknesses are real and specifiable: he does not engage prevailing-wage premiums or the LIHTC factory-built scoring penalty, and his framework underdevelops the 0-30% AMI tier where federal public-housing authority is the binding instrument.

Saikat Chakrabarti offers the most institutionally ambitious response in the field. His RFC is precisely tailored to the financing gap in the entitled-but-unbuilt pipeline, and his commitment to Faircloth repeal addresses the deepest-subsidy tier directly. He undermines his own thesis by dismissing Austin’s supply-side success as a market accident — the cleanest contemporary evidence that production at scale moves rents — and his framework concentrates federal power in a DC institution at exactly the moment when decentralized mechanisms (LIHTC expansion, the pending state bond, factory-built reform, transit-oriented upzoning) are beginning to deliver without it.

Connie Chan represents the position the evidence most directly contests: skepticism of private capital across the income spectrum and continued reliance on the publicly-stacked production model whose true per-unit cost the cost data has now made transparent.

What to watch

Whether the $10 billion state housing bond clears the legislature and reaches the November 2026 ballot, and what it funds if voters approve it. The federal LIHTC reauthorization and whether it reforms factory-built scoring rules. Whether the Family Zoning Plan’s entitlements convert to construction starts in 2026. Whether prevailing-wage and impact-fee structures become legible as cost drivers in the political conversation, or remain off-limits.
