US-China Summit Analysis: Trump Meets Xi in Beijing

Executive Summary

Former Deputy Secretary of State Kurt Campbell, widely regarded as the foremost US expert on Asia diplomacy, joins former National Security Adviser Jake Sullivan and former Deputy National Security Adviser John Finer on “The Long Game” podcast to analyze the Trump-Xi summit in Beijing. Campbell argues that Xi enters the meeting with significant confidence, believing China is in ascendance and that Trump can be manipulated, while Trump arrives underprepared, surrounded by businessmen rather than Asia experts. The conversation covers Taiwan, semiconductor export controls, Iran’s intersection with the summit agenda, and the broader risk that Trump may make improvised concessions that undermine decades of bipartisan US strategy in the Indo-Pacific. Campbell’s core warning is that staffers should aim simply for the “diplomatic Hippocratic oath: do no harm.”

TL;DR

Kurt Campbell warns Trump is dangerously underprepared for Beijing while Xi arrives with a detailed plan to extract concessions on Taiwan, technology, and trade.

Key Points

  • Xi Jinping’s key ideological adviser, Wang Huning, has long argued the US is in terminal decline, citing January 6th, infrastructure decay, economic dysfunction, and the DOGE cuts as evidence; this worldview shapes China’s summit strategy
  • China’s approach in Trump’s second term has shifted from the off-balance reaction of the first term to a deliberate game plan: treat Trump with personal flattery while responding to every trade measure with precise, calibrated countermeasures
  • Campbell describes the summit as “single combat” — China has prepared exhaustively while Trump reportedly did minimal preparation and dispatched Treasury Secretary Bessent only at the eleventh hour
  • On Taiwan, Campbell warns Trump may signal weakening US commitment by shifting from “do not support independence” to “oppose independence,” deferring arms sales, or saying something flattering about Xi as a “peacemaker” — any of which would send shockwaves through Japan, South Korea, and Australia
  • The Trump administration contains two factions: a “let’s do deals and make money” group currently in the ascendant, and a “China is an existential threat” group — the former appears to be driving summit posture
  • On semiconductors, Campbell criticizes the Trump administration for relaxing Biden-era chip export controls; he argues selling chips to China allows Beijing to advance while using purchases strategically to slow-walk its own domestic chip development until it can indigenize
  • Iran has unexpectedly become a central summit topic; the Straits of Hormuz closure is causing cascading economic damage across Asia including to China, but Campbell expects Beijing to apply only quiet “encouragement” rather than real pressure on Tehran
  • John Culver’s intelligence assessment is cited: China now leads or matches the US in missiles, space, cyber, air-to-air missiles, and electronic warfare — with US advantages narrowed primarily to submarines and undersea warfare
  • Campbell worries about a revival of “G2” framing — Trump has repeatedly used the phrase, and China will try to use the summit to suggest the two superpowers are jointly managing the Indo-Pacific over the heads of US allies
  • The broader Indo-Pacific strategy built across the Biden administration — including India relations, Taiwan engagement, allied semiconductor controls — is described as “all at risk,” with the US-India relationship currently “in the gutter”

Context & Background

This episode of “The Long Game” was recorded on the eve of Donald Trump’s summit with Xi Jinping in Beijing — Trump’s first visit to China since 2017 and the first by any US president in nearly a decade. The hosts, Jake Sullivan (former National Security Adviser) and John Finer (former Deputy National Security Adviser), are both veterans of the Biden administration’s China and Indo-Pacific strategy. The transcript is largely clean and coherent despite being auto-captioned. The intended audience is foreign policy professionals, informed citizens, and national security practitioners who have baseline familiarity with US-China relations, Taiwan’s political status, semiconductor export controls, and the ongoing Iran conflict (described in this alternate-timeline scenario as an active war involving US military operations and the closure of the Strait of Hormuz).

Takeaways & Implications

Campbell’s analysis suggests the most dangerous outcome of this summit is not dramatic confrontation but quiet concession — Trump improvising favorable signals to Xi on Taiwan, technology access, or investment that erode strategic positions built over decades without any formal policy change. The “do no harm” standard may itself be aspirational given the structural imbalance: an exhaustively prepared Chinese side facing an improvisational US president with minimal expert support. For allied nations in the Indo-Pacific — Japan, South Korea, Australia, Taiwan, India — the summit is an anxiety-inducing moment where US reliability as a strategic partner is effectively on trial, and the absence of pre-summit consultations with those allies compounds the risk of damage that outlasts any single meeting.
