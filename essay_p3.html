PART THREE: THE CANDIDATES

What Voters Actually Heard — and What the Evidence Says About It

Introduction: From Theory to the Room

There is a particular kind of political meeting that San Francisco does better than almost any other American city. It happens in community centers and church basements and neighborhood libraries, and it follows a recognizable format: a folding table at the front, a microphone that cuts out at inconvenient moments, an audience of people who have done their homework and are not afraid to say so. The D9 Neighbors for Housing candidate nights at the Mission community center are exactly this kind of meeting — earnest, informed, occasionally contentious, and conducted by citizens who understand that housing policy is not an abstraction but the material condition of their daily lives.

It is worth pausing on that before diving into candidate evaluation. Parts One and Two of this document covered a lot of analytical ground — RAND cost ratios, AMI scissors effects, capital stack archetypes, the New Deal left versus the New Left, the organizational ecosystem of Mission District advocacy groups, the RHNA clock. That ground needed to be covered because the housing crisis in San Francisco is genuinely complex, and the political debate about it is genuinely consequential. But the voters in that community center room are not reading RAND reports. They are asking a simpler and more urgent version of the same questions: Can I afford to stay here? Will my kids be able to live in this city? Is this candidate going to do something real, or just say the right things?

Part Three tries to honor both registers simultaneously. It evaluates Saikat Chakrabarti’s appearance at the D9 Neighbors for Housing event against the analytical framework developed in Parts One and Two — which is the honest thing to do if you want to assess whether his proposals would actually work. But it also tries to capture what the conversation in that room actually felt like: the back-and-forth between a candidate with a genuinely ambitious policy vision and an audience that has heard ambitious policy visions before and wants to know what is different this time.

The transcripts of Scott Wiener and Connie Chan’s appearances are forthcoming and will complete this section. For now, the focus is Chakrabarti — what he said, what he didn’t say, and what the evidence suggests voters should make of both.

Chapter Eight: Saikat Chakrabarti at D9 Neighbors for Housing

Who Is in the Room

Before evaluating what Chakrabarti said, it helps to understand who he was saying it to. The D9 Neighbors for Housing audience is not a typical campaign crowd. These are residents of District 9 — the Mission, Bernal Heights, portions of the Excelsior — who have organized specifically around housing issues. Many of them have direct experience with the dynamics described in Part Two: they have watched neighbors receive Ellis Act notices, seen beloved businesses close as rents rose, attended planning commission hearings for and against various developments, and parsed the difference between “affordable” as a legal term of art and “affordable” as a description of what their families can actually pay.

The host’s questions reflect this sophistication. She asks Chakrabarti about his housing plan “on different timelines” — two years, four years, ten years — because she understands that housing policy operates on long horizons and that a congressman’s first-term leverage looks very different from their long-term vision. She asks him to explain the Faircloth Amendment, then catches herself and asks for a “brief explanation” when she realizes she may be losing the room — a small but revealing moment about the challenge of bridging policy depth and public accessibility that this entire document shares. She presses him on the Road to Housing Act, on his comparison to other U.S. cities, on why he is running for Congress rather than a local office. These are not softball questions. They are the questions of voters who want to be taken seriously.

The Three-Part Housing Framework

Chakrabarti organized his housing vision around three components, which the host helpfully summarized for the audience midway through the exchange:

“There are three main components. The first is federal funding for cities that approve housing quickly. Second is a public financing entity for housing that the market isn’t building. And the third is repealing the Faircloth Amendment to allow new public housing.”

This is a useful organizing frame, and it maps onto the income ladder analysis from Part One in ways that are worth making explicit. The first component — federal funding tied to permitting speed — addresses the process vetocracy problem identified in Chapter Five and directly echoes the by-right permitting evidence from the Portland comparison. The second — the Reconstruction Finance Corporation — addresses the financing gap that Part One identifies as the primary near-term constraint on the 54,394 approved-but-unbuilt units in San Francisco’s pipeline. The third — repealing the Faircloth Amendment — addresses the deep-subsidy gap at the 0-30% AMI tier that Chapter Six’s income ladder analysis identifies as requiring permanent public housing solutions that private capital cannot provide.

In other words: Chakrabarti’s three-part framework maps, at least structurally, onto the three-tier policy architecture that the evidence in Part One suggests the problem actually requires. That is not nothing. Most political candidates discussing housing at community meetings do not demonstrate this level of structural coherence between their proposals and the underlying market dynamics.

On Permitting Speed and San Francisco’s Record

The host’s question about what “quickly approve” would mean in practice — and whether San Francisco would qualify today — produced one of the most candid exchanges of the evening.

Chakrabarti’s answer was unambiguous: “No.” San Francisco, he noted, takes approximately 280 days to approve new construction, compared to roughly 100 days in Austin — “more than twice, almost three times as bad as the fastest place in the country.”

This is an important moment in the transcript. Chakrabarti is speaking to an audience that includes people who have used San Francisco’s lengthy approval processes as a tool to shape or slow development in their neighborhoods. He is not soft-pedaling the comparison. He is saying, directly, that San Francisco’s approval timeline is a problem — one that his federal funding incentive program would be designed to pressure cities like San Francisco to fix.

The honest question this raises, which the host begins to probe but does not fully develop, is whether Chakrabarti believes the cause of San Francisco’s 280-day approval timeline is primarily bureaucratic inefficiency, political obstruction, or the deliberate exercise of community veto power by organized groups. His answer gestures toward all three without fully distinguishing them: “I do think at a federal level we should start creating general guidelines for how you approve housing faster that have to be adopted locally because it needs to have different needs — like in San Francisco we care about earthquake safety, not everywhere does.”

This is a politic answer. It acknowledges local variation while asserting the principle that federal incentives for speed are appropriate. It does not directly confront the organizational ecosystem described in Chapter Four’s section on The Organizational Ecosystem and Its Political Power — the Plaza 16 Coalition, CEQA deployment as a delay mechanism, discretionary review appeals — as contributors to that 280-day timeline. Whether this reflects a genuine belief that bureaucratic reform alone can achieve speed improvements, or a political calculation about what the D9 Neighbors for Housing audience wants to hear, is not entirely clear from the transcript.

What is clear is that his RHNA analog — a national framework similar to California’s Regional Housing Needs Assessment process, but with federal technical assistance and financing muscle behind it — is a structurally sound idea. The RHNA process works, at the level of identifying need and assigning targets, but it lacks the financing and execution infrastructure to ensure that identified need translates into built units. A federal analog with RFC-style financing attached would address exactly this gap. The Road to Housing Act, which he endorses as “a good first step,” moves modestly in this direction with $200 million in permitting streamlining grants — a figure he correctly characterizes as insufficient for the scale of the problem.

The RFC — The Most Distinctive and Most Debated Proposal

The Reconstruction Finance Corporation proposal is where Chakrabarti distinguishes himself most clearly from the conventional range of housing policy proposals, and it deserves careful evaluation against the evidence assembled in this document.

His explanation of the RFC concept in the D9 transcript is worth quoting at some length, because the historical analogy he uses is illuminating:

“I’m thinking of it as more like a public development bank… The RFC wasn’t just a public investment bank, it was a project manager. It would go out and find problems and find bottlenecks and push and actively make sure stuff got done, things got built, and do whatever was necessary to push things along.”

He illustrates the concept with the synthetic rubber story — how the WWII-era RFC pushed chemical manufacturers step by step through the development of mass-production synthetic rubber despite industry resistance at every stage, spinning off a government corporation when private actors wouldn’t move, providing financing where needed, and using every available tool to solve each successive bottleneck as it appeared. The result: more synthetic rubber produced within 18 months than existed as natural rubber in the world.

The analogy is instructive precisely because it captures what is missing from both sides of San Francisco’s current housing debate. The far-left’s social housing framework has the right instinct — public institutions should be directly involved in housing production, not just in subsidizing it — but lacks a theory of how to deploy public capital proactively across the full range of bottlenecks, from land acquisition to construction financing to materials supply chains. The YIMBY/abundance framework has the right instinct about process reform and private capital leverage, but lacks a mechanism for filling the financing gap that prevents 54,394 approved units from breaking ground regardless of what the zoning says.

The RFC, as Chakrabarti describes it, is neither a pure subsidy program nor a pure market-facilitation mechanism. It is an active problem-solving institution — one that goes looking for the specific constraint that is preventing a specific project or class of projects from being built, and then deploys whatever financial and organizational tool is needed to remove that constraint. Applied to San Francisco’s housing crisis, this would mean: identifying why the 54,394 approved units are not financing, finding the specific interest rate, loan structure, or equity return requirement that is preventing each category of project from proceeding, and either providing the financing directly, guaranteeing the private financing, or spinning off a public developer to build the projects that private capital won’t touch.

This is a more sophisticated theory of public intervention than either the traditional gap financing model (wait for projects to apply for subsidy and then fill the gap) or the traditional public housing model (government builds and owns housing, full stop). It is also a more ambitious one, requiring institutional capacity, political will, and sustained funding at a scale that has not existed in American housing policy since the postwar era.

The host’s question about how much federal funding this would realistically require gets a candid answer: “It should be billions and billions of dollars.” Chakrabarti’s reference to the Housing Accelerator Fund — a philanthropic model that provides low-interest revolving loans for affordable housing — as a proof-of-concept for what the RFC could do at federal scale is useful, though the jump from philanthropic revolving loan fund to federal public development bank is considerably larger than the analogy suggests.

The most important thing the RFC proposal gets right, from the perspective of Part One’s analysis, is the proactive vs. reactive distinction. Every existing federal housing finance program is reactive: it waits for projects to be proposed, applies for funding, meet eligibility criteria, and then considers whether to fill the gap. The RFC model is proactive: it goes out and finds the bottlenecks, then deploys whatever tools are needed to remove them. This is exactly the institutional posture that the 60,000-unit entitled-but-unfinanced pipeline demands. Those units don’t need someone to wait for them to apply for gap financing. They need someone to figure out, project by project and category by category, why the math doesn’t work and what change in the financing structure would make it work.

On Austin — The Moment That Deserves the Most Scrutiny

No moment in the Chakrabarti transcript rewards closer reading against the evidence in Part One than his answer to the host’s question: “Which U.S. city do you think is effective at building housing today, and why are they doing well policy-wise?”

His answer:

“It’s actually tough to find a U.S. city… People point to Austin and Austin has done decently, but Austin has also done it with a lot of sprawl, right? And they built out a ton and the other thing about Austin that doesn’t — why I think it’s a hard model to replicate — is the private developers basically made a mistake. They overbuilt, uh, you know, right during and right after the pandemic, and so they’re starting to build down. There’s also had, you know, outflow of residents from Austin after the pandemic, and that’s part of why the cost of rental went down. And I don’t think we can always rely on private developers making a mistake.”

This answer has three components, and they deserve to be evaluated separately.

On sprawl: Chakrabarti is partially correct that Austin’s growth pattern has included significant suburban sprawl, which carries real costs — longer commutes, car dependence, infrastructure expenses, and environmental impacts. This is a legitimate critique of Austin as a model for a dense, transit-oriented city like San Francisco. San Francisco cannot solve its housing crisis by building outward; it must build upward and inward, which requires different policy tools than Austin’s primarily greenfield development pattern.

On “private developers made a mistake”: This framing is directly contested by the supply-side evidence presented in Chapter Six’s section on What the Far Left Gets Wrong. Austin sustained production of approximately 15,000 to 20,000 units per year at its peak — a rate corresponding to the 3x-6x multiplier that the stock/flow analysis identifies as necessary to bend the price curve. Vacancy rates rose from approximately 5% to 12% as that supply came online. Rents fell by 8-15% across unit types. This is not a market accident. It is supply-and-demand mechanics operating exactly as predicted when supply is deployed at sufficient scale. Characterizing it as developers “making a mistake” by overbuilding is analytically equivalent to saying that the synthetic rubber program worked because the government accidentally made too much rubber. The mechanism worked. The “mistake” framing obscures the mechanism.

The RAND data is unambiguous on this point: Austin’s production rate, sustained over multiple years, produced measurable rent relief through the vacancy-rate channel. San Francisco’s production rate — 2,500 units per year against a demand base that has grown by hundreds of thousands of jobs since 2009 — has never come close to testing the supply mechanism. You cannot conclude that supply-side solutions “don’t work” in San Francisco based on a production level that is approximately one-sixth of what the stock/flow analysis suggests would be required to move the price curve.

On population outflow: Chakrabarti is correct that Austin experienced some net out-migration in the post-pandemic period, and that this contributed to rent relief alongside the supply increase. But the out-migration was itself partly a consequence of the affordability improvement — people who could not afford Austin at peak-boom prices moved there once rents moderated. The causal story is more circular and more supply-driven than “people left, so rents fell” implies.

What makes this moment in the transcript particularly important is its political context. Chakrabarti is speaking to a D9 audience that includes people skeptical of market-rate development and sympathetic to the Martí framework. His Austin skepticism plays well to that audience. It is also, by the standards of the evidence this document has assembled, his least defensible empirical claim. A candidate whose central housing proposal is an RFC designed to deploy public capital proactively to unlock stalled housing production should be the last person dismissing the most significant supply-side success story in recent American urban history as a market accident. The Austin evidence is not an argument against the RFC — it is an argument for getting production to RFC-enabled scale. Chakrabarti’s framing inadvertently undermines his own central thesis.

On the Faircloth Amendment and Public Housing

The third leg of Chakrabarti’s housing framework — repealing the Faircloth Amendment to allow net new public housing construction — is the proposal most clearly aligned with the far-left CCHO/Martí framework, and the one with the strongest long-term policy logic and the weakest near-term political path.

The Faircloth Amendment, passed in 1998, effectively prohibits the federal government from funding the net addition of public housing units beyond the stock that existed at that time. Combined with decades of chronic underfunding that has left existing public housing in serious disrepair, it has produced a federal public housing system in steady managed decline. Chakrabarti’s Green New Deal for Public Housing legislation, developed in his time as AOC’s chief of staff, attempted to address both the Faircloth prohibition and the funding deficit simultaneously.

His argument for why public housing is necessary tracks the income ladder analysis from Part One closely: every country with “a robust housing program with minimal displacement” — Finland, Singapore, Austria — has “very strong social housing programs.” The private market, even optimally incentivized, cannot serve the 0-30% AMI households for whom the gap between what they can pay and what it costs to house them is simply too large for any financing mechanism short of direct public subsidy to bridge. This is correct. It is also the point on which there is the most genuine cross-ideological agreement in the housing policy community — even ardent YIMBY advocates like Klein explicitly state support for public housing.

The political path to Faircloth repeal, however, runs through a Congress that has been systematically cutting HUD’s budget, a Republican administration that has proposed a 44% cut to HUD, and a Democratic caucus that has not made public housing a legislative priority in decades. Chakrabarti’s theory of political change — building toward a 2028 Democratic platform that includes public housing as a centerpiece, through the same inside-outside organizing model that moved climate from the margins to the center of Democratic politics between 2019 and 2020 — is credible as a long-term organizing strategy and almost certainly insufficient as a near-term legislative path for a first-term freshman representative.

His Green New Deal for Climate analogy is instructive here, in ways that are both encouraging and cautionary. The GND did succeed in moving climate from a peripheral issue to a central Democratic commitment in the span of roughly two years. It did help create the political conditions for the Inflation Reduction Act, which represented a genuine, historic investment in clean energy even if it fell short of GND ambitions. On the other hand, the IRA passed not through GND-style insurgent politics but through careful, incremental coalition-building by experienced Senate institutionalists — exactly the career-ladder DC politics that Chakrabarti explicitly rejects. The question of whether his theory of change — insurgent pressure from the outside, anti-establishment organizing on the inside — can produce legislative outcomes in housing comparable to what climate advocates achieved through a more institutionalist path is genuinely open.

On Homelessness — Connecting Root Cause to Immediate Need

The exchange on homelessness — triggered by the host’s note that the U.S. reached a record 770,000 unhoused people in 2024, with California accounting for roughly one in four — reveals both the strength and the limitation of Chakrabarti’s framing.

His insistence on addressing root causes is analytically correct: “I believe the root cause of homelessness is the cost of living crisis in the city.” The evidence supports this. The correlation between housing cost increases and homelessness rates across U.S. cities is strong and well-documented. Cities that have maintained lower housing costs have lower rates of unsheltered homelessness, controlling for other factors. The Whack-a-Mole dynamic he describes — spending more on homelessness services without addressing the underlying cost crisis produces the same number of unhoused people because the inflow continues at the same rate — is confirmed by San Francisco’s own experience, where per-capita homelessness spending has increased dramatically while the unhoused population has remained stubbornly persistent.

But the host’s question implicitly presses for what happens in the interim — for the “few thousand people” in San Francisco who are unhoused right now, while the RFC is being capitalized and the Faircloth Amendment is being repealed and the national housing plan is being developed. Chakrabarti’s answer — stronger federal support for street teams, permanent supportive housing, the Continuum of Care grants, and the structural healthcare argument for Medicare for All as part of addiction and mental health treatment — is directionally correct but thin on specifics. The near-term and the long-term are both real, and a voter who is watching someone live in a tent outside her neighborhood park wants to know both what the long-term plan is and what happens while the long-term plan is being built.

On His Competitors — A Careful Distinction

The host’s direct question — “What is one major way that your housing plan differs from Scott Wiener and Connie Chan, and why is that meaningful?” — produced Chakrabarti’s most explicitly comparative answer:

“The other candidates don’t have a real plan for national finance or institutional change on how you’re financing federal housing. I know that Supervisor Chan agrees with me on repealing the Faircloth Amendment, and I know Senator Wiener calls for more investment in housing, but I do believe we have to be institutional changes here. This is not a one-time fix.”

This is a fair characterization of his distinctive contribution, and it maps directly onto the analysis in Part One. The RFC proposal is structurally different from either incremental investment increases (Wiener’s anticipated position) or targeted deep subsidy advocacy (Chan’s anticipated position) because it proposes to change the institutional architecture of federal housing finance — not just add more money to existing channels, but create a new proactive institution capable of finding and removing bottlenecks that existing programs cannot address.

His sharper critique — that going to DC and “working your way up the ranks” by supporting leadership and raising money is an institutional structure incapable of producing the scale of change required — is more politically charged and raises genuine questions about his theory of legislative effectiveness. Is it true that the career-ladder model cannot produce transformative housing legislation? The IRA’s climate provisions suggest that it can, at least when the political conditions are right. Is it true that insurgent anti-establishment politics can produce transformative housing legislation? The Green New Deal’s failure to pass Congress suggests the limits of that approach as well.

The honest answer is probably that both models are necessary at different stages of the policy cycle — insurgent politics to shift the Overton window and create political demand for ambitious action, institutionalist politics to translate that demand into enacted legislation. Chakrabarti’s theory of change is well-suited to the first phase and underspecified on the second.

Preliminary Scorecard: Chakrabarti Against the Evidence

Before Wiener and Chan have presented, a complete comparative evaluation is not possible. But it is possible to assess Chakrabarti’s performance against the evaluative framework established at the end of Part Two — the set of questions that the evidence demands any serious housing candidate be able to answer.

Does the candidate understand the stock/flow distinction? Partially. Chakrabarti understands that the crisis is structural and requires large-scale, sustained intervention. His acknowledgment that 70,000 entitled units are sitting unbuilt because “the financing isn’t there” demonstrates stock/flow literacy. His Austin “market mistake” framing, however, suggests he underestimates the scale of supply response required to move the price curve — a framing inconsistent with the stock/flow analysis. Assessment: Solid, with a significant blind spot.

Does the candidate have a theory of construction cost reduction? Weakly. The RFC concept, properly implemented, could address construction cost bottlenecks through its active problem-solving mandate. But the transcript contains almost no direct engagement with the specific cost drivers identified in Part One — the soft cost premium, the timeline-driven carrying costs, the prevailing wage differential, the factory-built housing opportunity, the TCAC/CDLAC timeline mismatches that prevent modular construction from scaling. A candidate whose central proposal is a WWII-style mobilization institution should be able to name the specific production bottlenecks that institution would target. Assessment: Conceptually present, empirically thin.

Does the candidate understand the income ladder and tool differentiation? Yes, more than most. The three-part framework — permitting incentives for the market tier, RFC financing for the stalled middle, public housing for the deep-subsidy tier — maps coherently onto the income ladder analysis. The candidate demonstrates awareness that different problems at different income levels require different policy tools. Assessment: Strongest element of his housing framework.

Does the candidate have a plausible theory of political change? Ambitious but underspecified on the legislative path. The GND-to-IRA analogy is instructive but imperfect. The 2028 presidential campaign organizing theory is credible as a long-term strategy and insufficient as a 2026 first-term action plan. Assessment: Inspiring vision, uncertain near-term mechanics.

Does the candidate’s Austin/market analysis hold up against the evidence? No, not fully. The “market mistake” framing is the weakest empirical claim in the transcript. It mischaracterizes a supply-side success as a market accident, in a way that inadvertently undermines his own RFC argument. Assessment: Needs significant revision.

Does the candidate address the 54,394 unfinanced approved units? Yes — this is arguably the most important thing he gets right. His recognition that tens of thousands of entitled units are waiting for financing, not permission, is the central insight of Part One and the strongest empirical foundation for the RFC proposal. Assessment: Correct and important.

Does the candidate address RHNA compliance and its consequences? Indirectly. He references the RHNA process as a model for his national housing plan but does not directly engage with San Francisco’s specific obligation, its current non-compliance trajectory, or the Builder’s Remedy consequences of continued underproduction. Assessment: Nationally framed, locally underspecified.

What the Room Heard — and What It Might Have Missed

Voters leaving the D9 Neighbors for Housing event after Chakrabarti’s appearance heard something that is genuinely unusual in American political discourse: a congressional candidate with a detailed, historically grounded theory of how federal institutional architecture could be redesigned to solve a housing crisis that local and state policy has been unable to crack. The RFC concept is not a talking point. It is a thought-through policy framework with real intellectual depth, rooted in New Deal history, responsive to the specific financing bottlenecks that the evidence identifies as the primary near-term constraint on housing production in San Francisco and across California.

They also heard a candidate who is more comfortable with long-term structural vision than with short-term legislative mechanics, more fluent in the language of movement building than in the specifics of construction cost reduction, and more cautious about the evidence for supply-side solutions than the data in Part One would support. His Austin skepticism is the clearest example: in a presentation built around the urgent need to unlock housing production at scale, dismissing the most significant supply-side success in recent American urban history as a market accident is not just analytically weak. It is a missed opportunity to make the affirmative case for why supply matters — and why the RFC he is proposing would matter if it succeeded in replicating Austin’s production levels in California’s more constrained market.

The voters in that room are sophisticated enough to notice these things if the evidence is put in front of them. That is what this document is attempting to do. When Wiener and Chan present their cases, they will be evaluated against the same framework — and the same evidence. The question is not which candidate has the most compelling personal narrative or the most energetic base of support. The question is which candidate has the most complete and accurate theory of a problem that is destroying the livability of a city that all three of them, in their different ways, love.
