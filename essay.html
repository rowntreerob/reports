The Colorado Affordable Housing Cost Puzzle: Analysis and Research Design## I. Framing the Question Preciselyattempting an answer, it is worth being precise about what the RAND data actually show regarding Colorado’s anomaly, because the specificity matters for both hypothesis generation and research design.RAND findings establish the following:- Texas: LIHTC hard costs are 1.5x market-rate hard costs; LIHTC TDC per NRSF is $228 vs. $157 for market rate — a roughly 50% premium- California: LIHTC TDC per NRSF is $640 vs. $415 for market rate — a 1.5x premium at NRSF level, wider still in hard costs at 1.7x- Colorado: LIHTC TDC per NRSF is $249 vs. $303 for market rate — LIHTC is actually cheaper than market rate, the only state in the sample where this is true. Hard costs for LIHTC in Colorado are $183 vs. $237 for market rate, a ratio of 0.8the question is not merely "why is Colorado’s LIHTC premium small" — it is the more striking question of why Colorado LIHTC projects cost less than Colorado market-rate projects, a relationship that is inverted relative to every other state in the sample. Ward himself describes this as "one of the mysteries that came out of this research" and explicitly states he does not yet have an answer.—## II. What We Can Rule Out or Constrainproposing hypotheses, it is useful to eliminate explanations that the existing RAND data allow us to dismiss or significantly discount.### Not Explainable by Land CostsLIHTC land costs per NRSF are $1.50, versus $23 for Colorado market-rate projects. This is the lowest land cost in the entire analysis. While subsidized land programs are a plausible contributor, the RAND methodology already excludes projects with near-zero land costs to filter out free-land programs. So even after that filter, LIHTC land costs in Colorado are extraordinarily low — roughly 6% of market-rate land costs in the same state. This is a meaningful data point but likely reflects site selection patterns (LIHTC projects in lower-cost suburban or exurban Colorado locations) rather than a structural policy explanation for the hard cost differential.### Not Primarily a Unit-Size ArtifactRAND per-unit analysis shows Colorado LIHTC TDC per unit at $94,000 vs. $193,000 for market rate — a ratio of 0.5. This means LIHTC units in Colorado are both cheaper per NRSF and substantially smaller per unit. Unit downsizing is clearly a partial mechanism, but it cannot by itself explain why hard costs per NRSF are lower for LIHTC than market rate, since smaller units generally increase cost per NRSF (more kitchens and bathrooms per square foot of rentable space). The fact that Colorado LIHTC hard costs are lower per NRSF despite this dynamic makes the finding more, not less, puzzling.### Not Explained by Prevailing Wageexplicitly notes in the podcast that prevailing wage requirements on LIHTC projects are "at minimum much less common in Colorado and Texas." The RAND report estimates prevailing wage requirements explain 40-56% of California LIHTC hard cost premiums. Since Colorado does not appear to impose these requirements systematically on LIHTC projects, this removes what is otherwise the single largest driver of LIHTC cost premiums in California. However, Texas also largely lacks prevailing wage requirements, yet still shows a 1.5x LIHTC-to-market-rate cost ratio. So the absence of prevailing wage is a necessary but not sufficient explanation for Colorado’s result.### Not Simply a Soft Cost Storycosts in Colorado for LIHTC are $75/NRSF vs. $44 for market rate — a ratio of 1.7x, which is actually similar to the California market-rate to LIHTC soft cost ratio pattern and consistent with the complexity of LIHTC compliance, reporting, and financing across all states. The Colorado anomaly is concentrated in hard costs and land costs, not soft costs. Soft costs in Colorado follow the expected pattern of being higher for LIHTC than market rate.—## III. Plausible Hypotheseswhat can be ruled out, the following hypotheses remain viable and are ranked roughly by their prior probability based on available evidence.### Hypothesis 1: Colorado’s LIHTC Projects Are Systematically Simpler in Building Type and ConfigurationRAND model controls for building type using fixed effects, but the Colorado LIHTC sample may be disproportionately composed of garden-style, low-rise, wood-frame construction in suburban or smaller metro locations compared to Colorado market-rate projects, which may skew toward podium or wrap construction in the Denver metro area where land costs and density pressures are higher.-style construction is the lowest-cost building typology. If Colorado LIHTC developers have been more disciplined or more geographically constrained toward simpler building types than Colorado market-rate developers — who may be chasing higher-density urban infill — this could produce a real hard cost advantage even after the regression’s building-type controls, if the within-category variation is large and the Colorado LIHTC sample is concentrated at the low-cost end of the garden-type distribution.*Why this is plausible**: Denver’s market-rate development has increasingly concentrated in urban infill podium construction, which is inherently more expensive even within the same building-type category. Colorado’s LIHTC pipeline may reflect a different geographic and typological distribution.*Limitation: RAND’s building-type fixed effects should partially capture this, but if Colorado LIHTC projects are systematically simpler even within the garden category — smaller footprints, less complex site conditions, fewer amenity packages — the model would not fully capture it.—### Hypothesis 2: Colorado Has Unusually Streamlined or Consolidated LIHTC Funding Requirementsrepeatedly emphasizes that the "union of all requirements" across multiple funding sources is a primary driver of LIHTC cost escalation — every funding source adds prescriptive design, labor, and amenity requirements that stack multiplicatively. California projects routinely carry six to eight funding sources, each with independent requirements.’s CHFA (Colorado Housing and Finance Authority) administers both federal and state LIHTC. Colorado does not have a large independent municipal affordable housing funding apparatus comparable to LAHD or the San Francisco Mayor’s Office of Housing. This means Colorado LIHTC projects may typically have shallower capital stacks with fewer independent funding sources**, resulting in fewer layered requirements.a Colorado LIHTC project can be completed with two or three funding sources rather than six or eight, the prescriptive requirement burden drops dramatically. This would directly reduce architectural and engineering costs, eliminate certain labor compliance requirements, and reduce the complexity of the design process itself.*Why this is plausible**: Colorado’s affordable housing ecosystem is less mature and less well-capitalized than California’s, which perversely may mean developers face less requirement accretion. The absence of a large municipal funding apparatus that would otherwise add requirements is itself a structural feature.*Why this needs testing**: It is possible that Colorado simply has fewer very-low-income projects requiring deep subsidy stacks, and that the projects in the RAND sample represent a less severely income-targeted population than California’s, rather than a genuinely streamlined compliance environment.—### Hypothesis 3: Colorado LIHTC Developers Have Adopted More Standardized or Industrialized Construction Approachesstate LIHTC markets have developed what practitioners call "developer industrialization" — a small number of repeat developers who build essentially the same building repeatedly, achieving cost reductions through repetition, established subcontractor relationships, pre-negotiated material pricing, and design reuse. This is more common in less competitive, less politically complex markets where the same developers win allocations repeatedly.’s LIHTC market, administered through CHFA with a relatively concentrated allocation pool, may have fostered a small group of sophisticated repeat developers who have effectively industrialized their production process. These developers may be building near-identical projects in different locations, reusing design drawings, using the same subcontractors at pre-negotiated rates, and achieving genuine economies of repetition that are not available to California developers who face idiosyncratic site-specific requirements from multiple funding sources on every project.*Why this is plausible**: The podcast transcript notes Ward has been contacted by people asking "what is going on in Colorado" — suggesting the result is recognized as unusual by practitioners, not just researchers. Practitioner recognition often points toward structural market features like developer concentration.*Why this needs testing: RAND’s sample of Colorado LIHTC projects may be small enough that a few unusually efficient developers could drive the average down. The finding could be real but not generalizable if it reflects two or three highly efficient operators rather than a systemic feature of Colorado’s market.—### Hypothesis 4: Colorado’s Regulatory Environment Produces Less Schedule-Driven Cost Inflation for LIHTC Projects SpecificallyRAND report establishes that longer timelines are a primary driver of higher costs, with pre-development time in California more than twice that of Texas. If Colorado’s approval and permitting processes for LIHTC projects are faster than for market-rate projects — perhaps because LIHTC projects receive expedited processing, by-right treatment, or preferential permitting — then the timeline cost driver would work in reverse** for LIHTC versus market rate in Colorado.Colorado municipalities have adopted fast-track processes for affordable housing specifically, which could mean that the pre-development and construction timelines for LIHTC projects in Colorado are genuinely shorter than for market-rate projects, while in California and Texas the reverse is true (LIHTC projects take longer due to compliance complexity).*Why this is plausible**: Colorado Governor Polis has championed housing reform aggressively, and some Colorado cities have adopted affordable housing priority processing. If this produces meaningfully shorter timelines for LIHTC projects specifically, it could directly translate into hard cost savings.*Limitation**: RAND’s timeline data is only available for market-rate projects (Figure 3.4), so there is no direct timeline comparison for LIHTC projects across states in the existing research. This is itself a significant data gap.—### Hypothesis 5: Selection Effects in the RAND Colorado LIHTC Sampleis the most cautious hypothesis and the one Ward would likely want to test first as a researcher. The Colorado LIHTC sample in RAND’s data may be too small or geographically concentrated to support a robust conclusion about systemic cost differences. If the Colorado LIHTC sample is dominated by projects in lower-cost suburban Denver locations, or if it happens to include several unusually efficient projects built by a single developer, the finding could be a statistical artifact rather than a structural phenomenon.RAND report acknowledges sample size limitations and notes that some estimates "do not reach the level of statistical significance at the 95 percent confidence level." Given that Colorado LIHTC projects represent a subset of an already modest overall LIHTC sample of 88 projects across three states, the Colorado-specific LIHTC cell may contain relatively few observations.*Why this must be taken seriously**: Ward himself acknowledges uncertainty about the Colorado finding in the podcast. The fact that it is described as a "mystery" rather than a robust finding suggests the research team is appropriately cautious about its generalizability.—## IV. Research Design for RAND or a Similar Organizationthat a conclusive answer cannot be reached from existing data, the following research program would be capable of resolving the question rigorously.—### Study 1: Expanded Colorado LIHTC Cost Census*Purpose**: Determine whether the RAND finding holds across a larger, more representative sample of Colorado LIHTC projects, or whether it reflects sample composition artifacts.*Data Sources**:- Complete LIHTC cost certification data from CHFA for all completed projects from 2015-2024 (public records request, same methodology as RAND’s existing LIHTC data collection)- Supplementary data from Colorado state LIHTC programs- Cross-reference with CHFA’s own project databases for building type, location, developer identity, and funding source composition*Key Variables to Collect**:- Hard costs, soft costs, land costs per NRSF and per unit- Building type and number of stories- Geographic location (Denver metro vs. other Colorado markets)- Number of funding sources in capital stack- Identity of developer (to test for developer concentration effects)- Number of units and average unit size- Project timeline (predevelopment and construction phases separately)- Whether prevailing wage was paid- Number and identity of funding sources*Analytic Approach**:- Replicate RAND’s regression model on the expanded Colorado sample to test whether the market-rate/LIHTC cost inversion persists with more data- Decompose the Colorado LIHTC cost advantage into building type, location, unit size, and developer identity components to identify how much each explains- Test whether the finding is robust to exclusion of any single developer or geographic cluster*Sample Size Target**: Minimum 60-80 Colorado LIHTC projects to support meaningful subgroup analysis*Timeline**: 12-18 months for data collection and cleaning given the RAND team’s established methodology—### Study 2: Capital Stack Complexity and Requirement Accretion Analysis*Purpose**: Directly test whether Colorado LIHTC projects have simpler capital stacks and fewer prescriptive requirements than California and Texas projects, and quantify the cost implications of requirement differences.*Data Sources**:- LIHTC cost certifications (already partially available from RAND’s existing data)- Funding source documentation for each project (loan agreements, grant agreements, regulatory agreements) obtained through public records requests to CHFA, CDOH, and comparable California and Texas agencies- Interviews with 20-30 LIHTC developers who have built in at least two of the three states (purposive sample targeting developers with cross-state experience)- Design standards documents from major funding sources in each state (LAHD, LACDA, CHFA, TDHCA)*Key Constructs to Measure**:- Number of funding sources per project by state- Number of distinct design/labor/amenity requirements per funding source- Total number of non-overlapping requirements per project (the "union" measure Ward describes)- Architectural and engineering fee as a percentage of hard costs by state and funding source count- Presence/absence of prevailing wage requirements by funding source*Analytic Approach**:- Construct a "requirement burden index" for each project based on the count and stringency of funding source requirements- Regress hard costs and A&E fees on the requirement burden index while controlling for building type, location, and timeline- Compare the distribution of requirement burden index values across states to test whether Colorado LIHTC projects systematically carry lower requirement burdens- Use developer interviews to validate quantitative findings and identify requirements that practitioners identify as most cost-consequential*Novel Contribution**: This would be the first study to directly quantify the cost of requirement accretion in LIHTC capital stacks, addressing a gap that Ward identifies in the podcast as important but unmeasured.—### Study 3: LIHTC Production Timeline Comparison Across States*Purpose**: Test whether Colorado LIHTC projects have shorter timelines than market-rate projects in the same state, and whether timeline differences explain the hard cost inversion.*Data Sources**:- CHFA project approval records (application date, award date, construction start date, placed-in-service date) for all Colorado LIHTC projects 2015-2024- Comparable timeline data from California Tax Credit Allocation Committee (TCAC) and Texas Department of Housing and Community Affairs (TDHCA)- RAND’s existing market-rate timeline data for Colorado and Texas (17 California, 7 Colorado, 26 Texas projects)*Key Variables**:- Predevelopment time: LIHTC application date to construction start- Construction time: construction start to placed-in-service date- Total timeline: application to placed-in-service- Whether project received expedited/priority processing- Municipality of project location*Analytic Approach**:- Compare LIHTC and market-rate timelines within each state using the same regression framework as RAND’s existing timeline analysis- Test whether Colorado LIHTC projects have shorter timelines than Colorado market-rate projects (which would be consistent with fast-track processing for affordable housing)- Estimate the cost implications of observed timeline differences using RAND’s established elasticity of cost to production time (~$1,284 per unit per month)- Map timeline data against municipal fast-track affordable housing policies to test for a policy-timeline-cost causal chain*Key Test**: If Colorado LIHTC predevelopment times are shorter than Colorado market-rate predevelopment times, while the reverse is true in California and Texas, this would strongly support the regulatory fast-track hypothesis and explain the hard cost inversion.—### Study 4: Developer Concentration and Industrialization Analysis*Purpose**: Test whether Colorado’s LIHTC cost efficiency is driven by a small number of highly efficient repeat developers using standardized construction approaches, versus being a market-wide phenomenon.*Data Sources**:- CHFA allocation records identifying developer entity for all Colorado LIHTC projects 2010-2024- Cost certification data linked to developer identity- Structured interviews with the five largest Colorado LIHTC developers by unit count- Site visits to three to five Colorado LIHTC projects identified as low-cost outliers to assess construction approach and building configuration*Key Questions**:- What share of Colorado LIHTC units are produced by the top five developers?- Do high-volume repeat developers in Colorado show meaningfully lower costs than lower-volume developers in the same state?- Do Colorado LIHTC developers reuse design drawings across projects? Do they use standardized unit plans or pre-approved building types?- What is the nature of subcontractor relationships — are they long-term repeat relationships with negotiated pricing, or competitive bid procurement on each project?*Analytic Approach**:- Calculate a Herfindahl-Hirschman Index for developer concentration in Colorado’s LIHTC market versus California and Texas- Regress per-NRSF hard costs on developer volume (cumulative units built) within Colorado to test for learning curve or scale effects- Compare cost distributions between high-volume and low-volume Colorado LIHTC developers- Use interview and site visit data to construct qualitative case studies of the most efficient developers’ production processes*Benchmark**: Compare Colorado developer concentration and repeat-developer cost premiums to findings from analogous research in manufactured housing and modular construction where industrialization effects are well-documented.—### Study 5: Quasi-Experimental Test Using Colorado’s 2022 Energy Efficiency Law*Purpose**: Use Colorado’s 2022 passage of HB 22-1362 (which moved Colorado toward California-style energy efficiency requirements) as a natural experiment to test whether stricter energy requirements increase LIHTC costs and whether Colorado’s pre-2022 lower requirements explain part of the cost advantage.*Design**:- Identify Colorado LIHTC projects that broke ground before versus after the new requirements took effect- Compare hard costs for pre- and post-requirement projects, controlling for building type, location, and timeline- Use Texas projects as a control group (no equivalent policy change) to implement a difference-in-differences design*Identification Strategy**:- Treatment: Colorado LIHTC projects subject to new energy efficiency requirements- Control: Colorado LIHTC projects not subject to new requirements (pre-2022 construction start) and Texas LIHTC projects throughout- Key assumption: absent the policy change, Colorado and Texas LIHTC cost trends would have continued in parallel*Expected Finding**: If energy efficiency requirements meaningfully increase costs, post-2022 Colorado LIHTC projects should show higher hard costs relative to the pre-2022 baseline and relative to Texas, narrowing or eliminating Colorado’s cost advantage. If no such effect is observed, energy efficiency requirements are not a primary driver of the Colorado anomaly.*Timeline**: This study requires data from 2023-2026 completions to have sufficient post-treatment observations, making it a 3-5 year research horizon.—## V. Synthesis and Priority SequencingRAND were to pursue this research agenda, the logical sequencing would be:*Phase 1 (Years 1-2)**: Studies 1 and 3 — expanded cost census and timeline comparison. These use data that is largely already available through public records and can be collected using RAND’s established methodology. They would quickly establish whether the finding is robust (Study 1) and whether timeline differences are a primary mechanism (Study 3).*Phase 2 (Years 2-3)**: Study 2 — capital stack and requirement accretion analysis. This is the most novel and policy-relevant study but requires the most intensive data collection (obtaining and coding funding source requirement documents). It should follow confirmation of the core finding from Phase 1.*Phase 3 (Years 2-4)**: Study 4 — developer concentration analysis. This can run partially in parallel with Phase 2 using CHFA administrative data and targeted interviews. It addresses the most important alternative explanation (sample selection via developer concentration) and could be incorporated into the Phase 1 report as a robustness check if developer identity data are collected early.*Phase 4 (Years 3-5): Study 5 — natural experiment using the 2022 energy efficiency law. This is the most methodologically rigorous study but requires the longest time horizon to accumulate post-treatment observations. It would produce the strongest causal evidence on one specific mechanism and represents the kind of policy-relevant quasi-experimental work Ward mentions pursuing in the podcast.—## VI. What the Answer Would Mean for Policystakes of resolving this question extend well beyond academic interest. The RAND report calculates that if California had Colorado’s LIHTC production costs, the same $1.25 billion in recent state spending would have produced more than four times as many units. If the research program above identifies replicable structural features of Colorado’s approach — whether simpler capital stacks, faster timelines for affordable projects, developer industrialization, or some combination — those features become directly actionable policy targets** for California, New York, and other high-cost states.is correct that Colorado represents the most promising near-term opportunity to extract generalizable lessons about efficient publicly subsidized housing production. The research design outlined here would give RAND or a similar organization the evidence base to make those lessons actionable at scale.
