What the Market Is Telling Us: Price Signals, Construction Costs, and the Widening Gap Between What Housing Costs and What It Can Afford to Cost

Section 1: The Economics of Shelter — Why Market Signals Still Matter

There is a temptation, when confronting the housing crisis in San Francisco, to treat it as a problem so locally specific, so deeply embedded in the city's political culture and physical geography, that the ordinary tools of economic analysis lose their explanatory power. The city sits on a peninsula with finite land. Its neighborhoods carry the weight of decades of displacement, redevelopment battles, and demographic transformation. Its political vocabulary is saturated with the language of community, justice, and protection from market forces. In this environment, invoking supply and demand can feel almost naive — a sophomore economics lecture delivered into a room full of people who have watched the market, as they understand it, price their neighbors out of their homes for a generation.

But the laws of economics do not suspend themselves out of sensitivity to local politics. They operate in San Francisco exactly as they operate in Austin, Texas, or Denver, Colorado, or any other American city where housing costs have become a defining civic problem. What changes from city to city is not whether markets send signals, but whether the policy environment allows those signals to be heard, and whether the actors receiving them are free to respond. In San Francisco, the signals have been loud for years. What is remarkable — and what this chapter will document in detail — is how systematically the policy architecture has prevented the responses those signals demand.

To understand what follows, the reader needs a working grasp of one foundational concept from microeconomics: the role of relative prices. Unlike nominal prices — the raw dollar amounts on a price tag — relative prices express the cost of one thing in terms of another. They are ratios, and as ratios they carry information that nominal prices alone cannot convey. A rising relative price signals that a good is becoming scarcer or more valued compared to its alternatives; it tells producers to move resources toward that good, and it tells consumers to consider substitutes. A falling relative price sends the opposite message. Together, relative prices constitute the signaling system through which a market economy coordinates the decisions of millions of independent actors without any central authority directing traffic.

In housing development, the relevant relative prices include: the cost of construction labor relative to other labor markets; the cost of land relative to the density of housing that land is permitted to support; the cost of regulatory compliance relative to the value of the housing produced; and most critically for this chapter, the relative cost of producing a unit of affordable housing through a publicly financed process versus a privately financed one. Each of these ratios is sending a signal. The signals, taken together, compose a picture that is simultaneously a diagnosis of how the current system has failed and a map of where a better-functioning system might go.

The Development Decision as a Market Signal

Housing development is not a spot transaction. When a developer — public or private, nonprofit or for-profit — commits to building a multifamily housing project, they are making a long-duration capital commitment whose financial logic must hold across a time horizon of thirty years or more. The upfront costs of land acquisition, design, permitting, and construction must be recoverable from the downstream stream of rental income the completed project generates. If they are not — if the sum of construction costs, financing costs, operating costs, and required returns exceeds what tenants can pay — the project will not be built. In the language of the development industry, it will not pencil.

This is not greed. It is arithmetic. And it is arithmetic that operates identically whether the developer is a for-profit firm seeking a return for equity investors or a nonprofit developer seeking to deploy scarce public subsidy dollars toward the maximum number of affordable homes. In both cases, total development costs must be financed, and the financing must be serviced. The only difference is the source of the money and the mechanism by which the gap between what tenants can pay and what the project costs is bridged. In the private market, the bridge is profit — rental income sufficient to service debt and return equity. In the publicly subsidized affordable housing market, the bridge is a complex, layered stack of public subsidies, tax credits, and gap loans. In both cases, the bridge must reach across the same chasm. And in San Francisco, that chasm has become, for a growing share of projects, unbridgeable.

The RAND Corporation, in its 2025 study of multifamily housing production costs across California, Colorado, and Texas, measured the construction cost component of this chasm with unusual precision. Using completed cost data from 144 projects built between 2016 and 2024, RAND found that production costs per net rentable square foot for market-rate housing in California average $415 — 2.6 times the $157 average in Texas. For publicly subsidized affordable housing built under the federal Low Income Housing Tax Credit program, California's costs are higher still: $640 per net rentable square foot, or 4.1 times the cost of market-rate production in Texas. Within California, the San Francisco Bay Area is the most expensive region by a substantial margin: $531 per square foot for market-rate projects and $731 per square foot for LIHTC projects — figures that, adjusted to 2025 dollars, approach $650 and $900 respectively.

These are not small variations attributable to local geography or the premium of building in a dense urban environment. They are ratios of a magnitude that constitute, in the language of relative price theory, a screaming signal. The signal reads: the cost of producing housing here, under current rules, has risen so far above what the market can support that normal investment behavior cannot function. The private market has heard this signal and responded, predictably, by retreating: San Francisco currently has approximately 60,000 units that have been approved through the zoning and permitting process but remain unbuilt, sitting in a pipeline paralyzed not by regulatory obstruction but by financial infeasibility. The public market has heard the same signal and responded, also predictably, by requiring ever-larger infusions of public subsidy to close the gap — with the result that the same pool of public money produces fewer and fewer units each year.

The Elasticity That Connects Costs to Rents

The relationship between production costs and housing affordability is not merely intuitive — it is quantifiable. RAND estimated the elasticity of rental prices to production costs across their dataset of California, Colorado, and Texas projects, finding a mean elasticity of 0.44. In plain English: a 10 percent increase in production costs is associated with a 4.4 percent increase in market rents. This relationship held consistently across four different model specifications, and the models explained more than 75 percent of the observed variation in rental prices across the regions studied.

The policy implication is direct. If California could halve the production cost gap between the Los Angeles metropolitan area and Texas — a reform achievable, RAND argues, through a combination of timeline compression, impact fee reduction, and upzoning — production costs would fall by approximately 32 percent, and feasible market rents would decline by roughly 14 percent, or about $363 per month from an average of $2,577. That is not a trivial number for a working-class household in a city where median rents consume more than half of median household income for lower-wage workers.

The inverse also holds: every increment of cost increase is passed through to rents, or absorbed in the form of projects that no longer pencil. When projects stop penciling, supply falls short of demand, vacancy rates decline, and rents rise further. The cost signal and the rent signal are not independent variables — they are coupled in a feedback loop that, in San Francisco's current environment, is running in the wrong direction. Costs rise, projects stall, supply falls, rents rise, the gap between affordable rents and construction costs widens, more public subsidy is required to bridge the gap, and the same public dollars produce fewer units, which reduces supply further. Understanding this loop is the first requirement for understanding why the chapter's data looks the way it does.

Section 2: Reading the Gaps — Public Finance, Private Finance, and the Widening Distance Between Them

[Note to reader: This section contains detailed cost data from multiple sources. The numbers are important — they are the evidence for the chapter's argument — but readers who prefer narrative to tables can follow the argument from the text alone without losing the thread.]

The most important single axis for understanding San Francisco's housing cost crisis runs between two poles: publicly financed affordable housing, produced through a complex stack of federal tax credits, state subsidies, city gap loans, and nonprofit developer infrastructure; and privately financed market-rate housing, produced by for-profit developers using equity investment and commercial debt, with no public subsidy beyond whatever zoning rights the city has granted. Between these poles lies a spectrum of hybrid models — mixed-income developments with partial LIHTC equity, market-rate projects with inclusionary affordable units, and an emerging tier of for-profit developers entering the affordable space through LIHTC alone, without the layers of city and state gap financing that have historically defined the publicly subsidized model.

The gaps between these financing models — in cost per unit, in public subsidy required, in units produced per dollar of public investment, and in the construction methods and timelines they enable — are the subject of this section. Three distinct gaps require attention: the gap between what affordable rents can support and what construction costs require; the gap between what California spends to build publicly subsidized housing and what comparable housing costs in other states; and the gap between what conventional construction methods cost and what emerging factory-built alternatives can deliver.

Gap One: The Scissors Effect — When AMI Ceilings and Construction Costs Diverge

The fundamental financial logic of affordable housing rests on a regulatory concept: Area Median Income, or AMI. The federal Department of Housing and Urban Development calculates AMI annually for each metropolitan area, and affordable housing programs use AMI as the basis for setting maximum rents. A unit restricted to 30 percent AMI must be rented at a price affordable to a household earning 30 percent of the area median — in San Francisco, where AMI for a family of four exceeds $150,000, that means a 30 percent AMI household earns roughly $45,000 annually, and the rent on a three-bedroom unit cannot exceed approximately $858 per month under standard affordability definitions.

Eight hundred and fifty-eight dollars per month. In San Francisco. For a three-bedroom apartment that cost more than one million dollars to build.

This is not a rhetorical flourish. It is the actual arithmetic of the 1979 Mission Street project currently in predevelopment by Mission Housing Development Corporation and Mission Economic Development Agency, with financing evaluation completed by the Mayor's Office of Housing and Community Development in May 2024. The Family component of the project — 250 units targeted at households earning 30 to 80 percent of AMI — carries a total development cost of $261,765,993, or $1,047,063 per unit. The land was free: MOHCD acquired the 1.32-acre site at zero cost through a land dedication arrangement completed in 2022. The parking requirement is zero. The site is adjacent to a major BART transit hub. Every cost reduction that San Francisco's policy toolkit can offer has been applied. And the per-unit cost still exceeds one million dollars.

The Permanent Supportive Housing component — 150 units of studios and one-bedrooms for individuals experiencing homelessness, an even more vulnerable population requiring even more intensive services — comes in at $958,636 per unit. Rents in PSH units are typically set at 30 percent AMI or below, meaning monthly rent income of perhaps $700 to $900 per unit. The gap between that rental income and the financing cost of a $958,636 unit is not closeable by any private financing mechanism. It requires the entire project to be publicly funded, with MOHCD alone committing $30 million in gap financing — $200,000 per unit — on top of whatever LIHTC equity, state credits, and other federal sources the project will assemble.

The 1979 Mission project is not an outlier. It is representative. Of the 23 San Francisco projects that received tax credit funding over the three years preceding December 2025, eleven exceeded $1 million per unit and six exceeded $900,000 per unit. The city-compiled gap finance data examined for this chapter shows per-unit gap financing requirements ranging from $43,255 at 1515 South Van Ness to $434,556 at 730 Stanyan Street — a tenfold spread that itself signals something important: even within the universe of publicly financed affordable projects in a single city, there is dramatic variation in how efficiently public dollars are being deployed. The projects at the low end of the gap range are telling policymakers something the projects at the high end are not.

The scissors effect driving this crisis has two blades. One blade is the slow, HUD-regulated growth of AMI-based rent limits — these move with area incomes, which in San Francisco have risen substantially for high-wage workers but far more slowly for the low-wage workers who most need affordable housing. The other blade is the rapid, policy-driven escalation of construction costs — RAND documents cost growth that has substantially outpaced general inflation, particularly in the publicly subsidized sector where California-specific requirements have compounded the underlying market cost increases. As the blades of the scissors diverge, the gap between what a project can earn in rent and what it costs to build widens, requiring more public subsidy to close — and since the pool of public subsidy is finite and itself subject to political and budget pressures, more subsidy per unit means fewer units built.

Ben Metcalf, managing director of the Terner Center for Housing Innovation at UC Berkeley, identified the specific mechanism with precision in a December 2025 interview with the San Francisco Chronicle: every additional layer of public financing added to a project's capital stack imposes approximately $20,000 per unit in administrative costs — compliance requirements, reporting obligations, legal fees, monitoring costs — that are themselves part of the gap the next layer of financing must close. The gap generates the administrative burden that widens the gap. San Francisco's most complex publicly financed projects assemble six, seven, or eight separate financing sources, each with its own requirements and timelines. The administrative cost of that complexity, at $20,000 per layer, can add $100,000 to $160,000 per unit before a single brick has been laid.

Gap Two: The Interstate and Public/Private Cost Chasm

The second gap is perhaps the most analytically clarifying, because it isolates the portion of California's cost premium that is attributable to policy choices rather than geography or market conditions.

The RAND study is careful to control for building type, project size, inflation timing, and unit size when making cross-state comparisons. After these controls, the cost differences that remain are persistent, large, and decomposable into specific categories that map directly onto specific policy decisions. The headline findings deserve to be stated plainly, because they are extraordinary: California's publicly subsidized affordable housing costs $662 per net rentable square foot — more than four times the $157 per square foot cost of market-rate housing in Texas, and more than 2.5 times the $260 per square foot cost of publicly subsidized affordable housing in Colorado. Within California, the San Francisco Bay Area is the most expensive region in the entire study sample, with LIHTC project costs of $731 per square foot in 2019 dollars — figures that translate to approximately $900 per square foot in 2025 terms.

These ratios are not driven primarily by land costs, which RAND analyzes separately. They are driven by three categories of policy-influenced costs: hard construction costs inflated by prevailing wage requirements and California-specific building code mandates; soft costs inflated by municipal impact fees, architectural and engineering requirements, and the administrative burden of complex funding structures; and timeline-driven carrying costs inflated by approval processes that average 22 months longer in California than in Texas.

Consider the hard cost component first. RAND found that hard construction costs for market-rate projects average $115 per square foot in Texas and $253 per square foot in California — a ratio of 2.2 to 1 that holds across all major cost subcategories: framing, electrical, plumbing, foundations, drywall, masonry. For publicly subsidized LIHTC projects, the hard cost differential is more severe: $173 per square foot in Texas versus $427 per square foot in California, a ratio of 2.5 to 1. A substantial share of the LIHTC hard cost premium in California is attributable to prevailing wage requirements — rules mandating that workers on publicly subsidized projects be paid union-scale wages that run approximately 1.5 times the market wage in Los Angeles and San Diego, and 2.2 times the market wage in San Francisco. RAND's labor cost analysis found that prevailing wage differences can explain 32 to 56 percent of the hard cost gap between California LIHTC projects and their Texas equivalents, depending on the region.

The soft cost component tells an equally stark story. California market-rate projects pay average soft costs of $84 per square foot — 3.8 times the Texas market-rate average of $22. For LIHTC projects in California, soft costs average $187 per square foot — 8.5 times the Texas market-rate average. In the Los Angeles metro region, LIHTC soft costs reach $221 per square foot. To give that figure the context it deserves: the soft costs alone on a Los Angeles LIHTC project — the architectural fees, municipal impact fees, legal costs, compliance monitoring, financing fees, and environmental reports — exceed the total hard construction cost of a market-rate apartment in San Diego, which is itself subject to California's stringent seismic and energy efficiency requirements. Paper and process cost more than lumber and concrete.

Municipal impact fees deserve particular attention, because they represent perhaps the most transparent example of a policy choice directly converting into a cost that falls entirely on new housing production. Texas state law limits impact fees to water and wastewater infrastructure costs, caps the amount, and requires that charges be offset by the increased property tax revenue new development generates. The result: Texas market-rate projects average $816 per unit in municipal impact fees. California is a different world. San Diego market-rate projects average $36,992 per unit in impact fees — 45 times the Texas average, and representing more than 14 percent of total development cost. San Francisco market-rate projects average $30,712 per unit — 37 times the Texas average. These fees are levied on every unit of new housing, including affordable housing, without exception.

The production timeline premium compounds all of these cost categories. RAND found that bringing a market-rate project to completion in California takes an average of 49 months — nearly twice the 27-month average in Texas. California's predevelopment phase alone averages 27.9 months, compared to 13.1 months in Texas, a 15-month gap driven primarily by lengthy approval processes, CEQA litigation exposure, and sequential rather than concurrent permitting and inspection procedures. Each additional month of predevelopment and construction carries a cost: RAND estimates approximately $1,284 per unit per month in timeline-related expenses, including land carrying costs, property taxes, equipment rental, and financing costs on committed but undeployed capital. The 22-month California-Texas timeline differential translates to roughly $28,000 per unit in pure timeline-driven cost premium — before a single additional regulatory requirement has been satisfied.

The cumulative effect of these cost differences on the productive efficiency of public housing subsidy is captured in a single RAND counterfactual that should be read as a policy challenge as much as a data point: if California's publicly subsidized affordable housing were produced at Colorado's production costs — not Texas's, not some theoretical optimum, but the actual costs achieved by a comparable western state with real winters, real seismic exposure, and real urban density challenges — the approximately $1.25 billion in recent spending by California's four largest affordable housing funding programs would have produced more than four times as many units as it actually did. Four times. The same public investment. The difference is not generosity or commitment; it is efficiency.

The data assembled for this chapter from San Francisco projects makes this calculation local and vivid. The six publicly financed affordable projects in our dataset average $1,032,000 per unit. The four privately financed market-rate projects in the same city average $543,000 per unit — roughly half the public project cost. The same land market. The same seismic requirements. The same labor pool. The same city. The 2-to-1 ratio between public and private production costs in San Francisco is not a California-versus-Texas comparison that can be dismissed on grounds of regional difference; it is a within-city comparison that isolates the cost premium attached to the public financing model itself.

The 1111 Sutter Street project illustrates the alternative. A mixed-income development of 303 units — including 101 affordable apartments — at $700,000 per unit total, with approximately $54 million in state tax credits and loans covering 25 percent of the $220 million total cost. The public subsidy per affordable unit: $54 million divided by 101 affordable units equals approximately $534,000 — substantially less than the $1,032,000 average total cost of a fully public affordable unit in our dataset, and less than half the cost of the 1979 Mission project's family units. The remaining 202 market-rate units are financed entirely with private capital, contribute to the city's overall housing supply, and generate property tax revenue that partially offsets the public subsidy invested. The private capital does not displace the public mission; it amplifies it.

This is the arithmetic at the heart of what Ezra Klein and Derek Thompson, in their book Abundance, describe as the moderate left's emerging position on housing: not that the market should replace public investment in housing, but that targeted public incentives can leverage private capital to produce more housing, for more income levels, at lower cost per public dollar spent, than the fully public model currently achieves. The data supports that argument. Whether the political will exists to act on it is a question for the subsequent chapter.

Gap Three: The Factory-Built Horizon — What Substitution Could Deliver

The third gap is temporal — it exists between what current construction methods cost and what emerging factory-built alternatives can deliver — and it is the gap that most clearly illustrates the operation of the substitution effect in economic theory. When the relative price of a production input rises sufficiently above its alternatives, rational producers substitute toward the cheaper alternative. This is not a theoretical prediction; it is an observed regularity in virtually every industry that has undergone technological disruption. The question for California's housing market is not whether factory-built housing represents a viable lower-cost alternative to conventional stick-built construction — it demonstrably does — but whether the policy environment will allow the substitution to occur at the scale the cost signal demands.

The evidence that factory-built housing can dramatically reduce per-unit costs in the Bay Area is no longer theoretical. Danny Haber's company, oWOW, has already built a 236-unit market-rate tower at 1510 Webster Street in Oakland using mass timber and prefabricated standardized components, assembled on site in 65 days, at a total cost of approximately $425,000 per unit. Full floors of the building were installed in as little as one day. The company is breaking ground on 284 affordable apartments in downtown Oakland at 1523 Harrison Street targeting a per-unit cost, with LIHTC tax credits applied, of approximately $350,000. It has also won tax credit allocations for 185 affordable units at 960 Howard Street in San Francisco.

Three hundred and fifty thousand dollars per affordable unit, with no gap financing. In the Bay Area. In 2025.

To appreciate the significance of this figure, set it against the context established earlier in this chapter. The 1979 Mission project — also in San Francisco, also publicly supported, also targeting low-income households — carries a TDC of $1,047,063 per unit for the family component, and $958,636 per unit for the PSH component, with free land and zero parking requirements. The oWOW figure is one-third of the 1979 Mission figure, achieved without a land dedication, without city gap financing, and without the multi-year predevelopment process that MOHCD's evaluation documents suggest will extend well into the late 2020s before 1979 Mission reaches construction.

The mechanism behind oWOW's cost structure involves four interlocking strategies that, together, address nearly every cost driver that RAND and the Terner Center identify as inflating conventional project costs. First, prefabricated standardized components and mass timber dramatically compress the on-site construction phase, eliminating the carrying costs that accumulate during California's average 21-month construction timeline. Second, vertical integration — combining architect, engineer, contractor, and developer functions within a single firm — eliminates the fragmented profit stacking that oWOW president Andy Ball describes with precision: "Typically, in construction, you have a separate architect, engineer, contractor and developer. If each one of those is representing their separate profits, they are going to protect their interests." The RAND data on San Francisco Bay Area architectural and engineering fees for LIHTC projects — averaging $24,943 per unit, more than five times the Texas figure — reflects exactly the cost of that fragmentation. oWOW's internalization of these functions removes the category. Third, the no-gap-financing thesis — if construction costs are low enough that LIHTC equity alone covers the capital stack, the entire administrative premium that Ben Metcalf quantifies at $20,000 per financing layer simply does not arise. Fourth, the for-profit efficiency culture applied to affordable product: a developer who cannot rely on gap financing backstops has every incentive to minimize cost; a developer whose projects are routinely completed with six or eight public financing sources has, historically, had those incentives partially cushioned.

The nonprofit sector is pursuing its own version of this substitution. Mercy Housing California's 1633 Valencia Street project — 145 units of supportive senior housing in San Francisco's Mission District — used a construction method called design-build, which unifies design and construction teams from the earliest stages of project development to reduce coordination costs, shorten timelines, and eliminate the adversarial dynamics that conventional sequential design-bid-build processes often produce. The target per-unit cost, with tax credits: $530,000. That is roughly half the San Francisco average for publicly financed affordable projects, achieved by a nonprofit developer operating within the conventional LIHTC framework, through process reform alone — without factory production, without vertical integration, and without abandoning the community-serving mission that distinguishes nonprofit affordable developers from their market-rate counterparts. Tiffany Bohee, Mercy Housing California's president, described the urgency driving this experimentation simply: "There's definitely a sense of urgency when it comes to bringing costs down. It's forcing us all in this space to be very creative."

The barriers preventing factory-built housing from scaling to the level the cost signal demands are well-documented by the Terner Center's March 2026 white paper on the subject. The barriers are not technical — the technology works, as 1510 Webster Street demonstrates. They are institutional. The Low Income Housing Tax Credit program, the primary financing vehicle for affordable housing production in the United States, was designed around the sequencing of conventional stick-built construction, where significant costs are incurred on site as physical construction progresses, and financing is disbursed as on-site milestones are achieved. Factory-built housing inverts this sequence: significant costs — factory deposits, off-site production expenses, module fabrication — are incurred before any on-site work begins. LIHTC's disbursement structure, calibrated to the stick-built sequence, forces FBH developers to bridge factory costs with expensive interim financing that conventional stick-built developers do not require.

More specifically, LIHTC awards require developers to begin on-site construction within six months of credit award — a "shovel-ready" requirement designed to prevent developers from sitting on tax credit allocations without building. For modular construction, where the building is substantially fabricated off site before the on-site assembly phase begins, this rule is perversely counterproductive: it forces a project into nominal "construction" commencement before the factory production that will constitute the bulk of physical construction activity has been completed. The requirement does not prevent factory-built housing from being built, but it forces developers into a compliance dance that adds timeline complexity, legal costs, and lender uncertainty — the frictions that, in a capital-intensive industry with thin margins, are often sufficient to tip a borderline project from viable to infeasible.

The California Tax Credit Allocation Committee and California Debt Limit Allocation Committee, the state agencies that administer LIHTC and tax-exempt bond financing respectively, operate on timelines and readiness standards calibrated to conventional construction. As one industry expert quoted in the Terner white paper put it: "There's this whole development timeline that we have to fall into that is from a stick-built construction world. There is very little flexibility for the types of situations that we run into with modular projects around factory availability, around deposits, around all the uncertainty that's wrapped up in our world." The competitive procurement requirements that govern how public money is spent create an additional friction: factory selection and general contractor selection must be conducted as separate competitive processes, even when established factory-GC partnerships already exist with proven working relationships and lower transaction costs. In a nascent industry where the pool of qualified factory-GC pairings is still small, mandatory re-bidding of known relationships introduces delay, uncertainty, and cost without producing the competitive pricing benefits the rules were designed to achieve.

These are not intractable problems. They are administrative mismatches between rules designed for one production technology and a production technology that the cost signal is urgently demanding. The Terner Center's recommendations — modify TCAC and CDLAC timelines to accommodate FBH cash flow sequencing, reform the shovel-ready requirement to recognize off-site factory production, update LIHTC scoring criteria to reward cost efficiency and timeline predictability — are targeted, specific, and implementable without legislation. They are, in the language of Klein and Thompson's Abundance, exactly the kind of second-order policy reforms that unlock large gains from first-order reforms already achieved: California has done significant work on zoning and entitlement; the next frontier is the financing and construction architecture that governs what gets built once the zoning barriers have been cleared.

Section 3: Where Markets Can Go — The Emerging Architecture of Affordable Abundance

The argument assembled in the preceding two sections might seem to lead toward a pessimistic conclusion: costs are too high, gaps are too wide, the policy environment suppresses the very substitutions that could reduce costs, and the same public dollars produce fewer and fewer units each year. But the data assembled for this chapter does not support pessimism. It supports urgency — which is a different thing. The difference matters because urgency implies that action is possible and consequential, while pessimism implies that the situation is beyond the reach of policy. The evidence suggests the former.

Consider what the San Francisco housing market looks like as of late 2025, through the lens of the market signals this chapter has been tracking. The California Tax Credit Allocation Committee approved 25,781 affordable housing units in 2025 — approximately 10,000 more than the previous year, a 63 percent increase attributable directly to a bipartisan expansion of the federal LIHTC program that increased the volume of credits available and simplified the debt structure required to access them. Roughly a quarter of those approvals are in the Bay Area. The supply response to a relatively modest incremental policy improvement is measurable within a single year.

Market-rate developers — firms whose existence depends on projects that pencil without public subsidy backstops — are entering the affordable housing space, drawn by the expanded LIHTC opportunity and deterred by the near-paralysis of the market-rate pipeline. DM Development has 450 affordable units under construction at 300 De Haro Street in San Francisco at an estimated $480,000 per unit. Align Real Estate Partners and Martin Building Company together have nearly 1,200 additional affordable units in the works in San Francisco. These are not mission-driven nonprofit developers with decades of community relationships and cross-subsidized service programs; they are businesses that have identified an opportunity to deploy their construction efficiency in a market where the financing conditions have temporarily aligned. Jeremy Harris of oWOW stated the calculus directly: "In the affordable world, there is an opportunity — we can build three times as much housing for the same dollars we are getting from the government."

Three times as much housing. For the same dollars. This is not an ideological claim. It is a statement about productive efficiency that the cost data in this chapter supports: at $350,000 per unit versus $1,047,000 per unit, the same capital investment does indeed produce approximately three times as many homes. The question of whether those homes serve the same populations and fulfill the same long-term community functions as fully publicly financed nonprofit projects is real and requires honest engagement.

Malcolm Yeung of the Chinatown Community Development Center raises the legitimate concern about exit strategies: for-profit developers are entering the affordable housing space during a period when market-rate development has stalled and LIHTC credits are abundant. When market conditions shift — when interest rates fall, when market-rate equity becomes available again, when the LIHTC expansion moderates — will they stay? LIHTC compliance periods require affordability restrictions for a minimum of thirty years under current law, providing structural protection against early conversion to market-rate use. But the deeper question Yeung is asking is about institutional permanence: nonprofit affordable developers are community anchors that reinvest developer fees in afterschool programs, literacy classes, tenant services, and neighborhood organizing. A for-profit developer distributes those fees to equity investors. The buildings may be identical; their relationship to the communities they house is not.

Sharon Lai of the East Bay Asian Local Development Corporation frames the long-term sustainability question with equal clarity: "We are in the business of production and operation, so looking at the cash flow beyond 10 years is really important. Nonprofits really need sustainable funds on an ongoing basis." This is a real constraint that the pure cost-efficiency argument does not fully address. Affordable housing is not simply a construction problem; it is an ongoing operational commitment to populations whose needs — for stability, for services, for community connection — extend well beyond the initial lease-up of a new building. Nick Friend of the Silicon Valley Housing Trust identifies the precise population where this tension is sharpest: the formerly homeless, the severely disabled, the extremely low income — "those are the projects that are most expensive and rely most on subsidies, and those are the projects that will be even harder to do going forward."

These concerns are legitimate. They deserve weight in the policy conversation. But they do not constitute an argument against the cost-reduction agenda that this chapter's data demands. They constitute an argument for clarity about which tools are appropriate at which points on the income spectrum — and that clarity, it turns out, is exactly what the market signals are providing.

The income ladder of housing need in San Francisco runs from zero — the street, the shelter, the tiny homes village that currently occupies the 1979 Mission site — through 30 percent AMI PSH, to 50 percent AMI family housing, to 60 to 80 percent AMI workforce housing, to market rate. At the very bottom of this ladder, where rents are set at 30 percent AMI or below for people exiting homelessness and carrying the full weight of mental illness, addiction, and trauma, no private financing mechanism can close the gap between what tenants can pay and what it costs to house them. Deep public subsidy is not an option here; it is the only option. The debate about efficiency is entirely valid — the RAND counterfactual showing four times as many units for the same public dollars at Colorado production costs is a devastating indictment of California's cost structure even for this most-subsidized tier — but the mechanism of delivery must remain public.

As one moves up the income ladder, however, the arithmetic changes. At 60 percent AMI — the target income level for oWOW's Oakland LIHTC project — rents are high enough, and construction costs low enough with modern methods, that LIHTC equity alone can close the financing gap without city gap loans, state supplemental credits, or the six-layer funding stacks that generate $20,000 per unit in administrative overhead. Mark MacDonald of DM Development, describing his company's 450-unit project at 300 De Haro Street, frames the crossover point with the directness of someone running a business: "The development business is driven by the income we receive by constructing housing, so it's paramount that we keep our projects moving. We need housing of all stripes."

Housing of all stripes. That phrase, from a market-rate developer who has crossed into affordable construction, captures the argument that this chapter has been building through its data. The housing crisis in San Francisco — and in California more broadly — is not one crisis. It is a stack of crises at different income levels, each requiring a somewhat different response, each sending somewhat different market signals, each amenable to somewhat different policy tools. The mistake of the last fifteen years has been to treat this stack as a single problem requiring a single solution — a solution that, in San Francisco's political culture, has defaulted to the most expensive, most publicly dependent, most process-laden model available, applied uniformly across the income spectrum regardless of whether the economics of a given project tier require it.

The data says something different. It says that at 60 to 80 percent AMI, private capital with targeted LIHTC incentives can produce housing at $350,000 to $480,000 per unit without gap financing, freeing public subsidy dollars for the 30 percent AMI households who genuinely cannot be served any other way. It says that even within the nonprofit sector, process reforms like design-build can reduce costs by 40 to 50 percent, producing more units from the same subsidy pool. It says that factory-built construction can compress timelines from 21 months to 65 days on site, eliminating an enormous component of carrying costs and financing drag. It says that the 60,000 units sitting approved and unbuilt in San Francisco's pipeline are not waiting for more public money — they are waiting for conditions under which the math works, and that creating those conditions is a policy choice, not a budgetary one.

None of this requires abandoning the commitment to serving the deepest-need populations. It requires directing the tools most appropriate to each tier of need toward that tier, rather than applying the most expensive and most restrictive model to every project regardless of what the market signals indicate. The laws of economics are not hostile to affordable housing. They are indifferent — they will produce outcomes consistent with the incentives and constraints that policy establishes. For the last decade and a half in San Francisco, the incentives and constraints have produced a system in which publicly financed housing costs three to four times what comparable housing costs in other states, where a fixed pool of public subsidy buys one-quarter as many homes as it could, and where sixty thousand approved units sit unbuilt because the economics have been systematically destroyed by the very policies intended to protect affordability.

The market signals have been sending a clear message throughout this period. The message is that costs have risen to the point where the system is approaching the limits of what public subsidy can sustain, that private capital is available for a substantial portion of the income spectrum if the incentive structure invites it, and that construction technologies exist that could dramatically reduce costs if the financing architecture were updated to accommodate them. Reading those signals, and allowing the policy response those signals demand, is not a concession to the market. It is a recognition that the market — its prices, its incentives, its substitution effects, and its productive efficiency — is a tool that housing policy can work with or against, but cannot simply ignore.
