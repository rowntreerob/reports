Texas Senate Race, MAGA Cowardice, and the 2026 Democratic Outlook

Executive Summary

Mona Charen and Will Salatin of The Bulwark discuss the stunning Texas Republican Senate primary result in which Ken Paxton — a multiply-accused, impeached former attorney general — defeated incumbent John Cornyn, despite Cornyn spending tens of millions exposing Paxton’s corruption. They assess Democratic nominee James Talarico’s strengths and vulnerabilities, analyze the “YOLO Republicans” (retiring or defeated anti-MAGA figures like Cornyn, Bill Cassidy, and Tom Tillis who are only now speaking out), and consider whether 2026 could be a good cycle for Democrats — with cautious optimism tempered by structural polarization.

TL;DR

Paxton’s Texas primary win over Cornyn reveals hardcore MAGA voters’ dominance, while Democrat Talarico offers real but uncertain hope against an obviously corrupt opponent.

Key Points

  • Ken Paxton won the Texas Republican Senate primary over John Cornyn despite being impeached by his own party, reported to the FBI by his own staff, and subjected to tens of millions in negative ads — Paxton received nearly identical vote totals in both the primary and the runoff, suggesting his hardcore MAGA base held firm while Cornyn’s broader voters stayed home
  • The last Democrat elected to the Texas Senate was Lloyd Bentsen; Talarico (age ~37) notes no Democrat has won since before he was born
  • Talarico is praised for economic populism (“top vs. bottom” framing), religious authenticity, and a willingness to call his past trans-related comments “cringey” — contrasted favorably with Kamala Harris’s evasiveness on the same issues
  • Talarico’s vulnerabilities include past quotes about gender (he claimed there are six biological sexes), a somewhat “elfin” physical presentation that Republicans are weaponizing as a “beta male” narrative, and a general Democratic weakness with Texas swing voters
  • The false “vegan” smear against Talarico is seen as actually useful to him — his rebuttal line: “I’ve been eating barbecue since before Ken Paxton’s first indictment”
  • Hispanic voters are identified as a key swing factor; Texas Republicans built recent dominance partly on growing Hispanic support, which national polls suggest is now cratering under Trump
  • The “YOLO Republicans” (Cornyn, Cassidy, Tillis, McConnell) are criticized for only speaking out once their political careers are over — and even then not going far enough, e.g., Tillis attributing Trump’s actions to unnamed advisers rather than Trump himself
  • Bill Cassidy’s moment of laughter when asked “Has Donald Trump been honest with you?” is cited as the most honest thing he’s communicated — he then refused to answer
  • Trump’s “alpha male” brand is described as visibly fading, undermined by his physical decline, the inconclusive Iran military campaign, and a rambling cabinet meeting focused on the reflecting pool
  • Both hosts urge caution about overreading a potential 2026 Democratic wave, noting high polarization and fewer swing seats than in past wave elections

Context & Background

This is an episode of The Mona Charen Show, a center-right/Never Trump podcast associated with The Bulwark. Charen is a veteran conservative commentator; Will Salatin is a Bulwark writer from Texas. The conversation assumes familiarity with Texas political history, the 2024 presidential race dynamics, Trump’s ongoing policy actions (Iran strikes, January 6 pardons, redistricting), and figures like RFK Jr., Pete Hegseth, and Russell Moore. The intended audience is politically engaged, anti-MAGA, center-right or center-left listeners interested in 2026 electoral strategy.

Takeaways & Implications

Paxton’s primary victory is a stark demonstration that informed voters — even in a heavily covered, well-funded race — can choose flagrant corruption when motivated by MAGA identity, and that low-turnout runoffs amplify that dynamic. Talarico represents a potentially compelling Democratic archetype (religious, economically populist, border-security-accepting) but faces real structural and stylistic headwinds in Texas. The “YOLO Republicans” phenomenon — principled defection only after political survival is off the table — underscores how few Republicans were ever willing to pay a real price for opposing Trump, weakening the moral weight of their belated criticism. Democrats should resist overconfidence even if 2026 goes well, keeping focus on 2028 as the decisive election.
