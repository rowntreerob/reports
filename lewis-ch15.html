A Debate: LCOE, Marginal-Cost Markets, and the Road to 2050

Framing note: Below is a constructed debate. “PROF. LEWIS” channels the views in your textbook excerpt (E. E. Lewis — nuclear-sympathetic, skeptical of FERC-style marginal-cost markets, skeptical that plant-level LCOE is a fair comparator). “AMORY” is a composite “Lovins-type” advocate — a faithful, strong* version of the renewables/efficiency/Green New Deal position, not a weak strawman. Both draw on your two source documents (the book and Lazard LCOE+ v18.0, June 2025). I interject occasionally as moderator/referee where the numbers actually adjudicate the point.*

Round 1 — Is LCOE the right yardstick at all?

PROF. LEWIS: My core complaint is that LCOE was invented for a world of dispatchable fossil plants at vertically integrated utilities. It is a per-MWh average cost over a plant’s life. It works fine when you can compare two plants that both produce on demand. It breaks the moment you put a weather-dependent generator on the same axis as a controllable one, because a MWh delivered at 2 p.m. on a sunny day is not the same product as a MWh delivered on a windless January evening. Lazard’s own Table 16.2-equivalent — their headline bar chart — puts solar at $38–78 and onshore wind at $37–86, below new gas. But that chart silently assumes the MWh are fungible. They are not.

AMORY: And Lazard says so — on the very first page of the LCOE section they disclose that the base analysis “does not consider the intermittent nature of selected renewable technologies.” Nobody is hiding the ball. But here’s where you overreach, Professor: you take that legitimate caveat and inflate it into a claim that renewables are secretly the most expensive option. Lazard did the honest thing this year — they built a Cost of Firming Intermittency section to answer exactly your objection. Let’s use their numbers, not a 2023 Bank of America slide.

PROF. LEWIS: I’m happy to. Let’s.

Round 2 — The system-cost fight: $331 wind vs. $122 firming

PROF. LEWIS: My Table 16.3 — the BofA Global Research figures — separates direct (plant-level) from system (grid-level) cost:

Plant Direct System Total
Nuclear 82 122 204
Wind 40 291 331
Solar 36 413 449

When you add the integration costs that LCOE omits — backup, transmission, lost stability — nuclear is the cheapest carbon-free option, not the most expensive. The plant-level ranking inverts.

AMORY: That table is the weakest brick in your wall, and I think you know it. Look at what it claims: $291/MWh of “system” cost for wind and $413 for solar. Those are not measured costs; they’re modeled costs at very high, near-100% renewable penetration with essentially no other tools — the worst case dressed up as the typical case.

Now look at Lazard’s empirical, ISO-by-ISO firming analysis — built on the actual ELCC and Net CONE values that MISO, CAISO, SPP, PJM, and ERCOT publish:

  • MISO solar: $66 LCOE + $50 firming = $116
  • MISO wind: $53 + $50 = $103
  • ERCOT solar: $55 + $42 = $97
  • PJM solar: $74 + $48 = $122
  • PJM solar-plus-storage: $114 + $51 = $164

The firming adder is $42–51/MWh, not $291. That’s an order of magnitude below your BofA figure. And the all-in, firmed renewable number — roughly $100–165 — still lands at or below your new nuclear at $141–220. Your own thesis — “compare on a system basis” — when run with real grid-operator data, still favors renewables-plus-firming over new reactors.

MODERATOR (referee): This is the crux, and Amory has the better of it on sourcing. The BofA $291–413 system costs are model outputs at deep decarbonization; Lazard’s $42–51 is grounded in published ELCC/Net CONE. But two honest caveats cut Lewis’s way: (1) Lazard’s firming cost is computed at today’s moderate penetration and rises as renewables grow — Lazard explicitly says “the value of firm capacity rises” with penetration, and CAISO solar ELCC has already collapsed to 7%; (2) Lazard’s firming “resource” is a gas peaker (or a 4-hr battery), which is a fossil or short-duration backstop — it firms reliability, not deep decarbonization. So the gap between the two estimates is largely a gap between “the grid today” and “the grid at 80–100% VRE.”

Round 3 — The collapsing ELCC and the penetration trap

PROF. LEWIS: Thank you — that’s precisely my point and it deserves emphasis. Look at CAISO: solar’s effective load-carrying capability is 7%. Seven. You build 1,000 MW of solar nameplate and the grid can count on 70 MW at the peak. SPP winter solar is 62%, summer 25% — wildly seasonal. As you saturate the daytime, each new panel is worth less, because it produces when the others already produce. This is the duck curve I describe in Chapter 5 — Figure 5.5 — the residual-demand belly that deepens until midday electricity is nearly worthless and the evening ramp becomes brutal. Nuclear’s “ELCC” is essentially its 90%+ capacity factor, flat across all seasons and all penetrations. It doesn’t cannibalize itself.

AMORY: Correct on the physics, wrong on the conclusion. Three responses.

First — the firming cost already prices that in. Lazard’s formula divides the firm-capacity shortfall (1 − ELCC) × Net CONE over delivered MWh. The 7% CAISO solar number is why CAISO uses a battery, not a gas peaker, as the marginal firming resource. And even with a $18.92/kW-month battery Net CONE — the highest in the country — solar-plus-storage firms out competitively.

Second — you’re describing a solved problem, not an unsolvable one. The answer to the duck curve is staring at us in the other half of the Lazard report: storage costs are falling fast. Utility-scale 4-hour LCOS dropped to $115–254/MWh, down with cell oversupply and rising energy density. PV-plus-storage gets you a 41% effective ELCC in CAISO versus 7% standalone. You shift the belly into the neck.

Third — and this is the part your book underplays — diversity and geography. Your own Figure 5.2 shows Texas and New England wind are anti-correlated much of the time. A continental HVDC-linked grid turns “the wind isn’t blowing here” into “it’s blowing there.” You treat each VRE plant as an island; the modern thesis treats the fleet as a portfolio.

PROF. LEWIS: Storage that discharges in four hours does not solve a 12-hour winter night, let alone a multi-day overcast “Dunkelflaute.” I say so plainly: a 4-hour lithium bank used against a 12-hour night runs at a third of its rating, and if you cycle it that hard you replace it every two years against a 30-year panel life. And your continental transmission — thousands of miles of HVDC — is exactly the grid-level cost LCOE omits and that nobody is paying for. Your “portfolio” requires building the most expensive transmission network in history first.

Round 4 — The “missing money” problem and FERC markets

PROF. LEWIS: Now to the market design, which I regard as actively dangerous. Under FERC’s deregulated energy markets, generators are paid the marginal clearing price — the fuel cost of the last unit dispatched. That’s fine for a coal or gas plant whose cost is mostly fuel. It is catastrophic for any high-capital, low-marginal-cost asset — which describes both nuclear and wind and solar. When VRE floods the market, the clearing price crashes toward zero, sometimes negative. So the very assets we need for decarbonization — capital-intensive, near-zero marginal cost — cannot recover their capital in the market that’s supposed to finance them. That’s the “missing money problem,” and capacity markets with 1–3 year horizons don’t fix a 60-year asset.

AMORY: Here is the delicious irony, Professor: we agree. Marginal-cost pricing under-rewards capital-intensive zero-marginal-cost generation. But notice what that argument actually proves. It proves the market design is broken — not that renewables are expensive. You want to use the missing-money problem to rescue nuclear. I want to use it to expand contracts-for-difference, PPAs, and clean-energy standards that let wind, solar, storage, geothermal recover capital too. The policy fix — long-term revenue certainty for low-marginal-cost clean resources — is technology-neutral, and it disproportionately helps the cheapest clean resources, which Lazard shows are solar and wind.

And note the empirical kicker you put in your own book: the data-center PPAs restarting Palisades and Three Mile Island are great — but those same hyperscalers are signing vastly more gigawatt-hours of solar and wind PPAs, because they clear faster and cheaper. The market is voting.

PROF. LEWIS: They’re signing nuclear PPAs because they need firm, 24/7, carbon-free power for a constant load, and they’ve discovered renewables can’t provide it without a firm partner. That’s a concession, not a vote.

MODERATOR: Both are right about the diagnosis — marginal-cost markets create missing money for all capital-heavy clean resources. Where they differ is the remedy’s beneficiary. A neutral CfD/PPA regime helps the lowest-LCOE clean resource most (favoring Amory). But firm-capacity accreditation reforms (PJM/CAISO cutting correlated-resource ELCC) shift value toward whatever is genuinely firm (favoring Lewis). Lazard documents both trends happening simultaneously.

Round 5 — Cost of capital and the Vogtle ghost

AMORY: Let me press my advantage on financing, because this is where the nuclear case quietly falls apart. Look at Lazard’s cost-of-capital sensitivity. Nuclear swings from $115/MWh at a 4.2% WACC to $242/MWh at 10%. It is the single most capital-sensitive technology on the board, because ~85% of its lifetime cost is upfront and it takes 84 months to build. Solar utility barely moves — $48 to $71 across the same range — because it’s quick and modular.

Your own book hands me the rebuttal to your strongest counter-argument. You say: “stretch nuclear’s cost recovery to 60 years and the levelized fixed cost halves, making it competitive at ~$106/MWh.” Fine — but that only works at a low, government-guaranteed cost of capital over half a century. The instant you expose a 60-to-84-month build to private equity at a 12% cost of equity, the compounding eats you alive. Vogtle came in at ~$32 billion for 2.2 GW. That’s the real number, and it’s why no merchant utility will build one.

PROF. LEWIS: You’ve just made my argument for me — and it’s the thesis of my final chapter. Nuclear is uneconomic under the financing regime we chose, not under the physics. The world’s reactors get built by state-backed entities — EDF, KEPCO, Rosatom, China’s CGN — and America’s own large low-marginal-cost, long-lived assets, the great dams, were built by the Bureau of Reclamation and TVA on 50-year Treasury terms. Hoover Dam was a 50-year federal mortgage. If you finance a 70-to-80-year nuclear asset at sovereign rates — the way we finance highways, airports, and water systems — its LCOE is excellent. The problem isn’t the reactor; it’s that we’ve forced century-scale infrastructure to live in a 3-year capacity market.

AMORY: Then be consistent! If you’ll grant nuclear cheap public capital and an 80-year horizon, grant the same terms to renewables and storage and transmission — and renewables, starting from a 4x lower capital cost and a 2-year build, win running away. Cheap public capital is a rising tide that lifts the least capital-intensive boat highest. You can’t make “give clean capital-heavy assets sovereign financing” a nuclear-only argument.

MODERATOR: Strong exchange. Lazard’s sensitivity table is decisively on Amory’s side for the cost-of-capital point: nuclear is uniquely WACC-exposed. Lewis’s valid rejoinder is that this is an argument about institutions and financing, not about thermodynamics or land use — and that 80-year asset life (IAEA) genuinely changes the levelized math if, and only if, the discount rate is low.

Round 6 — Subsidies, RPS, and “give it away and still profit”

PROF. LEWIS: I want to flag something I find genuinely distorting. With the federal Production Tax Credit plus state Renewable Portfolio certificates, a wind operator can bid negative and still earn money. That suppresses the clearing price for everyone — including the carbon-free nuclear plant down the road that gets no such treatment until Illinois bolted on a Zero Emission Standard to stop its reactors from closing. We are subsidizing one form of clean energy into destroying the economics of another form of clean energy. That’s not climate policy; that’s industrial favoritism.

AMORY: Two things. First, Lazard quantifies the subsidy you’re worried about, and it’s not magic — the PTC takes onshore wind from $37–86 down to roughly $15–75, and the ITC takes utility solar from $38–78 to $24–57. Meaningful, but the unsubsidized numbers are already competitive. Strip every subsidy tomorrow and solar/wind remain the cheapest new energy in Lazard’s stack. Your own book concedes the parallel point — that nuclear withered after the ’90s deregulation without equivalent support. So the honest position is: clean energy of all kinds needs capital support in a marginal-cost market. Welcome to my coalition.

Second — on negative prices “destroying” nuclear: negative prices are a signal that we lack storage and flexible demand, not a moral outrage. Electrolyzers, EV charging, data centers, and batteries all want that cheap midday power. The negative-price problem is a transition artifact, and it’s already shrinking as storage soaks up the belly of the duck.

PROF. LEWIS: A “transition artifact” that closes existing zero-carbon reactors is a permanent loss of clean generation we then have to rebuild. Closing Indian Point and backfilling with gas raised emissions. That’s the real-world scorecard.

Round 7 — Land, materials, and the things LCOE and firming both omit

PROF. LEWIS: Both LCOE and Lazard’s firming analysis omit my Figure 16.2’s outer ring — the social and environmental costs. Energy sprawl, transmission corridors, the doubled CO₂ from gas plants forced to ramp in start-stop mode to chase the duck curve (I cite the Irish CCGT data — nearly twice the CO₂/MWh when balancing wind), methane leakage at 30–80× CO₂ potency that can erase the entire benefit of switching coal to gas. A renewables grid that runs on gas firming is not as clean as its LCOE implies.

AMORY: Agreed on gas firming — which is exactly why the endgame is storage and clean-firm resources, not gas peakers. And note Lazard’s firming section is already migrating from “gas peaker” to “4-hour battery” as the marginal firm resource in CAISO. On land and materials: nuclear has its own outer ring — uranium mining, the still-unsolved repository, decommissioning (Lazard bakes decommissioning into the nuclear marginal cost), and the security/proliferation externalities your own book lists in the keywords. Every source has an outer ring. The question is magnitude and reversibility, and a solar farm is decommissioned in months; a reactor site is stewarded for generations.

MODERATOR: This round is closest to a genuine values disagreement rather than a data dispute. Lewis is right that gas-firmed renewables carry hidden emissions and that ramping doubles CO₂ intensity. Amory is right that this is an argument for storage/clean-firm, and that nuclear’s externalities are simply different, not absent. Carbon pricing would adjudicate much of this: Lazard shows $40–60/ton adds ~$25–40/MWh to gas and ~$37/MWh to coal, leaving renewables and nuclear untouched. Both debaters should want a carbon price — and a clean-firm-capacity payment.

Round 8 — What actually gets built by 2050?

PROF. LEWIS: My honest forecast: a diverse fleet, but you cannot deep-decarbonize a high-latitude, winter-peaking industrial economy on weather-dependent generation plus 4-hour batteries. The last 10–20% — the windless overcast week in January — needs firm clean power. That’s nuclear, large hydro (geographically capped in the US), or fossil-plus-CCS. The cheapest complete system includes a substantial nuclear baseload, and getting there requires the US government to finance it the way it financed the dams.

AMORY: My honest forecast: renewables do the heavy lifting because they’re already the cheapest electrons and the fastest to build — Lazard’s “lowest-cost and quickest-to-deploy.” We firm them with a portfolio: 4-to-8-hour batteries for the daily cycle, geothermal ($66–109, firm, and IRA-supported), long-duration storage, demand flexibility, continental transmission, and — yes — some clean firm capacity, where SMRs or existing reactors pencil out. I’m not anti-nuclear; I’m anti-expensive. If an SMR ever hits Lazard’s pages below $100/MWh, I’ll buy it. Until then, every dollar spent on a $141–220/MWh new reactor is a dollar not spent on three times as many clean MWh elsewhere.

PROF. LEWIS: And every renewable MWh you can’t deliver in January is the one that keeps a gas plant on the system for another forty years.

Bottom line: The strongest synthesis is one neither man fully concedes: Lewis is right that LCOE understates VRE system costs and that marginal-cost FERC markets can’t finance any capital-heavy clean asset; Amory is right that the real-world integration premium is far smaller than nuclear advocates claim and that renewables win the bulk-energy contest decisively. The honest fight is not “renewables vs. nuclear” but “what institution finances zero-marginal-cost, long-lived capital, and how do we pay for genuine firm capacity” — which is exactly the missing-money / capacity-accreditation frontier Lazard’s 2025 report spends its last section on.
