# The Great Housing Debate: A Comprehensive Analysis and Verdict\n\n## PART ONE: FRAMING THE DEBATE FAIRLY\n\nBefore rendering a verdict, intellectual honesty requires acknowledging what each side gets right. This is not a debate between villains and heroes. It is a debate between people who share the goal of housing affordability but disagree fundamentally about mechanism, timeline, and the role of private capital.\n\nThe organizing reality of this entire debate is **unit economics**. Cost per unit is destiny. It determines what can be financed, what rents must be charged, how many households can be served per public dollar spent, and ultimately whether any housing strategy - progressive or moderate - is viable at the scale the crisis demands.\n\nEvery other policy question - zoning, environmental review, labor requirements, social housing vs. market housing - is ultimately subordinate to this central question: **At what cost per unit can housing be produced, and who can afford to live in it?**\n\n---\n\n## PART TWO: THE UNIT ECONOMICS REALITY THAT FRAMES EVERYTHING\n\n### The Cost Per Unit Crisis is the Foundation of the Debate\n\nBefore evaluating either political position, the unit economics must be understood clearly because they have changed dramatically and continue to change:\n\n**Current SF Cost Benchmarks:**\n| Production Method | Cost Per Unit | Timeline | Financing Mechanism |\n|------------------|---------------|----------|-------------------|\n| SF nonprofit public housing | $900K-$1M+ | 6+ years | Deep public subsidy |\n| Traditional affordable (LIHTC) | $600-800K | 4-6 years | LIHTC + multiple gap sources |\n| Private affordable (hybrid stack) | $480-530K | 3-4 years | LIHTC + bonds + reduced gap |\n| oWOW prefab + LIHTC | $350K | ~2 years | LIHTC + bonds, no gap financing |\n| Portland missing middle | $350-400K | 1-2 years | Private capital, by-right |\n| Market rate Texas | $200-250K | 2 years | Pure private capital |\n\nThis table is the entire housing debate in compressed form. The spread between $1M+/unit and $350K/unit, both in San Francisco, both in 2025-2026, both serving low-income households, is not explained by land costs or labor costs alone. It is explained by **regulatory burden, timeline inflation, and production method.**\n\n### The Gap Financing Crisis: Why Public Projects Are Becoming Unsustainable\n\nThe gap financing problem deserves special emphasis because it is the immediate mechanism by which the unit economics crisis manifests in project failures.\n\nThe structure is as follows:\n- LIHTC + bond equity typically covers 60-70% of total development cost\n- Allowable rents are capped at approximately 30% of AMI-based income\n- AMI grows at roughly 3% annually in normal economic conditions\n- SF construction costs are growing at approximately 8-9% annually\n- The gap between what LIHTC equity covers and total project cost **widens every year**\n- That gap must be filled by scarce local and state soft money\n\nThe consequences are severe and compounding:\n\n**Every year this gap grows:**\n- More projects fail to close financing\n- More approved units remain in the pipeline unbuilt\n- More public soft money is required per unit\n- Fewer units are produced per public dollar spent\n- The 54,394 stalled approved units represent the accumulated result of this widening gap\n\nThis is the financial reality behind Martí's observation that \"tens of thousands of already approved units cannot get construction financing.\" He correctly diagnoses the symptom. He misidentifies the cure. The gap is not primarily caused by Wall Street greed or interest rate levels - it is caused by construction costs in California growing faster than the AMI-linked rent structure can support.\n\nThe solution is not to wait for interest rates to fall or Wall Street to return. The solution is to **compress construction costs** so that the gap either closes or becomes small enough to fill with available soft money.\n\nThis is why unit economics is destiny. And this is why Factory Built Housing is not a future aspiration - it is an urgent near-term financial necessity.\n\n### Factory Built Housing: The Cost Revolution Already Underway\n\nFactory Built Housing (FBH) - the umbrella term covering off-site prefabrication including panelized elements and volumetric modular units - represents the most important near-term cost reduction tool available to housing policy.\n\nDocumented performance data on FBH for three-to-four story wood-frame multifamily projects:\n- **Cost savings: up to 20%** vs. traditional site-built construction\n- **Timeline reduction: 40-50%** vs. traditional construction schedules\n\nThese are not theoretical projections. They are recorded outcomes from completed projects. The implications for unit economics are transformative:\n\n**If a traditional affordable project costs $700K/unit with a 5-year timeline:**\n- FBH reduces cost to approximately $560K/unit\n- FBH reduces timeline to approximately 2.5-3 years\n- Shorter timeline reduces interest carry by 40-50%\n- Reduced interest carry further reduces effective cost by $50-80K/unit\n- Combined effect: FBH-produced unit at approximately $480-510K/unit\n- Combined with LIHTC + bonds under OBBB 25% threshold: potentially $350K/unit\n\noWOW's Oakland tower assembled in 65 days, with full floors installed in a single day, at $425K/unit market rate declining to $350K/unit with LIHTC applied, is not an anomaly. It is the leading edge of a production revolution that policy must now be designed to accelerate.\n\n**The FBH barrier is not technology. It is regulation.**\n\nCurrent obstacles to FBH scaling in California:\n- Building codes written for site-built construction penalize modular dimensions\n- Inspection regimes assume sequential on-site processes incompatible with factory production\n- Plan check procedures require project-specific review that negates standardization benefits\n- Labor agreements written for craft-based site work do not address factory production jurisdiction\n- Financing institutions still price FBH projects as \"novel risk\" despite documented track record\n- Permitting processes do not accommodate parallel factory/site work timelines\n\nEach of these is a policy choice, not a physical constraint. Austin's 2025 single-stair ordinance for 5-story buildings is one example of code reform enabling lower-cost construction on smaller lots. California needs a comprehensive FBH facilitation framework covering permitting, approvals, inspections, and labor protections that captures the documented 20% cost savings and 40-50% timeline reductions at scale.\n\nThe political argument for this is straightforward: **If public dollars can produce 20-25% more affordable units by enabling FBH methods, opposing FBH adoption is not progressive. It is wasteful of the public resources the progressive left claims to prioritize.**\n\n---\n\n## PART THREE: THE \"GO SLOW AND SAY NO\" CASE - STEEL-MANNED\n\n### What Martí and the Progressive Left Get Genuinely Right\n\n**1. The 54,000 Stalled Units Argument is Real**\n\nMartí's most powerful empirical point cannot be dismissed. San Francisco has 54,394 approved but unbuilt units sitting in the pipeline. If zoning were the primary constraint, these units would be under construction. They are not.\n\nThis proves that zoning reform alone, absent financing reform and cost compression, is insufficient. Politicians who campaign primarily on upzoning are offering incomplete solutions. The gap financing crisis described above is the mechanism behind this stalled pipeline - costs have outrun the financing structures available to build these units.\n\n**2. Upzoning Without Land Value Capture Transfers Wealth Upward**\n\nThe land value capture problem is analytically sound:\n- Rezoning from 5 to 10 units doubles land value instantly\n- Landowners rationally hold sites waiting for higher rents\n- Outside investors buy up rezoned parcels before local developers can act\n- Smaller community-rooted developers get priced out\n\nThis is not anti-market fantasy. It is basic real estate economics. Portland addressed it by making density mandatory rather than optional, forcing land value into units rather than speculation. San Francisco's reforms have repeatedly failed to include this mechanism.\n\n**3. Deep Poverty Cannot Be Solved by Markets at Any Scale**\n\nThe 48,000 unit gap for households earning under $25,000 annually (30% AMI) in Austin - a city that executed supply reform better than almost any other American city - proves this point conclusively. Even Austin's remarkable success left the bottom of the income distribution unserved by market mechanisms.\n\nMartí is correct that:\n- 0-30% AMI housing requires deep permanent subsidy\n- Vouchers (Section 8) are being cut in the current federal environment\n- Market production will never serve this population profitably\n- Public or quasi-public mechanisms are necessary here\n\nThis concession is important and the moderate abundance position must make it explicitly.\n\n**4. The \"Accretion of Requirements\" Problem is Real but Cuts Both Ways**\n\nMartí's observation that SF's public housing program layers requirements from multiple funding sources - each well-intentioned, collectively destructive to cost efficiency - is confirmed by Ward's RAND research and by the Terner Center's finding that each additional layer of public financing adds approximately $20,000/unit in administrative costs.\n\nThis is a genuine problem. But it undermines his own social housing proposal as much as it undermines the LIHTC model. Any public financing system accumulates requirements over time. The solution is disciplined requirements consolidation, not a switch from one public financing mechanism to another.\n\n**5. Social Housing Has Working Precedents**\n\nSeattle's social housing program, the Netherlands, Denmark, Singapore - these are not fantasies. They are functioning systems producing housing at lower cost with greater long-term affordability guarantees. The moderate position must acknowledge these precedents rather than dismissing them.\n\n**6. The Blue State Construction Cost Premium is a Real Policy Failure**\n\nMartí touches on this when he notes his developer friend builds $100,000/unit cheaper in South San Francisco. This is the RAND finding made personal: California's regulatory environment, labor requirements, and soft cost structure create a construction cost premium that is a policy choice, not a geographic inevitability.\n\nThis is where the progressive left and the abundance moderates should agree: **The absurd differential in construction costs between California/New York and Texas/Colorado is a crisis that demands direct policy intervention.** The RAND data showing California soft costs at 4× Texas levels, with pre-development times more than twice as long, is not a market failure. It is a regulatory failure that happens to be concentrated in blue states.\n\n---\n\n### Where the Progressive Case Collapses\n\n**1. The unit economics results are empirically measurable:\n\n- Affordable housing costs $900K-$1M+ per unit\n- Construction timelines average 6+ years\n- Gap financing requirements have grown to the point where many projects cannot close\n- The same public dollar that builds 1 unit in SF builds 2+ units in Colorado (Ward/RAND)\n- 54,000+ approved units remain unbuilt because the financing math doesn't work\n\nThis is not a theoretical failure. It is a documented, measurable misallocation of scarce public subsidy resources. When the progressive model costs twice what the moderate hybrid model costs per unit, every dollar spent on the progressive model is a dollar that housed half as many people.\n\n**2. The Social Housing Alternative Has No Viable Unit Economics**\n\nMartí calls for a public bank, project revenue bonds, and a city-as-builder model. But provides zero cost per unit projections for any of these at scale.\n\nThe available evidence is not encouraging:\n- SF's current public housing costs $900K-$1M+/unit\n- Colorado is the only state where nonprofit affordable housing costs approach market-rate costs - and no one yet fully understands why\n- The city cannot bond its way to 54,000 units at even $600K each ($32.4 billion)\n- Social housing systems in Europe took decades to build and operate within construction cost structures fundamentally different from California's\n\nThe social housing proposal is not a plan. It is an aspiration without unit economics. Calling for social housing while SF nonprofit production costs $1M/unit is not a solution to the housing crisis. It is an expensive way to produce very few units for a very small fraction of the people who need them.\n\n**3. The Three-Pathway Framework is Already Obsolete**\n\nMartí's framework:\n1. Deep subsidy public housing\n2. Wall Street luxury (boom-dependent)\n3. Social housing (aspirational)\n\nThis framework misses the fourth pathway that is *already operating* in San Francisco:\n\n**4. Hybrid capital stack: LIHTC + bonds + reduced gap financing + FBH productivity**\n\n960 Howard (200 units, ~100% affordable), 300 De Haro (450 units, 100% affordable at $480K/unit), 1633 Valencia (145 units, $530K/unit with design-build method) are not Wall Street luxury. They are not deep-subsidy public housing. They are not social housing. They are hybrid market-public instruments producing affordable units at costs dramatically below the $1M+ nonprofit benchmark.\n\nAnd with OBBB's 2026 reduction of the bond financing threshold from 50% to 25%, this fourth pathway is about to become dramatically more accessible and scalable.\n\nMartí's framework was accurate in 2019. It is empirically false in 2026.\n\n**4. The Filtering Mechanism is Now Empirically Confirmed at Speed**\n\nStorper's 124-year timeline calculation assumed static conditions. It is an already-occurring reallocation of private capital into affordable housing production, driven by changing risk-return profiles and enabled by federal and state financing innovations.\n\n**The Capital Stack Pivot: What Changed and Why**\n\nThree structural changes converged to make hybrid affordable development more attractive to private capital than market-rate development:\n\n1. **Market-rate returns collapsed**: High interest rates plus high construction costs made traditional private development unfeasible. The 20% returns Wall Street required for market-rate SF projects became unachievable.\n\n2. **LIHTC + bond returns became relatively superior**: Tax credit equity delivers predictable, credit-driven returns that do not depend on rent appreciation or exit cap rates. In a volatile market, predictability commands a premium.\n\n3. **The OBBB financing threshold reduction**: Dropping the bond financing requirement from 50% to 25% in 2026 dramatically expands the universe of projects that can access 4% LIHTC, allowing California to stretch its bond cap much further.\n\nThe result: market-rate developers DM Development, Align Real Estate Partners, Martin Building Co., and oWOW have a combined 1,200+ affordable units in progress in San Francisco alone. California's TCAC approved projects totaling 25,781 units in 2025 - 10,000 more than the prior year.\n\nThis is not ideological conversion. This is rational capital reallocation in response to changing risk-return profiles. Adam Smith's invisible hand, properly constrained by the LIHTC regulatory framework, is producing affordable housing in San Francisco today.\n\n**The RAND Study as the Best Practices Cornerstone**\n\nWard's RAND comparative cost analysis of California, Colorado, and Texas provides the empirical foundation for a best practices framework that California and San Francisco must adopt as policy priority.\n\nThe findings establish clear benchmarks:\n\n| Cost Component | California vs. Texas |\n|---------------|---------------------|\n| Total development cost | 2-3× Texas |\n| Land costs | ~3× Texas |\n| Hard costs | ~1.5× Texas |\n| Soft costs | ~4× Texas |\n| Pre-development time | 2× longer than Texas |\n| Construction time | 50% longer than Texas |\n| Affordable vs. market rate premium | 1.5× in CA (vs. no statistically significant difference in CO) |\n\nThe soft cost differential - 4× Texas - is the most policy-actionable finding because soft costs are **overwhelmingly the product of policy choices**, not geographic or market conditions. Impact fees, entitlement processes, legal requirements, multiple funding source compliance costs - these are all choices California has made and can unmake.\n\nColorado's anomaly is particularly important: it is the only state in the RAND sample where affordable housing costs no more than market-rate housing. Understanding and replicating Colorado's approach should be a California state priority. The preliminary hypothesis is disciplined capital stack management - fewer funding sources, fewer conflicting requirements, lower compliance burden per unit.\n\n**The Best Practices Regime Derived from RAND + Capital Stack Evidence:**\n\n1. **Pre-development time reduction is the highest-value intervention**: Ward's finding that pre-development time is the single largest cost driver means that anything which shortens the entitlement process directly reduces financing costs, investor risk premiums, and interest carry - without spending a single public dollar.\n\n2. **Soft cost compression through fee reform and requirements consolidation**: Impact fee waivers/deferrals for affordable projects, reduction of funding sources from 6-8 to 2-3, elimination of redundant reviews (SF Arts Commission, disability review redundant to ADA requirements) - each of these directly improves the unit economics that determine project feasibility.\n\n3. **By-right approval as the single most powerful reform**: When approval is predictable, developers can underwrite projects with confidence. When approval is discretionary, projects require higher returns to compensate for uncertainty - making fewer projects feasible.\n\n4. **Colorado's stack discipline as the model**: Until RAND investigates why Colorado controls affordable costs so well, the working hypothesis is requirements consolidation. California should investigate, document, and replicate this approach immediately.\n\nThis RAND-derived best practices framework, combined with the capital stack pivot already underway in SF, constitutes the strongest empirical argument for the moderate abundance position: **private markets, when properly structured through federal and state financing innovation and when released from the blue-state regulatory premium, can radically remake the housing supply for every income level above 30% AMI.**\n\n---\n\n### What the Full Evidence Record Supports\n\n**1. Austin cut site plan review times by more than half in one year. SF can do the same.\n\n**3. Impact Fee Reform**\nWaive or defer impact fees for projects below 80% AMI income averaging to TCO. Ward identifies SF impact fees as among the highest in the country - a primary driver of the soft cost differential. This directly improves gap financing feasibility at current interest rates.\n\n**4. Funding Source Consolidation**\nThe Terner Center finding - $20,000/unit added cost per additional funding source - means consolidating from 6-8 sources to 2-3 sources saves $60-100K/unit on a typical SF affordable project. TCAC and HCD should develop consolidated funding vehicles that replace multiple-source stacks.\n\n**5. Parking Minimums Eliminated Near Transit**\nAustin did this citywide in 2023. Each underground parking stall adds $70,000-$100,000 in SF. This is the single easiest hard cost reduction available.\n\n### Tier 2: Medium-Term Structural Changes - Remake the Cost Structure (2-5 years)\n\n**6. Comprehensive FBH Legislation**\nA California FBH Facilitation Act covering:\n- Building code amendments for modular dimensions\n- Labor protections adapted for factory production (organize factory workers, not just site workers)\n- Financing institution risk guidance from CalHFA and HCD\n- Public procurement requirements for FBH on state-funded projects\n- Factory siting support in California to reduce transportation costs\n\n**7. Portland-Style Mandatory Missing Middle**\nNot optional density bonuses. Mandatory allowance of 4-6 units on single-family lots with genuine by-right approval. Link size to density: larger buildings must include more units. This forces land value into housing production rather than speculation.\n\n**8. Land Banking Before Upzoning**\nMartí is correct on this specific point: acquire publicly-owned and opportunity sites at current values *before* upzoning raises land costs. Ground-lease them for affordable housing at below-market rates. This captures upzoning value for public benefit.\n\n**9. Colorado Best Practices Investigation and Implementation**\nCommission RAND follow-up research on Colorado's affordable/market-rate cost parity. Implement findings as California policy. The fact that Colorado produces affordable housing at no premium to market rate - while California produces it at 1.5× market rate - represents potentially billions in annual savings.\n\n**10. OBBB 25% Bond Threshold Maximization**\nCalifornia's TCAC should immediately begin retooling allocation processes for the 2026 bond financing threshold reduction. This is the single largest near-term federal policy change affecting affordable housing production and California must be positioned to maximize its benefit from day one.\n\n### Tier 3: Long-Term Commitments - Address What Markets Cannot (5-10 years)\n\n**11. Direct Subsidy for 0-30% AMI - More Efficient, Not Just More**\nThis population cannot be served by markets. The strategy:\n- Maximize efficiency of existing subsidy through FBH and streamlined requirements (same public dollars, more units)\n- Protect project-based vouchers where politically viable\n- Community land trusts for permanent affordability on public sites\n- Social housing pilots where political will exists (Seattle model as starting point)\n- Target: double units produced per public dollar through cost compression before seeking additional appropriations\n\n**12. Construction Industry Transformation**\n- Require FBH for all publicly subsidized projects above 20 units\n- Fund factory siting and workforce training in California\n- Reform union agreements to organize factory workers under appropriate frameworks\n- Use public procurement to create demand certainty justifying factory investment\n- Target: $200-250K/unit within a decade through productivity gains matching the manufacturing sector\n\n---\n\n## CONCLUSION: THE FINAL VERDICT\n\nThe housing affordability crisis in San Francisco and blue cities generally is not primarily a story of market failure. It is a story of regulatory failure - specifically, a self-reinforcing system where well-intentioned progressive requirements have raised construction costs to the point where the gap between what housing costs to build and what low-income households can afford has become unbridgeable without ever-increasing public subsidy.\n\n**Unit economics is destiny.** When affordable housing costs $1M/unit to build in San Francisco and $350K/unit through FBH-enabled hybrid financing, the progressive model is not more compassionate. It is less efficient. It houses fewer people per public dollar. It leaves more families in the stalled pipeline waiting years for financing to close. It wastes the scarcest resource in housing policy - public subsidy dollars - on process, compliance, and regulatory overhead rather than on roofs over people's heads.\n\nThe capital stack pivot already underway - market-rate developers producing 1,200+ affordable units in SF, TCAC approving 25,781 units statewide in 2025 - proves that private markets respond to incentive changes. The animal spirits of private capital are already reallocating into affordable production. The question is whether policy will accelerate this reallocation or continue to obstruct it.\n\nFactory Built Housing is the near-term cost revolution that makes the entire affordability equation work. Documented 20% cost savings and 40-50% timeline reductions are not theoretical. They are recorded outcomes. Policy must be redesigned to facilitate rather than obstruct this production method.\n\nThe RAND best practices framework - reduce pre-development time, compress soft costs to approach Colorado levels, eliminate the blue-state regulatory premium, consolidate funding source requirements - provides a precise, evidence-based roadmap that California and San Francisco can implement now.\n\nThe \"go slow and say no\" approach has had its trial. Fourteen years. The verdict is in. It produced the most expensive housing in America, the most visible homelessness crisis in America, and the departure of the working-class families it claimed to protect.\n\nThe moderate abundance approach is not a betrayal of progressive values. It is the application of progressive outcomes-thinking to a problem that progressive process-thinking has made catastrophically worse.\n\nAdam Smith is not the enemy here. The enemy is artificial scarcity, regulatory overhead, and the gap financing crisis that results when construction costs are allowed to inflate at 3× the rate of tenant incomes. These enemies serve the comfortable at the expense of the disadvantaged - exactly as Bayard Rustin warned in 1976.\n\nThe market, properly constrained and correctly incentivized through LIHTC expansion, OBBB financing reform, FBH facilitation, and by-right approval, is already building affordable housing in San Francisco. The cranes are going up. The capital has reallocated. The unit economics are improving.\n\n**Remove the friction. Reform the code for FBH. Consolidate the capital stack. Approve in 30 days or deem it approved. Capture land value before upzoning. Subsidize deeply only where markets structurally cannot reach. Build at $350K per unit, not $1M per unit.**\n\nThat is not magical thinking. That is what Austin did. That is what Portland did. That is what oWOW is doing in Oakland and San Francisco right now.\n\nThe only magic would be expecting 14 more years of \"go slow and say no\" to produce a different result.
