Summary: Bernal Heights Community Meeting on Affordable Housing & the Bernal Safeway

The Setup

The meeting, hosted in Bernal Heights (apparently under the auspices of the Bernal Heights Democratic Club), gathered around the question of what to do about the 3350 Mission St. “Bernal Safeway” proposal — a 379-unit, 6-story ministerial project in which only 76 units (20%) are affordable, the minimum required to unlock SB 330 and state density-bonus streamlining. The moderator (Leslie) framed the evening around the four Safeway redevelopment sites citywide and the histories of displacement attached to each neighborhood (Japanese American internment in the Fillmore, redevelopment-era Black displacement, Latino/Filipino gentrification in the Mission and Bernal).

Two presenters anchored the discussion, both firmly on the tenant/decommodification side of the housing debate: - Kyle Sneely — policy and development director, San Francisco Community Land Trust, with a background in progressive electoral and ballot-measure work (Prop I 2020 transfer tax, 2022 vacancy/“empty homes” tax). - “Shanti” — legislative and communications director of Tenants Together (a 70-org statewide tenant coalition), SFCLT board member, DSA member, and former member of the city’s housing fund oversight board.

The Principal Questions the Presenters Tried to Answer

The moderator deliberately posed two questions, and the audience forced a third:

  1. “What are the potential sources of funding to make affordable housing — ideally to make a Safeway site 100% affordable?”
  2. “What strategies could a coalition (tenants, land trusts, nonprofit developers, labor) use to push any Safeway site toward 100% affordability?”
  3. From the audience: “What is the moonshot — the systemic vision to actually deliver the ~46,000 affordable units, rather than fighting building-by-building?”

The unspoken premise behind all three: the gap between what the state’s streamlining laws actually deliver (≈13–20% affordable, market-rate-led) and what this room wants (100% affordable, decommodified). The presenters’ explicit goal was to argue that the path to the latter is funding and organizing, not zoning and supply.

How the Presenters Answered

Kyle (funding sources). He reframed the RHNA numbers — 82,000 total units mandated by 2031, of which more than half (≈46,000) must be affordable — and stressed that the city has poured its energy into market-rate tools (streamlining, upzoning) that are “a very different policy intervention than funding.” His three concrete revenue “asks”: - Local: expand the 2012 voter-approved Housing Trust Fund cap from $50M to $125M/year by capturing growth in property-tax revenue (“no new taxes”), creating a steady stream the city could bond against. He flagged a Rules Committee hearing the following Monday at 9 a.m. as an immediate action item. - Regional: revive a Bay Area affordable-housing bond (the failed 2024 ~$20B effort), this time with real grassroots buy-in. - State: a proposed ~$10B statewide housing bond (≈$7B for production) potentially on the November ballot.

Shanti (the systemic critique). Her central, sophisticated point was that the RHNA affordable mandate “is not a real mandate.” Streamlining laws penalize cities only for falling under overall targets — treating all income levels (all “rents”) the same — so leaders are never actually held accountable for chronically underbuilding low- and middle-income housing. Her diagnosis: San Francisco fights “development by development by development,” exhaustingly and reactively, because leaders refuse to bank land, earmark sites, and commit consistent public subsidy before projects appear. Even nominally “100% affordable” projects, she noted, require “Frankensteining” 10–15 financing sources (LIHTC — a “paltry,” Reagan-era investor tax credit substituting for direct public funding — plus a patchwork of others). Her conclusion: demand consistent public subsidy and a social housing system free of the speculative market.

The organizing template (a third presenter/“Speaker 3”). He offered the “Monster in the Mission” / Plaza 16 Coalition at 16th & Mission as the model: seven years (2013–2020) of organizing that began with a full year of listening to articulate an affirmative community vision, used dramatic tactics (people’s mic, neighborhood-sited Planning Commission hearings, branding the project a “monster”), and ultimately converted the site to 100% affordable — finishing the job by using one project’s inclusionary obligations to acquire the land. His honest caveat: state law has since stripped away many of those tools (e.g., the ability to demand a Planning Commission hearing for a ministerial project), so organizing must now focus on political pressure and the “bully pulpit.”

The social-housing frame. Kyle closed by reframing “social housing” not as a binary switch but a spectrum — “how social is my housing?” (permanence, community control, autonomy, off the speculative market) — and tied it to a coming ballot campaign to restore the Prop I transfer tax the mayor is threatening to cut and dedicate it to social housing.

How the Q&A Illuminated the Democratic Divide

This is the crux of your request, and the most revealing dynamic is who was in the room and who wasn’t. Despite the citywide debate pitting YIMBY/“abundance” moderates against tenant-left “values voters,” this audience was overwhelmingly the latter. The abundance/YIMBY position (embodied in the project itself, in Align Real Estate’s David Balducci, and in YIMBY Action’s Brandon Powell, who was “thrilled” in the press) was the absent antagonist being argued against, not a live voice at the mic. That self-selection is itself a portrait of the divide — these two camps increasingly meet in ballot-measure fights and the press, not in the same neighborhood rooms.

The audience questions broke down along recognizable lines:

  • The veteran organizer (Speakers 4/5). Pure values-voter energy: he cited the local precedent of Coleridge Park Homes (49 units of affordable senior housing forced onto a private developer’s site/roof above the old Big Lots in the 1980s), quoted Frederick Douglass (“power concedes nothing without a demand”), declared “we’re heretics,” and moved that the club formally adopt a position demanding 100% affordability on the Bernal Safeway. This is the worldview the article’s Stephen Torres voiced — that 76 of 379 units is an insultingly “small percentage,” and that upzoning/density bonus has been falsely “portrayed as increasing access to affordable housing.”

  • The “moonshot” questioner (Speaker 6). Notably, this was the closest thing to an abundance-curious voice — frustrated with one-off, building-by-building combat and asking how the coalition gets to scale: “How do we actually build 46,000 units?… How do we say in 2026, by 2030 we’re going to have this fund in place?” Tellingly, the presenters answered not with production or supply but by redirecting to consistent public funding (Kyle’s “depressing” state moonshot: California has no dedicated year-over-year housing fund, spends ~1% of the general fund on housing/homelessness vs. ~6% on prisons) and acquisition (Speaker 3’s moonshot: change state law to allow local income taxes on the city’s 40 billionaires, then buy distressed portfolios — e.g., Veritas buildings in foreclosure — to pull thousands of units off the speculative market “in a matter of weeks”). The exchange perfectly captures the divide: the abundance instinct asks “how do we build more?”; this room answers “how do we fund, acquire, and decommodify more?”

  • The labor/movement questioner (Speaker 7/6). A union educator recalled the exhilarating UESF-backed march from the Mission to City Hall during the Monster in the Mission fight, and asked the hardest political question of the night: how do you rebuild that kind of cross-class movement “in a city in which there is enormous wealth power and a lot of people are more attached to the big money than to the traditional San Francisco neighborhood”? Kyle’s candid, somewhat deflated answer — that housing organizers and labor are colliding simultaneously (mass city layoffs, a proposed transfer-tax cut “to literally Donald Trump”) and “we all need to hang out more” — conceded he didn’t have the answer he wished he had.

The Underlying Crisis the Meeting Surfaced

The evening crystallizes why San Francisco Democrats remain deadlocked on housing the nurses, bus drivers, teachers, and first responders the moderator invoked:

  • The moderate/YIMBY/abundance wing (Wiener’s Sacramento laws, SB 330, density bonus, the Lurie upzoning, the developers) treats the binding constraint as supply and process — remove discretionary review, let projects “pencil out,” and 20% affordability comes baked in alongside abundant market-rate units. To them, the Bernal Safeway is an unambiguous win: 379 homes and 76 affordable in a neighborhood that’s “not been part of the housing story for a generation.”

  • The progressive/values wing in this room treats the binding constraint as money, land, and power. They are not persuaded by “rising tide” supply arguments (“rising tide does not, in fact, lift all boats”), they distrust the speculative market as the root cause, and they regard 20% affordable as a corporate giveaway that streamlining laws have made harder, not easier, to contest. Their solutions — expanded trust funds, regional/state bonds, restored transfer taxes, local income taxes on billionaires, social housing, land banking, and Plaza 16-style organizing — all route around the private market rather than harnessing it.

The unresolved tension the meeting could not bridge is the one Shanti named directly: streamlining laws reward building anything, while this community demands building only the right thing (100% affordable) — and the state has simultaneously removed the leverage (hearings, discretionary review) that once let neighborhoods extract that outcome. The presenters’ honest bottom line was that, absent a transformative and consistent public funding source, “100% affordable on the Safeway” remains an organizing aspiration enforced through pressure and acquisition after the fact — exactly the exhausting, reactive, project-by-project trench warfare they admit is failing to close the 46,000-unit gap.
