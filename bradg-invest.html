Analysis of Brad Geddus’s Presentation

On the Sales Pitch Structure

This is a textbook “dinner seminar” prospecting presentation aimed at affluent pre-retirees. The structure follows a well-worn playbook:

  1. Establish credibility/trust (fiduciary talk, CFP credentials)
  2. Differentiate from competitors (disparage “95% of advisors”)
  3. Create FOMO/anxiety (taxes, RMDs, bond losses, market crashes)
  4. Tease sophisticated solutions (CRUTs, IDGTs, covered calls)
  5. Drive to a one-on-one meeting (“when we get a chance to connect”)

The transcript is riddled with grammatical incoherence (likely auto-transcribed), but the substantive issues stand on their own.

Substantive Analysis

Fiduciary Discussion — Mostly Accurate but Self-Serving

His description of fiduciary vs. suitability is correct in broad strokes. The “Reg BI” standard now applies to broker-dealers, which he glosses over. The three “checks” (independence, Series 65, CFP) are reasonable, though he conveniently describes a profile that matches his own firm. Standard prospecting move.

Roth Conversions — Correct Concept, Oversimplified

The basic logic is sound: paying tax on $1M is less than paying tax on $2M at the same rate. But the analysis is incomplete:

  • He never mentions the break-even math. A Roth conversion only wins if your future marginal rate ≥ current marginal rate. For many retirees who drop into lower brackets between ages 60–73, partial conversions make sense — but not always.
  • He ignores opportunity cost of taxes paid today (those dollars could compound).
  • He omits IRMAA Medicare surcharges, ACA subsidy cliffs, and state tax considerations — major real-world factors.
  • The “kids will pay tax” framing assumes the worst — but inherited Roths also have 10-year distribution rules under SECURE Act, and beneficiaries may be in low brackets.
  • His RMD scare-math ($75K growing to $150K) is misleading — RMD percentages are designed against a depleting portfolio assumption, and he ignores that the IRA is also growing tax-deferred.

Conclusion: Right idea, oversold as a near-universal solution.

Covered Calls — A Significant Red Flag

This is where I become skeptical. He pitches covered calls as a way to “earn 3–6% extra” on concentrated positions like Apple. Problems:

  • Covered calls cap your upside. On a stock like Apple, which has driven enormous gains precisely because of unbounded upside, capping it is potentially the worst possible strategy for a long-term holder.
  • Premiums of 3–6% annually are not free money — they reflect the market’s pricing of forgone upside.
  • If the call is exercised, you trigger the very capital gains event the client was trying to avoid. He glosses over this.
  • Covered calls generate ordinary income or short-term gains in many cases — tax-inefficient.
  • Crucially: covered call strategies often generate fees and trading activity that benefit the advisor.

This is not “tax planning” — it’s an options overlay strategy being marketed as tax management.

Charitable Remainder Unitrust (CRUT) — Real but Niche

CRUTs are legitimate for highly appreciated assets and charitably inclined clients. But:

  • You are giving the asset away. The remainder goes to charity, not heirs. He soft-pedals this enormously (“you’re not giving it away” — yes, you are; you just retain an income stream).
  • The charitable deduction is small relative to the asset value (present value of the remainder interest).
  • Irrevocable. Once funded, you cannot undo it.
  • Only makes sense if you have genuine charitable intent. Pitching it as a tax trick is misleading.

Estate Tax / IDGT — Misleading Urgency

He warns about “40 cents on the dollar” estate tax. Current federal exemption is ~$13.99M per person ($27.98M per couple) in 2025. His “$15M” figure is in the ballpark for individuals but he’s creating urgency for people who likely don’t have an estate tax problem. Yes, the exemption was scheduled to sunset, but the OBBBA (2025) made the higher exemption permanent. He’s selling complexity people don’t need.

Modern Portfolio Theory Bashing — A Major Red Flag

This is the biggest tell in the entire presentation. Disparaging MPT and the 60/40 portfolio is a classic pitch from advisors who sell:

  • Annuities (especially indexed annuities)
  • Structured products
  • Managed options strategies
  • “Bucket” strategies with high fees

His attack on bond funds (“you don’t own a bond”) is technically dishonest. A bond fund is a portfolio of actual bonds. Yes, they have mark-to-market volatility, but cherry-picking TLT’s performance from 2020 (when 30-year Treasuries yielded ~1.5%) is a deliberately rigged comparison. Any long-duration asset bought at peak prices declined. That’s not a flaw in bond funds — it’s a flaw in buying long duration at low yields.

“Distribution Plan” — Likely a Bucket Strategy Pitch

The transcript cuts off just as he’s about to reveal his alternative. The setup (“mountain chart,” “distribution phase,” dismissing pie charts) strongly suggests a time-segmented bucket strategy, often paired with annuities or structured notes. These can work, but they’re frequently wrapped in higher fees than a well-constructed total-return portfolio.

Would I Invest My Nest Egg With His Firm?

No.

Reasons:

  1. Heavy reliance on fear-based marketing — RMD horror stories, bond fund scare tactics, estate tax urgency for people who likely don’t owe it.

  2. Strategies that conveniently generate fees and complexity — covered calls, CRUTs, IDGTs all require ongoing management and reinforce advisor dependency.

  3. The MPT-bashing is a red flag. Decades of evidence support diversified, low-cost, rebalanced portfolios. Advisors who attack this are usually selling something more expensive.

  4. Cherry-picked examples (TLT, Apple) presented without context.

  5. He hasn’t shown his fees, his AUM, his track record, or any backtested performance — only sophisticated-sounding techniques.

  6. The “fiduciary” emphasis is heavy on marketing, light on substance. A dual-registered RIA can still wear two hats.

What I Would Do Instead

  • Get a flat-fee or hourly fiduciary CFP to do a one-time retirement and tax plan (NAPFA, XY Planning Network, Garrett Network).
  • Consider a low-cost custodian (Vanguard, Fidelity, Schwab) with a diversified portfolio.
  • Address Roth conversions, charitable strategies, and concentrated stock issues case-by-case with a tax professional — not a one-size-fits-all sales pitch.
  • Run the math yourself or with a fee-only planner on any strategy before paying AUM fees (typically 1%/year = ~25–30% of your lifetime returns).

The presentation is polished, partially accurate, and strategically incomplete in ways that consistently favor the advisor’s business model. That’s exactly the pattern your skepticism is right to flag.
