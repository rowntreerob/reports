The Debate, Extended: Two Forward-Looking Visions Collide

New inputs: Lewis’s Chapter 17, “Forecasting the Future” (the book’s closing argument) and Lazard’s LCOE+ v19.0, July 2026 (the updated annual). These are the first genuinely forward-looking* documents in the record, and — as you predicted — they point in nearly opposite directions. Lewis’s chapter is a total-system, planning-oriented forecast that concludes nuclear must lead. Lazard v19.0 is a project-level snapshot that explicitly refuses to name an optimal mix — yet still finds renewables the cheapest new electron. The most important development is that the ground has shifted under both of them: costs rose everywhere, storage costs reversed, and the grid operators tightened the very accreditation rules the debate turned on last round.*

Round 9 — The demand tsunami: who serves the AI load?

PROF. LEWIS: Chapter 17 opens with the fact that changes everything: demand is now rising rapidly — data centers, AI, electrification of transport and industry. After two decades of flat US load, we are back to steep growth, and it is a firm, 24/7, high-capacity-factor load. A hyperscaler running a training cluster does not care that solar is cheap at noon; it needs 1,000 MW at 3 a.m. in a January dark-doldrum. This is precisely the load profile that rewards nuclear’s 90%+ capacity factor and punishes a 20%-capacity-factor solar farm. The demand surge is a nuclear tailwind.

AMORY: The demand surge is real — Lazard v19.0 leads its executive summary with it, noting “continuous upward revisions to demand projections” driving “a sharp increase in announced new-build gas generation despite a 15-year-high LCOE.” But read what the market is actually doing with that demand, Professor. It is not queuing up for reactors that come online in 2035. Lazard says wind, solar, and storage “are expected to continue to account for the majority of near-term U.S. capacity additions given their relatively short deployment timeline.” When you need power this decade, the binding constraint is speed, and a solar-plus-storage project clears interconnection and construction in 18 months while a reactor takes 84.

PROF. LEWIS: “Announced” gas — note that word. The same summary admits gas turbine supply is constrained, extending timelines “well beyond historical norms.” So the fast option isn’t fast anymore either. When both firm options are slow, you should have started the reactors five years ago. My chapter’s point is that we keep making the near-term convenient choice and thereby foreclosing the long-term correct one.

MODERATOR: Both are describing the same v19.0 fact — a demand shock hitting a supply-constrained grid — and drawing opposite lessons. Amory owns the near-term deployment-speed argument (unambiguous in the data). Lewis owns the point that speed advantages are eroding as turbine and interconnection queues clog, which weakens the “just build renewables fast” reflex.

Round 10 — “Everything got more expensive” and the cost-of-capital vise

AMORY: Let me get ahead of the Professor’s favorite new number. Yes — v19.0 shows renewables rose. Utility solar went from $38–78 to $40–98; onshore wind from $37–86 to $37–99. Solar is up 85% and wind 70% off their 2021 lows. Tariffs, interest rates, supply-chain repricing. I concede it freely. But it happened to everyone. Gas combined cycle rose to $51–129, gas peaking to $144–276, coal to $72–177. The relative ranking is unchanged: renewables are still the cheapest new-build electron in America. Rising tides lifted every hull; mine still floats highest.

PROF. LEWIS: The relative ranking of plant-level LCOE, yes. But look at what inflation did to the cost-of-capital sensitivity, because this is where my thesis lives. New nuclear now runs from $130/MWh at a 4.2% WACC to $291/MWh at 10%. That spread widened year over year. And here is the argument I have made from the start, now sharpened by v19.0’s own data: nuclear’s uncompetitiveness is manufactured by the discount rate. At sovereign financing — 4.2% — nuclear is $130, in the same neighborhood as offshore wind and cheaper than gas peaking. At merchant equity rates, it’s $291. The technology didn’t change between those two columns. Only the financing did. That is the entire book in one row of a spreadsheet.

AMORY: And it cuts against you every time you invoke it! Apply that same 4.2% sovereign rate to utility solar and it falls to $53. To onshore wind, near $60. Cheap public capital lowers everyone’s cost — and it lowers it most for the asset with the shortest build and the lowest capital intensity. If the US government is going to socialize the cost of capital, the taxpayer gets three-to-four times more clean MWh routing that balance sheet through renewables than through reactors. You keep reaching for a lever that lifts my side higher than yours.

PROF. LEWIS: Not if the renewable MWh arrive when nobody needs them. You keep pricing energy; I keep pricing reliable capacity. We’ll get to that.

MODERATOR: The cost-of-capital exchange is a stalemate that both sides claim as a win — correctly, from their own framing. Lewis is right that nuclear’s headline number is an artifact of merchant financing. Amory is right that public financing is not a nuclear-specific rescue and disproportionately rewards short-cycle, low-capex assets.

Round 11 — The storage reversal (the year’s biggest surprise)

PROF. LEWIS: Now the number Amory hoped I wouldn’t reach. Last round he built his entire endgame on falling storage costs — “the answer to the duck curve is storage, and it’s getting cheaper.” v19.0 shows storage costs reversed and rose sharply. Utility-scale 4-hour LCOS went from $115–254 last year to $210–292 this year. The low end nearly doubled. Lazard attributes it to lithium-ion import tariffs and Foreign-Entity-of-Concern restrictions cutting off cheap Chinese cells. The storage cost-decline curve that your whole thesis rode has bent the wrong way. And 4-hour storage was never the hard problem anyway — my chapter’s point is that no known method economically stores energy for the 800 hours per year, or the 11-day British dark-doldrum, when VRE simply isn’t there.

AMORY: A fair hit, and I’ll take it honestly — this is the single strongest new data point for your side. But let me put it in proportion. First, Lazard is explicit that this is a tariff-and-supply-chain dislocation, not a technology reversal: “the materialization of tariffs on lithium-ion battery imports has curtailed access to the low-cost Chinese cell supply.” Cells got more expensive because of policy, not physics; supply chains are already relocating to Southeast Asia and domestic plants. The learning curve didn’t break — a trade wall was built across it. Second, the storage ITC survived to 2033 under the OBBBA, so the subsidized number is far lower. Third — and this is the concession I made last round and will repeat — I never claimed 4-hour batteries solve a multi-day lull. That’s what geothermal, long-duration storage, demand flexibility, and yes, some firm generation are for.

PROF. LEWIS: “Some firm generation.” We’re circling the drain toward my conclusion.

MODERATOR: Decisive round for Lewis on the facts. The storage reversal genuinely undercuts the “costs always fall, storage will mop up intermittency” narrative that anchored Amory’s Round 3. Amory’s mitigation — that it’s a tariff artifact, not a learning-curve failure — is legitimate but speculative about the future, whereas the cost increase is measured in the present. Advantage Lewis, appropriately hedged.

Round 12 — The penetration trap materializes in the accreditation data

PROF. LEWIS: Last round I predicted that integration costs would rise with penetration — that Lazard’s firming adder was a “today” number that would grow. One year later, watch it grow. The grid operators tightened their capacity-accreditation rules across the board: MISO is moving to Direct Loss of Load (DLOL), which it says yields “generally lower ELCC values for wind and solar.” CAISO replaced flat ELCC with the Slice of Day framework, forcing resources to prove they can serve all 24 hours on the worst day. NYISO went to marginal accreditation, “reducing accredited capacity values for intermittent resources and storage.” The regulators themselves are confirming, in their tariffs, that each additional VRE MWh is worth less to reliability. And the firming costs followed: firmed renewables in v19.0 now run $87 to $167/MWh — with ERCOT solar-plus-storage at $150, PJM solar-plus-storage at $167, NYISO wind-plus-storage at $146. Several of those now sit above the entire gas-combined-cycle band ($51–129) and are climbing into gas-peaking territory. My penetration trap isn’t a theory anymore; it’s in Lazard’s own bar chart.

AMORY: You’ve overstated the verdict, but I grant the direction. Three counterpoints from the same tables. First — the headline still holds: Lazard’s own summary says “even after incorporating these firming costs, renewables remain broadly cost-competitive with the LCOE of CCGTs.” The firmed solar numbers in MISO ($115), CAISO ($92), SPP ($110), PJM ($123) are at or below the gas-CC high end and far below gas peaking. Second — and Lazard flags this in bold — the comparison is rigged against me: it applies a firming charge to renewables but “absent any regional adjustment or offsetting firming charge applied to the CCGTs themselves.” And v19.0 adds a whole new appendix showing gas also gets derated — PJM accredits a gas peaker at just 63%, ERCOT applies an extreme-cold outage adder calibrated to Winter Storm Uri. Gas is not the perfectly firm resource your chapter assumes; it froze in Texas and it fails winter accreditation. Third — where firming bites hardest, it’s because penetration is already high (ERCOT wind at 49% penetration, $62 firming for solar). That’s a success signal — those grids are already running majority-renewable.

PROF. LEWIS: A gas plant that fails one accreditation test still spins synchronous inertia and can burn stored fuel for weeks. A becalmed wind farm produces nothing, no matter how you accredit it. The asymmetry is physical, not clerical.

MODERATOR: The richest exchange of the round. Lewis correctly claims vindication on the trajectory — tightening ELCC (DLOL, Slice of Day, NYISO marginal) is exactly the penetration effect he forecast, and some firmed hybrids now exceed the gas-CC band. Amory correctly notes (a) firmed standalone renewables mostly still beat gas CC, (b) v19.0’s new conventional-accreditation appendix undercuts the “gas is perfectly firm” premise, and (c) the highest firming costs occur precisely in the most decarbonized grids. This is the debate’s live frontier.

Round 13 — The reliability tail: the Iberian blackout and the dark doldrums

PROF. LEWIS: Chapter 17 is where I stop arguing cost and start arguing catastrophe. On April 28, 2025, the Iberian grid collapsed — a cascading blackout across Spain and Portugal, triggered by voltage oscillations on a grid running predominantly on solar with too little synchronous inertia to arrest the disturbance. Within one minute the shutdowns cascaded; it took 10 hours to restore 40% of demand. This is the ancillary-services/stability problem I warned about, made real at national scale. Add the dark doldrums: Germany, January 2017, VRE below 5% for days; Britain, 2021, an 11-day wind drought with offshore output at 3%. And my empirical trump card — Figure 17.3, plotting average electricity price against wind-and-solar share across ~70 countries: the correlation is positive and strong. Denmark and Germany, the VRE champions, have among the highest power prices on earth. The real world is running the experiment, and the bill is arriving. Meanwhile Texas 2021, Hurricane Florence, Hurricane Michael — nuclear ran flawlessly through all of them.

AMORY: Now we’re at the heart of it, and I have to separate three claims you’ve bundled. On the Iberian blackout: the cause is still contested — voltage control, protection settings, and grid-operator procedures are all implicated, not simply “too much solar.” The lesson isn’t “don’t build renewables”; it’s “build grid-forming inverters, synchronous condensers, and proper reactive-power management” — engineering fixes, not a fuel choice. A grid can be stabilized with spinning masses that aren’t reactors. On the dark doldrums: real, and my honest answer is the same as last round — that’s the job of long-duration storage, overbuild-plus-curtailment, continental transmission, and a modest firm backstop. On Figure 17.3: correlation is not causation, Professor, and you know it. Denmark’s high retail price is dominated by taxes, not generation cost — Danish pre-tax wholesale is competitive. You’re reading a tax-policy chart as a physics chart.

PROF. LEWIS: Every one of your fixes — grid-forming inverters, synchronous condensers, continental HVDC, long-duration storage that doesn’t yet exist — is a cost that never appears in the $40 solar LCOE. You keep solving intermittency with an ever-lengthening list of things the LCOE omits. At some point the honest accountant adds them up, and when he does, a firm nuclear kWh looks cheap.

AMORY: And every reactor you build to cover a 3%-of-the-year dark-doldrum sits at low value the other 97%, unless you run it flat-out and curtail me at noon — at which point you’ve built the most expensive peaking capacity in history. The tail risk is real; the question is whether you insure against it with a full fleet of reactors or with a lean layer of firm capacity behind a cheap renewable bulk.

MODERATOR: This is where the two documents most clearly diverge, and where the disagreement becomes partly values and risk-tolerance, not just data. Lewis’s reliability events (Iberia 2025, dark doldrums, Uri) are real and are his strongest forward-looking evidence. Amory is right that the blackout’s root cause is contested and that Fig. 17.3 conflates taxes with generation cost — a genuine methodological flaw in Lewis’s chart. But Amory’s rebuttals increasingly consist of costs that live outside the LCOE, which is precisely Lewis’s meta-point.

Round 14 — Systems analysis vs. snapshot: the quiet convergence

PROF. LEWIS: Let me name the deepest divergence between our two documents, because it’s methodological. My chapter uses systems analysis — the MIT 67-GW study, the California studies, the 42-country study. These model the whole grid over a whole year. Their findings are damning for VRE-dominance: to hit high renewable penetration you must overbuild capacity 3-to-10× (MIT: from 98 GW base case to 325 GW at 75% VRE; California: 500 GW installed to serve a 50-GW need), total system cost rises 5/21/42/98% at 10/30/50/75% VRE, and VRE still falls ~800 hours short every year. Lazard, by its own admission, does none of this. And here’s what’s remarkable about v19.0: Lazard now says so, explicitly. The new “Scope of LCOE+” section states the metric “excludes… system and grid-level costs,” is “not… a total system-cost analysis,” does “not say… the optimal mix,” and — I could have written this sentence — “storage… by itself cannot solve for intermittency” and “firming costs and dispatchability are increasingly critical.” Lazard has, in effect, conceded my central methodological objection from Round 1.

AMORY: I’ll accept that framing — and then turn it. Lazard added the “Scope” section not to endorse nuclear, but to stop both of us from misusing a project-level tool. Read the “Does Not Say” column: it also refuses to say “how demand growth… should be supplied” and offers no “recommendations for specific policy.” It’s a benchmarking instrument, not a manifesto — for either of us. And notice what else v19.0 did: it brought nuclear into the tent. For the first time it publishes nuclear restart economics (Crane/Three Mile Island, Duane Arnold, Palisades) at roughly $71/MWh, with a marginal cost near $31 — cheaper than almost anything on the board — and it adds SMRs (Clinch River, Darlington, Natrium) at ~$180. This is the convergence I offered last round: if firm clean power pencils, I’ll buy it. Restarting a paid-off reactor at $31 marginal is a magnificent deal, and I’m first in line. What I won’t sign is a $175–255 greenfield reactor when firmed renewables deliver at $90–150.

PROF. LEWIS: Then we agree on more than the audience expects. Restart every reactor. Build the SMRs. My chapter argues exactly that — SMRs built incrementally in factories, at lower financing risk, plus large plants financed the way we financed the dams. Where we part is the center of gravity: I want firm, dispatchable, inertia-providing generation to lead, with renewables supplementing. You want renewables to lead, with firm power supplementing.

AMORY: Correct — and that’s the honest disagreement. Not “renewables or nuclear,” but which is the spine and which is the ribs.

MODERATOR: The most productive round. Two real convergences: (1) Lazard’s new “Scope of LCOE+” section is a genuine methodological concession to Lewis — the metric is explicitly not a system-cost or planning tool; (2) v19.0’s addition of nuclear-restart (~$31 marginal, ~$71 LCOE) and SMR data points is the convergence Amory promised — cheap firm clean power he’ll actively support. The residual disagreement is now precisely stated: spine vs. ribs.

Closing statements

PROF. LEWIS (for a nuclear-led grid): “The forward-looking evidence has moved my way in a single year. Storage costs reversed. Grid operators tightened accreditation, confirming that each VRE MWh is worth less at the margin. Iberia blacked out on a low-inertia grid. Firmed renewables in the most decarbonized markets now cost more than gas combined cycle. And Lazard itself has conceded that its headline metric is not a system-cost analysis. The systems studies are unanimous: a renewables-dominant grid demands 3-to-10× overbuild, transmission we haven’t built, storage that doesn’t exist, and still leaves you dark 800 hours a year. The physically and economically coherent 2050 grid is led by firm, carbon-free, inertia-providing nuclear — large plants and SMRs — financed as the century-scale public infrastructure it is, with wind and solar contributing where they’re cheap. Anything else is a bet that the dark doldrums won’t come. They always come.”

AMORY (for a renewables-led grid): “One hard year does not overturn two decades of data. Even after tariffs, inflation, and a storage-cost reversal, renewables remain the cheapest new electron in America, and Lazard confirms they will supply the majority of near-term capacity because nothing else can be built fast enough for the demand shock. Yes, firming costs are real and rising — and the answer is a lean firm layer, not a reactor-first grid: restart the paid-off reactors at $31, build SMRs if they hit $100, deploy geothermal and long-duration storage, wire the continent together, and pay for genuine firm capacity through markets that reward it. The Professor wants to insure a 3%-of-the-year tail risk by making the expensive 97% the backbone. I want the cheap 97% as the backbone and a smart, diverse insurance policy for the tail. Lead with the cheap electrons; firm them intelligently.”

Why the two documents genuinely point in opposite directions

They are answering different questions, and v19.0 is unusually candid about this:

  • Lewis, Ch. 17 asks the planning question — “What is the cheapest, most reliable way to run an entire decarbonized grid for a full year, including the 800 worst hours?” Systems analysis answers: overbuilding weather-dependent generation is expensive and fragile, so lead with firm nuclear. His forecast is a whole-system optimization with reliability as a hard constraint.

  • Lazard, v19.0 asks the procurement question — “What does one incremental MWh cost to generate today?” — and now explicitly disclaims the planning question (“Does Not Say: the optimal mix”). Its answer is that renewables remain the cheapest marginal electron, so they will dominate near-term builds.

The deepest point of the extended debate is that both documents have moved toward the middle in 2026: Lazard added a firming analysis, a conventional-accreditation appendix, a scope disclaimer, and nuclear restart/SMR data points — importing Lewis’s concerns into a renewables-friendly frame. Lewis, for his part, endorses SMRs and incremental factory-built reactors — importing the modularity and speed that were renewables’ advantages. The unresolved question is no longer whether the grid needs both, but which is the spine and which the ribs — and that turns on how much you fear the dark-doldrum tail versus how much you value the cheap, fast, abundant middle.
