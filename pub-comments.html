Public Comment Analysis: Mayor’s Family Zoning Plan

A. HEADLINE TALLY

Total speakers identified: 162

  • SUPPORT: 56 (35%)
  • OPPOSE: 81 (50%)
  • MIXED: 22 (14%)

B. THEMATIC SUMMARY

Top 5 Arguments FROM SUPPORTERS

  1. Housing scarcity is forcing young people / friends / family out of SF (~25 mentions) — “I’m tired of watching my friends leave”
  2. More supply will lower/stabilize rents; cite research from Minneapolis, Austin, Furman Center, etc. (~18 mentions)
  3. 1978 downzoning was a historic mistake / exclusionary zoning has racist legacy that needs correcting (~12 mentions)
  4. West Side needs to do its fair share; equity demands spreading housing/affordable units to well-resourced neighborhoods (~11 mentions)
  5. Need family-sized units / multigenerational housing / want to raise kids here (~14 mentions)
  6. (Honorable mention) Avoid state preemption / builder’s remedy — better to act locally (~7 mentions)

Top 5 Arguments FROM OPPONENTS

  1. Plan threatens rent-controlled units; demolition equals displacement; no real tenant protections in writing (~35 mentions)
  2. Small businesses / legacy businesses will be evicted and destroyed (Joe’s Ice Cream cited repeatedly) (~20 mentions)
  3. 70,000+ units already entitled but unbuilt — problem is financing/affordability, not zoning (~18 mentions)
  4. Plan is a giveaway to developers/speculators; will produce luxury condos, not affordable housing (~22 mentions)
  5. Process was rushed/opaque; maps changed last-minute; District 3/Northeast Waterfront added without EIR review; request continuance (~20 mentions)
  6. (Honorable mention) Historic resources, neighborhood character, views, and infrastructure (water, sewer, fire, transit) at risk (~18 mentions)

Notable Mixed/Conditional Positions

  • SF Heritage / Catherine Petron / Courtney Damkroger / Catherine Howard: Support housing but want Category A historic buildings exempted.
  • Chinatown CDC (Abby, Sharon): Support more housing but want an affordable housing Special Use District + stronger family-size unit requirements.
  • Habitat for Humanity (Holden Weisman) & SF Housing Development Corp (Mitch Madken): Support plan but want stronger affordable-housing pathways and concessions.
  • Eric Munsing / Duboce Triangle NA: Support plan, want even more upzoning on West Side transit corridors + better small-business protections.
  • Christian (Marina developer): Support upzoning conditionally on liquefaction/foundation/grocery requirements.
  • Bob Akasfandiari / Grow the Richmond: Support but say plan should go further.
  • SPUR (Annie Freiman, Erica): Support with technical amendments.
  • Scott Feeney: Supports the equity intent but wants capacity shifted away from rent-controlled multifamily into single-family areas.
  • John Juana (Park Merced shopping center): Specific site-level support; not a citywide stance.

E. CAVEATS

  1. Low-confidence classifications: ~5 speakers (IDs 010 Emily Faxon, 114 Jane Jacobs speaker, 151 Donna Howe, 071 Paris speaker, 035 single mom). Stance was inferred from tone and partial statements.

  2. Ambiguous speaker boundaries:

    • Several speakers in segments 1–2 had no self-introduction (e.g., the Daniel Leary–referenced waterfront speaker, the anti-corruption speaker, the rent-control defender after Tyler in segment 4). I inferred boundaries from topic shifts and chair interjections.
    • The boundary between Amanda Scott (seg 3 end) and Benjamin Lam (seg 4 start) was clean.
    • The boundary between Robin Leslie (seg 4/5) and Jay Natoli was clean.
    • I likely under-counted by 2–5 in segments 1 and 4 where transitions between back-to-back oppositional speakers blurred (e.g., the run of REP coalition speakers).

  3. Transcription quality issues:

    • Some names almost certainly misspelled (Akasfandiari, Yalewalia, Dombalewski, Mechkey, Toretta, etc.).
    • Mid-comment cutoffs by the chair sometimes made the final sentence ambiguous, but rarely changed the stance.
    • Two Chinese-language comments delivered via AI translation — substance was clear (mixed/conditional support).

  4. Confidence in overall tally: HIGH. Even with ±5 speakers of error, the rough proportion (≈50% OPPOSE / 35% SUPPORT / 14% MIXED) is robust. Opposition dominated the room numerically, but supporters were highly organized (YIMBY Action, Grow the Richmond, D9 Neighbors, Housing Action Coalition, SPUR, AIA, building trades subset, Chamber). Opposition was a broader coalition spanning rent-controlled tenants, seniors, neighborhood associations, cultural districts, REP coalition, SF Labor Council, and historic preservationists.

  5. Notable absence: Despite 8+ hours of testimony, no commenter argued the plan goes too far in protecting rent control or historic resources — all critiques from one side were that protections are insufficient; from the other, that the plan should be bolder/faster.
