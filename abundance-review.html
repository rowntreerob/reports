Summary: “What We Got Right — and Wrong — in ‘Abundance’”

Ezra Klein in conversation with Derek Thompson and Marc Dunkelman

A year after publishing Abundance, Klein and Thompson reconvene with Marc Dunkelman (Why Nothing Works) to assess whether the movement they helped catalyze is actually delivering — particularly on the housing crisis crippling blue cities. The honest answer: the vibes are winning, the legislation is improving, but the outcomes aren’t there yet.

1. Three Levels of Assessment

Thompson frames the movement’s progress on three tiers:

  • Vibes (A+): “Abundance” has achieved near-total penetration of Democratic discourse. Hochul, Pritzker, Newsom, Mamdani, and Sherrill all now invoke supply-side framing. Newsom literally signed the “Abundant and Affordable Homes Near Transit Act.”
  • Legislation (B/B+): Real bills have passed in CA, IL, NY explicitly citing the book’s framework. California enacted its most significant housing reforms in decades.
  • Outcomes (Failing): California housing starts have not increased. Looking at FRED data from 2015–2026, you cannot detect either the YIMBY movement or Abundance in the construction numbers. This is the movement’s most damning critique of itself.

2. Why Legislation Isn’t Producing Housing — The Unit Economics Problem

Klein concedes the book under-emphasized a critical truth: passing a bill is not the same as building a unit. A project requires three things to clear:

  1. Legal permission (zoning, permits, CEQA-type reviews) — where reform is finally happening
  2. Financing (penciling out the deal) — gotten dramatically worse
  3. Construction cost (materials + labor) — also worse

Thompson offers a “Russian nesting doll” framework for the housing crisis: - 50-year story: Accumulated zoning/permitting/environmental rules from the 60s–70s (the book’s focus) - 20-year story: Post-2008 decimation of the construction industry — the 2010s had the fewest homes built per capita of any decade on record - 5-year story: Pandemic chaos, inflation, elevated interest rates, and a labor shortage worsened by Trump’s deportation agenda (40% of CA construction workers are foreign-born)

Mayors are now telling Klein: “We’ve made the projects legal. They still don’t pencil.” The financing gap is the new binding constraint, and Abundance didn’t address it.

3. The Power Question — Who Gets to Say No?

Dunkelman frames housing as uniquely difficult within the abundance agenda because it’s a “PB&J sandwich”: the state wants housing, the landowner wants to build, but the neighborhood vetoes. CA bills (Wiener, Wicks) have pushed power down to landowners — but cities (LA notably) are now using innovative foot-dragging to nullify state mandates.

Klein’s critical insight: a decade of CA housing bills has produced little. Many were billed as transformative; few were. When ground-level consensus is absent, planning commissions, councils, and lawsuits find ways to block.

4. The Texas Counterexample (Crucial for Blue-City Citizens)

Thompson hammers this point hard: - Austin: Built massively in the 2010s/early 2020s. Rents have declined 18–24 months running. - Dallas: Absorbed a population the size of urban Boston (2019–early 2020s). Housing prices have fallen over 3.5 years. Now the highest per-capita builder in America.

Texas has billionaires. Texas has corporate concentration. The difference is rules, customs, and permitting that let supply respond to demand. This is the empirical refutation of “building only helps the rich” and the strongest argument that supply elasticity beats rent freezes.

5. The Populism vs. Abundance Fight (and the Synthesis)

The biggest factional surprise has been internal Democratic conflict. Warren and others argue abundance ignores corporate power and is being co-opted by billionaires (Reid Hoffman is reportedly evangelizing the book).

The emerging synthesis Klein calls the “abundance mullet”: economic populism in the front, supply-side abundance in the back. - Mamdani: Rent freeze + fast-tracking developer permitting (18→10 months) - Sherrill (NJ): Utility price caps + supply-side solar/storage build-out - Blue Rose polling: A combined populist+abundance message tests best (“we stopped building the basics… and what gets built goes to the wealthy first”)

Klein’s concession: a system this porous to billionaire money makes “just empower government” insufficient — you need campaign finance reform too, or empowered government becomes captured government.

6. The Deeper Critique — Speed, Procedure, and Legitimacy

Drawing on Nick Bagley’s “procedure fetish”: Democrats — heavily lawyer-formed — locate legitimacy in following procedure. Most voters locate legitimacy in outcomes. Mamdani’s “time is money” applause line marks a cultural shift: delay is no longer treated as a costless virtue.

Dunkelman’s central diagnosis: progressives oscillated from “centralized power does big things” (1900s–1950s) to “speak truth to power, push decisions down” (1960s–today), creating a system with everyone having a voice and everyone having a veto. The needed reform: voice without veto.

7. What This Means for Concerned Blue-State Citizens

The hard truths for someone watching 60% of needed residents priced out:

  1. Don’t celebrate bills — track starts, permits issued, and units delivered. A bill signed is a hypothesis, not an outcome.
  2. The cost stack matters as much as the rule stack. Even with permits, if interest rates + construction labor + materials don’t pencil, nothing rises. Expect a coming Democratic conversation about developer financing, public construction banks, or subsidized capital.
  3. Local veto power is the persistent leak. State preemption is necessary but not sufficient; LA-vs-Sacramento fights will define the next phase.
  4. Texas is the existence proof. Supply works. Rents can fall. The political opponents of building cannot honestly cite “demand is infinite” anymore.
  5. The mullet is your political future. Expect every serious Democratic candidate from 2026 onward to pair price relief (rent stabilization, utility caps) with explicit supply expansion. Judge them on the back half of the mullet — the supply side is what actually lowers costs over time.
  6. The movement still lacks a vision. Klein’s most pointed self-critique: abundance risks collapsing into “efficiency” or “deregulation.” A firefighter affording a home in the city she protects should be as radical and motivating a goal as Medicare for All — but no candidate has yet articulated it that way.

Bottom Line

A year in, abundance has won the argument inside the Democratic Party but lost the year on the ground. The movement’s authors are now openly asking whether their framework underweighted financing, construction economics, local veto dynamics, and the personnel/state-capacity problem of executing on permits already granted. For citizens of expensive blue cities, the policy direction is finally correct — but the unit economics (rates, labor, materials, land) and the political economy of local obstruction mean relief is years, not months, away. The next test is whether Democrats can build a “good DOGE” — institutions and personnel obsessed with delivery, not procedure — when they return to power.
