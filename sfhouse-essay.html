Summary: “Changes Coming for San Francisco’s Housing Stalemate”

The Core Argument

San Francisco’s housing crisis is no longer primarily a zoning problem—it’s a financing problem. Roughly 50,000 units sit fully entitled but unbuilt because the math doesn’t pencil. The essay argues that a quiet revolution in capital stack design has, in the past 24 months, made it possible for private developers to deliver 100% affordable housing at roughly half the per-unit cost of traditional publicly-financed nonprofit projects—upending a core progressive-left assumption.

The Political Frame

Two factions inside the Democratic Party are locked in a defining fight: - Progressive-left (Fielder, Martí, Teachout-aligned): “The market never builds affordable.” Solution = Vienna-scale dedicated public financing. - Abundance/YIMBY (Klein, Thompson-aligned): Scarcity is a regulatory/finance architecture problem; aligned incentives unleash private capital to serve the affordability mission.

The Empirical Evidence: Four Projects

Each delivers 30–70% AMI affordable units without MOHCD local subsidy, using a hybrid stack of federal LIHTC + state tax credits + tax-exempt bonds (CDLAC/CMFA) + HUD-insured debt + mission-aligned private capital:

Project Units Per-Unit Cost Key Innovation
300 De Haro 425 ~$500K Pivoted market-rate → 100% affordable as incentives flipped
960 Howard (oWOW) 202 ~$425K AB 1287 density bonus; mass timber; vertical integration
1111 Sutter 303 ~$700K HUD 221(d)(4) + record AFL-CIO pension investment
1523 Harrison (Oakland) 284 ~$350K Mass timber, 1-day floor installs

Comparison: 1979 Mission Street, the traditional MOHCD/nonprofit model, runs >$1M/unit (with free city land). The cost gap is a factor of ~3.

Why the Math Flipped

  • 2025 federal LIHTC expansion drove a 63% YoY increase in California tax-credit approvals
  • State preemption tools (SB 35, AB 1287 density bonus) cut timeline and land cost
  • Vertical integration eliminates “profit stacking”
  • Mass-timber/prefab compresses construction from 21 months to weeks
  • RAND data identifies California’s cost premium drivers: prevailing wage (32–56% of premium), impact fees ($30,712 SF vs. $816 TX), 49-month vs. 27-month timelines

The November 2026 Inflection Point

The $10 Billion Affordable Housing Bond Act (AB 736 / SB 417) is the pivot. Its $5.25–7B Multifamily Housing Program allocation directly fills the ~30% gap LIHTC doesn’t cover. With 4-to-1 leverage, $10B catalyzes ~$40B in total investment—plausibly unlocking 8,000–12,000 stalled SF units in a 24–36 month window.

If it passes: the four-project model scales from frontier to mainstream; RHNA’s 82,000-unit 2031 target becomes plausibly achievable. If it fails: bespoke deal-by-deal heroics continue, but pace remains inadequate.

The Honest Accounting

  • Progressive-left right about: real displacement, financing as the binding constraint, 0–30% AMI requiring permanent deep subsidy, missing-middle social housing gap.
  • Progressive-left wrong about: “the market never builds affordable”—events have overtaken this claim; reflexive anti-upzoning forecloses density bonuses that make affordability pencil.
  • Abundance right about: small, well-designed reforms unlock disproportionate supply response; private capital under correct incentives delivers at far lower cost.
  • Abundance wrong if it ignores: 0–30% AMI is non-negotiably public; nonprofits provide community-anchor functions; institutional permanence remains a real concern.

The Synthesis

Match the financing tool to the income tier: - 0–30% AMI: deep public subsidy only (but ruthlessly attack the 3–4x California cost premium) - 30–70% AMI: hybrid private/federal/state finance (the four-project model, scaled) - 80%+ AMI: stop choking the pipeline; production lowers rents in 5–7 years (Austin proof)

Three reform levels are converging simultaneously for the first time: federal (LIHTC expansion/reform), state (the bond), and municipal (confronting SF’s own cost premium). November 2026 is when voters effectively ratify or reject the financing architecture that has already been quietly proven on the ground.

The Bottom Line for an Educated Reader

The traditional progressive-left/YIMBY binary is now empirically obsolete. The data shows that public capital structured to leverage private investment produces dramatically more affordable housing per public dollar than either pure-public or pure-market approaches. SF’s working-class tenants need a politics that absorbs this synthesis—and for the first time in a decade, the federal, state, and (potentially) municipal pieces are aligning to make it possible.
