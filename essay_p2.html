PART TWO: THE POLITICS OF SCARCITY

How San Francisco’s Democratic Coalition Learned to Fight Itself Over Housing — and Why the Evidence Is Beginning to Force a Reckoning

Introduction: From Markets to Politics — A Necessary and Uncomfortable Transition

Part One ended with a provocation. After assembling a detailed, evidence-based portrait of San Francisco’s housing cost crisis — the RAND cost ratios, the scissors effect, the 60,000 entitled-but-unbuilt units, the $350,000-per-unit alternative that factory-built housing is already demonstrating in Oakland — it noted that the laws of economics “are a tool that housing policy can work with or against, but cannot simply ignore.” It then acknowledged that San Francisco has, for a generation, chosen to work against those signals rather than with them. The political history of that choice, it promised, would follow.

That history is not orderly. It does not resolve into clean lessons or tidy conclusions. It is, instead, a story about how a city’s deepest values — its commitment to community, to equity, to the protection of vulnerable people from the indifference of markets — collided with the arithmetic of housing scarcity and produced, over decades, a policy architecture that has made the problem it was designed to solve progressively worse. It is also a story about how that architecture is now being challenged, from within the Democratic coalition that built it, by a newer and in some ways equally idealistic set of arguments about what abundance, supply, and functional markets can actually deliver for the people progressive politics has always claimed to serve.

The transition from Part One to Part Two is therefore a transition from one kind of rigor to another. Economic analysis offers the comfort of measurable things: cost per square foot, units per year, elasticity coefficients, timeline differentials. Political analysis offers no such comfort. What it offers instead is a map of competing moral frameworks, each internally coherent, each rooted in real experience, each capable of marshaling genuine evidence in its support — and each, when examined against the full weight of the data assembled in Part One, revealing significant blind spots that its adherents are often the last to acknowledge.

This part attempts to lay out that map honestly before turning analytical. The far-left progressive position on housing in San Francisco — the position associated with Fernando Martí, Jackie Fielder, the Plaza 16 Coalition, the Council of Community Housing Organizations, and the dense ecosystem of Mission District advocacy groups — deserves to be understood on its own terms before it is evaluated against the evidence. The same is true of the moderate abundance position associated with YIMBY Action, the policy framework of Ezra Klein and Derek Thompson’s book of the same name, and the emerging political coalition that passed Mayor Lurie’s Family Zoning Plan over vigorous progressive opposition in late 2024. Neither side is arguing in bad faith. Both sides are responding to real conditions with real moral seriousness. The question this part will ultimately ask is not which side has better values — both have defensible values — but which side has a more accurate theory of how housing markets work, and what the consequences of that theory’s policy prescriptions have been and will be for the people both sides claim to champion.

Chapter Four: Two Lefts, One Crisis

The Inheritance of the New Left

To understand why San Francisco’s Democratic coalition is so deeply divided over housing, you have to understand what it is divided about at the level of foundational assumptions — not just policy preferences, but the underlying theory of how cities work, who markets serve, and what government is for.

Ezra Klein, in his conversations with both Zephyr Teachout and Sam Seder about his book Abundance, offers the most historically precise account of the divide available in the public record. His argument, stripped to its essentials, is this: the American left has two distinct intellectual traditions that were, for most of the twentieth century, in productive tension with each other — and that have, over the past generation, come into increasingly dysfunctional conflict.

The first tradition is the New Deal left: growth-oriented, state-activist, focused on using government power to build things, employ people, and create material prosperity for working-class Americans. This is the tradition of the Tennessee Valley Authority, the Interstate Highway System, public housing projects, and the postwar urban boom. It is comfortable with large-scale development, with government as builder and employer, with the idea that physical construction — of homes, roads, dams, schools — is itself a form of social progress. It is, in Klein’s framing, a tradition that “in many ways was very reckless” about environmental consequences and community disruption, running highways through Black neighborhoods and demolishing working-class communities in the name of urban renewal. Its failures were real and their consequences lasting.

The second tradition is the New Left, which arose precisely in response to those failures. Ralph Nader, Rachel Carson, the environmental movement, the community organizing tradition of Saul Alinsky — these are its intellectual ancestors. It passed the National Environmental Policy Act, the Clean Air Act, the Fair Housing Act. It created legal mechanisms — environmental review, community input requirements, discretionary approval processes — designed to slow government down, force it to consider consequences it had previously ignored, and give communities a voice in decisions that affected their lives. These mechanisms were, in Klein’s careful formulation, “very effective and consequential in their day.” The problem is the day they were designed for — an era of reckless, top-down development that bulldozed communities without consent — is not the day we now inhabit. The day we now inhabit is one in which we are building too little, not too much, and the mechanisms designed to restrain reckless building are now being deployed against the affordable housing, clean energy, and public transit projects that the left most urgently needs to succeed.

This is the structural tragedy at the heart of San Francisco’s housing politics: a city governed almost entirely by people who share progressive values has used the tools of progressive governance to produce an outcome — a housing market of staggering unaffordability that has displaced working-class families, deepened racial segregation, and driven teachers, nurses, and firefighters out of the city they serve — that no progressive would endorse if it were presented as a policy goal rather than an accumulated consequence.

The Moral Geography of the Far Left

To understand the far-left position on housing in San Francisco, you have to begin not with economics but with geography and history. The Mission District — the epicenter of SF’s housing wars and the home district of the congressional race this document ultimately evaluates — is not an abstraction. It is a specific place with a specific history, and that history is inseparable from the policy positions of the organizations that defend it.

The Mission was, for most of the twentieth century, a working-class immigrant neighborhood. Irish and Italian families gave way, over decades, to Latino families — Mexican, Salvadoran, Nicaraguan, Guatemalan — who built a dense, culturally coherent community around 24th Street, around Valencia, around the 16th Street BART plaza that would later become the site of San Francisco’s most famous housing battle. The Mission had murals and taquerias and quinceañeras and community organizations and social networks of the kind that take generations to build and can be destroyed in a decade by rising rents.

The first tech boom, in the late 1990s, hit the Mission like a demolition crew. Evictions spiked. Latino families who had lived in the neighborhood for twenty years received Ellis Act notices. The cultural district that had been built over a generation began to dissolve under the pressure of rents that doubled and then doubled again. The organizations that would eventually form the Plaza 16 Coalition, United to Save the Mission, and the Council of Community Housing Organizations were forged in this crucible. Their foundational insight — that market-rate housing in the Mission functions as a displacement mechanism for the community they represent — was not derived from ideology. It was derived from watching their neighbors leave.

This is the moral and empirical foundation of the 100% affordable doctrine. When Fernando Martí writes, in “Upzoning Fantasies,” that the choice facing San Francisco is between low-income housing requiring deep public subsidies, market-rate luxury housing dependent on equity investors demanding 20% returns, and social housing dependent on shallow subsidies and low-interest loans, he is not engaging in rhetorical excess. He is articulating a theory of housing markets that is grounded in two decades of observation from one of the most expensive, most rapidly gentrifying neighborhoods in the United States. When Jackie Fielder argues against market-rate development in vulnerable communities, she is not ignorant of supply-and-demand theory. She is making a value judgment that the near-term displacement costs of market-rate development are not worth the long-term supply benefits that economic models predict — particularly when those models’ predictions have, from her community’s vantage point, consistently failed to materialize at the speed or scale that would matter for the families being displaced right now.

The Plaza 16 Coalition’s opposition to development at the 16th Street BART plaza — the “Monster in the Mission” battle that became the defining confrontation of SF’s housing wars — needs to be understood in this context. The site in question was not a random parcel. It was in the heart of the Mission, adjacent to a BART station, in a neighborhood under acute displacement pressure. The coalition’s argument was not that housing should not be built there. It was that market-rate housing built there would accelerate the displacement of the Latino working-class community that gave the Mission its identity — and that the only legitimate alternative was 100% affordable housing on publicly controlled land. That argument can be evaluated against the evidence (and will be, shortly). But it cannot be dismissed as irrational or bad-faith without first acknowledging the real displacement it was responding to.

Peter Cohen and Fernando Martí, as co-directors of the Council of Community Housing Organizations, have provided the intellectual architecture for this position over many years. Their “permanently affordable, community-controlled” housing model draws on international examples — Vienna’s social housing, Singapore’s public housing, community land trusts in Burlington and Boston — to argue that the only way to prevent housing from becoming a speculative asset that prices out working-class families is to remove it from the market permanently. This is not a fringe position in international housing policy. Vienna has maintained affordable, high-quality social housing for over a century. Singapore houses more than 80% of its population in publicly built, publicly maintained apartments. The claim that permanent public ownership is the only reliable mechanism for permanent affordability has substantial evidence behind it.

What makes this position politically explosive in San Francisco — and what generates the intensity of the intra-Democratic conflict over housing — is not the goal of permanent affordability but the corollary that follows from it: if market-rate housing accelerates displacement and only permanently affordable housing prevents it, then any policy that facilitates market-rate housing production is, in effect, a policy that facilitates displacement. This makes the YIMBY/abundance coalition not merely a policy disagreement but, from the far-left perspective, a moral adversary — a group whose proposals, however well-intentioned, will produce the erasure of the communities that progressive politics is supposed to protect.

The Organizational Ecosystem and Its Political Power

The far-left housing coalition in San Francisco is not just an ideological tendency. It is an organizational infrastructure with real political power, deep community roots, and decades of accumulated institutional knowledge about how to use the city’s permitting and review processes to block developments it opposes.

The Plaza 16 Coalition alone encompasses over 100 organizations — neighborhood associations, tenant unions, immigrant rights groups, youth organizations, cultural preservation groups, religious institutions. PODER, HOMEY, Calle 24, United to Save the Mission, MEDA — each of these organizations has its own funding streams, its own community base, its own staff, its own institutional history. Together they constitute a formidable political force capable of turning out community members to planning commission hearings, generating media coverage, pressuring elected officials, and deploying the legal tools available under CEQA and the city’s discretionary review process to slow or stop developments they oppose.

This organizational infrastructure was built over decades precisely because the political system, in the absence of organized community opposition, tended to produce outcomes favorable to developers and property owners at the expense of working-class tenants. The tools it uses — CEQA challenges, discretionary review appeals, community notification requirements, demands for environmental impact reports — were created by the environmental and community rights movements of the 1970s for exactly this purpose: to give organized communities the ability to slow down powerful actors whose decisions would otherwise be made without public accountability.

The problem, as Klein’s analysis makes clear, is that these tools are power-neutral. They can be used by communities defending themselves against displacement, but they can also be used by wealthy homeowners defending their property values, by developers seeking to block competing projects, by unions seeking labor concessions unrelated to the project under review, and by anyone else with the legal sophistication and organizational resources to deploy them. When CEQA has been used to try to stop UC Berkeley from expanding its dormitories on the grounds that more students constitute an environmental nuisance, the tool has traveled a very long way from its original purpose. When the same legal mechanism is used by a Mission District coalition defending Latino working-class families from displacement and by a Noe Valley homeowners association protecting its property values and neighborhood character, the mechanism itself has become something its creators did not intend: a generalized veto power available to whoever can most effectively use it.

Chapter Five: The Abundance Response — What the Moderates Are Actually Arguing

Beyond the NIMBY Caricature

The YIMBY (Yes In My Backyard) movement and its more intellectually sophisticated cousin, the abundance politics associated with Klein and Thompson’s book, are frequently caricatured in progressive housing discourse as a front for real estate interests, a “tech libertarian deregulation” project dressed up in equity language, or simply a naive faith in trickle-down economics applied to housing. Fernando Martí’s “Upzoning Fantasies” essay captures this critique in its most pointed form, dismissing YIMBY proposals as “magical zoning bullshit, pushed by politicians without the guts to address the real issues of financing and costs” and characterizing the broader movement as trapped in a “tech libertarian deregulation and austerity ideology” that cannot imagine public solutions to public problems.

This caricature is politically effective but analytically misleading. The abundance position, as articulated by Klein, Thompson, and the policy organizations aligned with them, is not a simple deregulation argument. It is a more complex — and, in important respects, more self-critical — argument about why liberal governance in blue cities has failed to deliver on liberal goals, and what structural changes would be required to fix that failure.

Klein’s clearest statement of the position, in his conversation with Sam Seder, deserves to be quoted directly because it is frequently misrepresented: “I want a more capable state. Tim Carney wants a less capable state. Tim wants a state that does fewer things. I want a state capable of doing more things, including the things it has already promised to do.” This is not a libertarian position. It is an argument that the left has, over decades, built a regulatory architecture so complex, so laden with veto points, so hostile to speed and scale, that it has become structurally incapable of delivering the outcomes — affordable housing, clean energy, public transit — that progressive politics most urgently requires. The problem, in this analysis, is not too much government. It is government that has become too slow, too expensive, and too capturable by incumbent interests to function as an instrument of progressive change.

The specific policy prescriptions that follow from this analysis are worth enumerating precisely, because they are often lumped together with positions the abundance movement does not actually hold:

What the abundance/YIMBY position actually argues: - Zoning reform to allow more housing in high-resource neighborhoods that have been protected from density by exclusionary land use rules with a documented history of racial discrimination - By-right permitting for developments that meet objective standards — eliminating the discretionary review process that allows any organized group to challenge any project for any reason, effectively for unlimited time - CEQA reform to exempt urban infill housing from environmental review that was designed for large-scale industrial and infrastructure projects, not four-unit apartment buildings - Streamlined approval timelines with mandatory shot-clocks, modeled on Texas’s 30-day approval requirement, to reduce the carrying costs that RAND identifies as the single largest driver of California’s cost premium over Texas - Expansion of the Low Income Housing Tax Credit program to make the financing of affordable housing more accessible and less dependent on complex, multi-layered public subsidy stacks - Support for factory-built and modular housing construction methods that can dramatically reduce per-unit costs by compressing on-site construction timelines - Mixed-income development models that leverage private capital to cross-subsidize affordable units, reducing the total public subsidy required per affordable unit

What the abundance/YIMBY position does NOT argue: - That market forces alone will produce affordable housing for very-low-income households - That public housing and deep public subsidy are unnecessary or undesirable - That the concerns of Mission District tenants about displacement are not real or not legitimate - That the history of racist housing policy in San Francisco is irrelevant to current housing debates - That environmental review and community input have no legitimate role in housing approval processes

This distinction matters because the progressive critique of abundance politics is often most effective against positions the movement does not actually hold. When Martí characterizes YIMBY as arguing that “deregulation and magical thinking will provide,” he is arguing against a straw man. The actual abundance argument is that the specific regulatory architecture California and San Francisco have built over the past fifty years is making it impossible to build enough housing of any kind — including the publicly subsidized affordable housing that Martí’s own framework depends on — and that reforming that architecture is a prerequisite for any housing strategy, market-rate or otherwise, to function at the scale the crisis demands.

The Klein/Thompson “Abundance” Framework

Ezra Klein and Derek Thompson’s book, Abundance, published in 2025, is the most prominent national articulation of the moderate-left case for supply-side housing reform, and it deserves careful treatment because it is more intellectually honest about its own limitations than its critics generally acknowledge.

The book’s central argument, as Klein explains in multiple interviews, is not that markets are good and government is bad. It is that the Democratic Party, in governing blue cities and states over the past generation, has produced outcomes — housing unaffordability, energy scarcity, infrastructure dysfunction — that contradict its own stated values, and that understanding why requires confronting the ways in which the party’s own policy choices have contributed to those outcomes. “This book is not aimed at the right,” Klein writes, “because they don’t share my goal on decarbonization. But then trying to understand why Texas and Georgia have been such incredible success stories from the perspective of the IRA… requires some untangling.”

The book identifies what Klein calls “everything bagel liberalism” as a primary mechanism of this self-sabotage: the tendency to load every public project with every progressive goal simultaneously — labor standards, environmental requirements, equity mandates, community input requirements, affordability standards, green building codes — such that the cumulative weight of well-intentioned requirements makes the project impossible to finance, impossibly slow to approve, or impossibly expensive to build. The RAND data on California LIHTC projects — costing more than four times the price of market-rate housing in Texas, with soft costs eight and a half times the Texas average, driven substantially by the administrative burden of satisfying six, seven, or eight separate funding sources each with its own requirements — is the empirical evidence for this argument. Klein’s insight is that applying the most demanding version of every progressive goal to every single housing project, regardless of whether the project’s specific circumstances require it, has not produced more equitable housing outcomes. It has produced fewer housing units, at higher cost, serving fewer people, than a more calibrated approach would have achieved.

This is a genuinely uncomfortable argument for the progressive left to engage with, because it is not made from outside the progressive tradition. It is made from within it — by someone who explicitly supports public housing, wants the government to build more, and is arguing that the current approach to building publicly subsidized housing is failing the people it is meant to serve. Klein’s challenge to Seder is pointed: “Can we build enough affordable housing if the units cost $650,000 to a million to complete per unit? Just flat — can we pay for that?” The question does not have a comfortable answer if you believe, as both Klein and the far-left do, that San Francisco needs many thousands of affordable units in the near term.

The Saikat Chakrabarti Synthesis

Saikat Chakrabarti occupies an unusual position in this debate. His organizational history — chief of staff to Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez, architect of the Green New Deal strategy, president of the New Consensus think tank — places him firmly in the progressive left tradition. His political endorsements, his rhetoric about Democratic Party leadership, and his willingness to challenge Hakeem Jeffries and other establishment Democrats mark him as an insurgent, not an institutionalist. Yet his actual housing policy framework — the Reconstruction Finance Corporation, the national housing plan, the acknowledgment that “70,000 units of housing entitled in San Francisco right now are simply not getting built” because “the financing isn’t there” — maps much more closely onto the abundance/Klein framework than onto the Martí/CCHO position.

In his interview with Klein and Teachout, Chakrabarti occupies an intellectually honest middle ground. He agrees with Klein that process vetocracy is a real problem — “the process we use to build housing is crazy” — and that financing gaps are the primary near-term constraint on production. He agrees with Teachout that money in politics is an enervating force and that corporate power shapes which projects get built and which don’t. He adds his own distinctive contribution: the argument that solving housing at the scale required is not a matter of incremental policy adjustment but of mission-mode governance — the kind of comprehensive, institution-building, bottleneck-clearing approach that the United States used to mobilize its wartime economy and that European countries used in their postwar reconstruction booms.

His RFC proposal is, in this context, the most intellectually serious attempt in the current field to bridge the left-progressive and abundance-moderate positions. It accepts the far-left’s premise that private capital alone cannot solve the affordability crisis — cannot serve the 0-40% AMI households who need deep subsidy regardless of what markets do. It accepts the abundance framework’s premise that the financing architecture for housing is broken and needs institutional reconstruction, not just more money through existing channels. And it proposes a mechanism — a public development bank with active bottleneck-detection and resolution functions, modeled on the New Deal RFC’s role in the wartime economy — that could, in principle, do what neither pure market solutions nor pure public subsidy programs have been able to do: deploy capital proactively at scale, across income tiers, without waiting for either Wall Street to decide SF housing is worth 20% returns or for Sacramento to appropriate enough gap financing to close the cost gap on individually negotiated projects.

Chapter Six: Where the Evidence Points — Returning to the Data

What the Far Left Gets Right

The far-left housing position in San Francisco is not simply wrong. Several of its core empirical claims are well-supported by the evidence assembled in Part One, and honest analysis requires acknowledging them before turning to the claims that are not.

The displacement critique is real. The Mission District has experienced documented, large-scale displacement of Latino working-class families during periods of market-rate development pressure. The correlation between tech boom cycles, rising rents, and community demographic change is not imaginary. Families who lived in the Mission for twenty years were evicted and could not return. Cultural institutions closed. Schools lost enrollment. The claim that market-rate housing in vulnerable neighborhoods functions as a displacement accelerator is not pure ideology — it is an observation grounded in specific, documented experience.

The financing constraint is real. Martí’s central empirical argument — that 54,394 already-approved units in San Francisco cannot obtain construction financing, proving that zoning is not the binding constraint — is confirmed by every data source in Part One. The RAND study identifies timeline and financing costs as the primary drivers of California’s cost premium. The Terner Center’s analysis shows that the gap financing problem is the “Achilles’ heel” of affordable housing development. The market-rate developer who told Martí’s graduate class that the Family Zoning Plan “is essentially just political posturing and won’t have much impact on increasing supply” because “development is all about costs and interest rates and financing” is making an argument that the evidence supports. Zoning reform is necessary but not sufficient. Martí is correct about this.

The construction cost threshold argument is real. Martí’s observation that height increases above certain thresholds trigger qualitatively different and significantly more expensive construction requirements — elevator shafts above certain heights, concrete-frame construction above others — is technically accurate and directly relevant to the Family Zoning Plan’s practical impact. Blanket upzoning that ignores the relationship between building height, lot size, and construction cost structure can produce theoretical capacity that never becomes actual housing because the economics of building at the allowed heights don’t work on the available sites.

The land value inflation risk is real. The “zoning capitalization” effect — the tendency for upzoning to immediately raise land values, making site acquisition more expensive for affordable developers who need those sites — is a documented economic phenomenon. Martí’s argument that upzoning without land banking can actually slow affordable housing production by pricing community land trusts and nonprofit developers out of the land market before they can acquire sites is not paranoid. It is a real risk that thoughtful upzoning policy needs to address.

The social housing gap is real. Martí’s three-pathway framework — deep public subsidy for 0-40% AMI, market-rate luxury for boom cycles only, and social housing as the missing middle — maps accurately onto the income ladder analysis in Part One. The observation that San Francisco does the first pathway “relatively well, meeting about 50% of need,” does the second “only when Wall Street says go,” and has “never done the third” is confirmed by the project-level data. The 300 De Haro, 960 Howard, and 1111 Sutter examples demonstrate that the second pathway is currently producing affordable units in an unusual market moment — but Martí’s skepticism about whether for-profit developers will stay in the affordable space when market-rate returns recover is a legitimate long-term concern that the LIHTC’s 30-year compliance period partially but not fully addresses.

What the Far Left Gets Wrong

Against these legitimate insights, however, the far-left position has several empirical blind spots that the evidence in Part One directly challenges.

The “market never builds affordable housing” claim is no longer accurate. This was, historically, a defensible generalization — in the high-cap-rate, cheap-debt years of 2015-2019, market-rate developers in San Francisco did build almost exclusively for the top of the income distribution, and trickle-down arguments about filtering did not materialize at the speed or scale that supply-side economists predicted. But the claim has been overtaken by events. The 300 De Haro conversion, the 960 Howard LIHTC project, the oWOW 1523 Harrison development targeting $350,000 per unit with no gap financing — these are not theoretical possibilities. They are projects under construction or in advanced development in 2025. DM Development, Align Real Estate Partners, and Martin Building Company have a combined 1,200 affordable units in the works in San Francisco. The California Tax Credit Allocation Committee approved 25,781 affordable units in 2025 — 10,000 more than the previous year, a 63% increase driven by the LIHTC expansion. The market has pivoted, and a political analysis anchored in the conditions of 2015 will misread the conditions of 2025.

The Austin dismissal is not supported by the supply-side evidence. Chakrabarti’s characterization of Austin’s rent decline as primarily the result of “private developers making a mistake” and “people leaving Austin” is contested by the data. Austin sustained housing production of 15,000-20,000 units per year at its peak — a rate that corresponds precisely to the 3x-6x multiplier that the stock/flow analysis in Part One identifies as necessary to bend the price curve. Rents fell by 8-15% across unit types as vacancy rates rose from approximately 5% to 12%. The supply-side mechanism worked as predicted when supply was deployed at sufficient scale. The lesson from Austin is not that the private market accidentally overbuilt — it is that sufficient volume of supply, sustained over multiple years, produces measurable rent relief. That is exactly the mechanism the abundance argument predicts, and exactly the mechanism that San Francisco’s 2,500-unit annual production rate has never come close to testing.

The “100% affordable or nothing” doctrine has a measurable opportunity cost. The Monster in the Mission battle — the successful blocking of market-rate development at the 16th Street BART plaza — is the far-left’s signature victory. But the site remains undeveloped years later, producing zero units of any kind for any income level. The counterfactual question — how many units of housing, at what income levels, could have been built on that site over the intervening years if a mixed-income development with deep affordability requirements had been permitted — is not one that the 100% affordable doctrine encourages its advocates to ask. But it is the question that the families on San Francisco’s affordable housing waitlist, many of them Latino, many of them from the Mission, have a legitimate interest in having answered.

The CEQA/process tools have externalities that fall on the people they’re meant to protect. The legal and procedural tools that Mission District coalitions use to block market-rate development are power-neutral instruments. They work equally well when deployed by wealthy Noe Valley homeowners to block affordable housing in their neighborhood, by unions seeking unrelated labor concessions, or by a competing developer seeking to delay a rival project. The RAND finding that California’s pre-development timeline averages 27.9 months compared to 13.1 months in Texas — a gap driven primarily by the approval and CEQA exposure processes that the far-left defends — falls as heavily on nonprofit affordable housing developers as on market-rate ones. When MOHCD’s gap financing requirements, CEQA compliance costs, and multi-agency review timelines push the cost of a 100% affordable project at 1979 Mission Street to $1,047,063 per unit, the process tools designed to protect the Mission’s working-class residents are contributing to a cost structure that limits how many working-class residents can be housed. This is not an argument that process protections have no value. It is an argument that their costs are not distributed the way their defenders assume.

What the Abundance Movement Gets Right

Against the far-left’s blind spots, the abundance/YIMBY position has several empirically well-grounded arguments that are often obscured by the political intensity of the debate.

The by-right permitting evidence from Portland is decisive. The Chronicle’s comparison of Portland’s Residential Infill Project with San Francisco’s SB9 implementation — 2,200 missing-middle units permitted in Portland versus approximately 370 in San Francisco over the same 2021-2024 period — is the clearest available evidence that process design determines production outcomes independently of zoning law on paper. Both cities passed functionally similar legislation permitting fourplexes on single-family lots. Portland made the process genuinely by-right — code-compliant projects approved without discretionary review, without community notification, without the possibility of CEQA challenge. San Francisco added owner-occupancy requirements, holding periods, and a “ministerial” process so laden with sub-approvals and fee requirements that a single design exception breaks ministerial status and reintroduces full discretionary review. Portland is building. San Francisco is not. The difference is not zoning law. It is process.

The “everything bagel” cost premium is measurable and falls hardest on the poorest. Klein’s most penetrating observation — “why are we making the government’s housing for the poorest people so much harder, slower, and more costly to build?” — is not rhetorical. The RAND data shows that California’s publicly subsidized affordable housing costs more than four times the price of market-rate housing in Texas. The Terner Center’s analysis shows that each additional layer of public financing adds approximately $20,000 per unit in administrative costs. The 1979 Mission Street project — with free land, no parking requirements, and every available cost-reduction tool applied — still comes in above $1 million per unit. If the goal is to house the maximum number of San Francisco’s most vulnerable residents within a finite public subsidy budget, then a system that spends $1 million to house one household is, by definition, leaving other households unhoused. The argument that public subsidy must be preserved for the deepest-need populations is correct. The implication that the current system is efficiently serving those populations is not.

The oWOW evidence cannot be dismissed. At $350,000 per unit for LIHTC-financed affordable housing in Oakland — one-third the cost of comparable publicly financed affordable housing in San Francisco — the oWOW model is not a theoretical possibility or a developer’s promotional claim. It is a demonstrated, financeable production system being applied to real projects in the Bay Area right now. The question it poses to the far-left framework is direct: if the same public subsidy dollars that currently finance one unit of housing at 1979 Mission Street could finance three units of housing using the oWOW prefabrication model, which approach serves more of the working-class Mission families on San Francisco’s housing waitlist? The answer is not complicated. The political resistance to it is.

What the Abundance Movement Gets Wrong

The abundance/YIMBY position has its own blind spots, and honest analysis requires naming them.

The land banking gap is a genuine policy failure. Martí’s argument that upzoning without land banking inflates the acquisition costs of exactly the sites that nonprofit affordable developers and community land trusts need to purchase is correct. The Family Zoning Plan’s passage without a parallel commitment to aggressive land banking before the upzoning takes effect will, predictably, make it harder and more expensive for MEDA, community land trusts, and other community-controlled housing organizations to acquire sites in the neighborhoods being upzoned. The abundance movement’s relative silence on this problem reflects a genuine gap in its policy framework.

The unit mix problem is real. Eliminating density limits without requiring minimum bedroom mixes does create an incentive for developers to maximize unit count by building the smallest possible units — studios and micro-units rather than the two-bedroom and three-bedroom apartments that families with children need. Martí’s proposal that San Francisco measure housing production in bedrooms rather than units, and require at least 50% two-bedroom-or-larger unit mixes in rezoned areas, addresses a real problem that the abundance framework has not adequately engaged.

The displacement risk in rent-controlled buildings is not paranoid. The argument that upzoning rent-controlled parcels attracts speculative investors who will pursue evictions even when demolition is legally difficult is supported by the historical record of how the Ellis Act has been used in San Francisco. Speculative investors purchase rent-controlled buildings, execute Ellis Act evictions to empty them, and then either hold for redevelopment or convert to other uses. Including rent-controlled parcels in upzoning envelopes without additional anti-speculation protections is a design choice with foreseeable negative consequences for exactly the tenants the Family Zoning Plan’s supporters claim to be helping.

The deep-subsidy gap at 0-30% AMI is not solved by supply-side reform. The filtering mechanism — the process by which market-rate units built at the top of the income distribution age into affordability over time — works, when it works, over decades and primarily for households in the 60-80% AMI range. For households at 30% AMI or below — the formerly homeless, the severely disabled, the extremely low-income — filtering produces no near-term relief, and the oWOW model at $350,000 per unit, while a dramatic cost improvement, still requires significant public subsidy to serve this population. Nick Friend of the Silicon Valley Housing Trust states the case clearly: “Those are the projects that are most expensive and rely most on subsidies — and those are the projects that will be even harder to do going forward with this new market reality.” The abundance movement’s emphasis on supply-side reform needs to be paired with an explicit commitment to deep public subsidy for the lowest-income tier, or it will be correctly perceived as a framework that addresses the housing needs of the middle class while leaving the most vulnerable behind.

Chapter Seven: The Shifting Balance — San Francisco in 2025

A Political Inflection Point

The passage of Mayor Daniel Lurie’s Family Zoning Plan by the San Francisco Board of Supervisors in late 2024 represents the most significant political shift in San Francisco’s housing wars in at least a decade. The 2024 elections produced a moderate majority on the Board for the first time in years. The far-left coalition that had successfully blocked major upzoning initiatives for a generation lost the Mandelman vote. The YIMBY Action / moderate alliance now holds the political initiative at City Hall.

This shift is the immediate political context for the D9 congressional race this document is ultimately evaluating. The three candidates — Chakrabarti, Wiener, and Chan — are running in a city where the ground has recently moved under the housing debate, where the far-left position has suffered its most significant local legislative defeat in years, and where the question of how to interpret that defeat — as a vindication of the abundance framework, as a betrayal of tenant communities, or as an incomplete victory that addressed zoning while leaving financing unresolved — is very much alive.

Supervisor Mandelman’s speech in support of the Family Zoning Plan captures the moderate coalition’s current argument in its most fully developed form. His most pointed challenge to the opposition is worth quoting directly: “The best way for us to help renters isn’t an obtuse tool that freezes the built environment in amber. It’s making sure renters have more places to call home and protecting existing tenants as our city creates housing to welcome new neighbors.” His characterization of some opposition arguments as “fear mongering” and “emotional exploitation” reflects the political confidence of a coalition that has just won a major vote — but also the risk of a coalition that, in winning, has made enemies of organizations with deep community roots and long institutional memories.

Martí’s response, in “Upzoning Fantasies,” is equally revealing in what it acknowledges and what it contests. He does not argue that San Francisco’s housing crisis is not real, or that rezoning is not needed. He argues that the specific rezoning enacted will not produce the construction its supporters promise, because the binding constraints are financial and cost-structural rather than zoning-based. He proposes a more targeted alternative — upzoning focused on large commercial parcels, tied to affordability requirements, with height limits calibrated to construction cost thresholds, and paired with public financing tools that the Family Zoning Plan does not include. Whether or not one finds his critique of the YIMBY movement’s motives persuasive, the policy substance of his alternative framework is more technically sophisticated than the broad-brush opposition to development that his coalition is sometimes associated with.

The RHNA Clock

Against the backdrop of this political debate, a legal clock is ticking that neither side can afford to ignore. San Francisco’s RHNA obligation — 82,069 units by 2031, requiring approximately 11,700 units per year from a city that currently produces approximately 2,500 — is not a political preference or a planning aspiration. It is a legal requirement with specified consequences for non-compliance: loss of Housing Element certification, loss of hundreds of millions of dollars in annual state funding, and exposure to Builder’s Remedy — the state override of local zoning that would allow developers to build at heights and densities that San Francisco’s current zoning prohibits, without the community input processes that both the far-left and the moderate coalition value.

The math of the RHNA obligation illuminates the inadequacy of both sides’ current positions with unusual clarity. The far-left’s 100% affordable doctrine, applied at current production rates and current per-unit costs, would require approximately $85 billion in public subsidy to house 82,069 households — a figure so far beyond any realistic public financing capacity that it functions as an argument against itself. The YIMBY/abundance framework’s emphasis on zoning reform and process streamlining, while necessary, cannot on its own produce 11,700 units per year in a market where 54,394 approved units are already waiting for financing. The RHNA clock is ticking for both sides simultaneously, and neither side has a complete answer to what it requires.

What the data assembled in Part One suggests, and what the political debate in Part Two has consistently obscured, is that meeting the RHNA obligation requires a policy framework that is deliberately differentiated by income tier: deep public subsidy for 0-40% AMI households, with aggressive cost reduction through prefabrication, design-build, and administrative streamlining; LIHTC-financed mixed-income development for 40-80% AMI households, leveraging the current market pivot by for-profit developers entering the affordable space; by-right permitting and missing-middle zoning for 80-120% AMI households who can be served by smaller-scale market production if the process allows it to happen quickly and cheaply enough; and land banking, rent-controlled building protections, and community benefit agreements to prevent the supply expansion from producing the displacement it is meant to relieve.

No single political actor currently in San Francisco’s housing debate is advocating for all of these elements simultaneously. The far-left advocates for the first and parts of the fourth. The abundance/YIMBY coalition advocates for the second and third and parts of the fourth, with varying degrees of commitment to each. The task of the next chapter — evaluating the three congressional candidates against this framework — is to ask which of them comes closest to understanding the whole problem, and which of them, if elected, would have the most credible theory of change for moving the national policy levers that San Francisco’s local debate ultimately cannot move on its own.

Transition to Part Three

The evidence assembled in Parts One and Two points toward a set of conclusions that are genuinely uncomfortable for everyone currently engaged in San Francisco’s housing debate.

For the far left, the discomfort is this: the organizational infrastructure and legal tools built to protect vulnerable communities from displacement have, in combination with well-intentioned but cumulative regulatory requirements, produced a publicly financed housing system so expensive that it cannot serve the communities it was designed to protect at anywhere near the scale those communities need. The 54,394 approved units waiting for financing are not waiting because of corporate power or YIMBY ideology. They are waiting because the economics of housing production in San Francisco have been systematically destroyed by the very policies designed to make housing more equitable. Acknowledging this is not a concession to “tech libertarian deregulation.” It is a prerequisite for building the kind of public financing institutions — the RFC, the public bank, the social housing program — that the far-left’s own framework requires.

For the abundance movement, the discomfort is this: zoning reform and process streamlining, while necessary, are not sufficient. The Portland comparison shows that genuine by-right permitting can produce meaningful missing-middle supply. The oWOW example shows that factory-built construction can dramatically reduce per-unit costs. The LIHTC expansion shows that incremental improvements to the financing architecture produce measurable supply responses. But none of these tools, individually or in combination, addresses the financing gap for the 0-40% AMI households who need deep public subsidy regardless of what the market does, or the land banking gap that allows upzoning to inflate the acquisition costs of sites that community land trusts and nonprofit developers need to purchase, or the unit mix problem that allows density deregulation to produce micro-units rather than family apartments.

For both sides — and for the three candidates in Part Three — the RHNA clock defines the stakes. Eleven thousand seven hundred units per year. By 2031. From a city currently producing 2,500. The gap between where San Francisco is and where it needs to be is not a policy disagreement. It is a measurement. And measurements, as Part One established at some length, do not adjust themselves out of sensitivity to political identity.

The three candidates and their respective theories of how to close that gap are the subject of Part Three.
